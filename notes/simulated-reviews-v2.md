## Synthesis of Recurring Themes

Ten simulated reviewers responded to the revised paper, spanning cognitive psychology (Gentner, Gibbs, Holyoak, Keil), philosophy of science (Khalidi, Godfrey-Smith), animal cognition (Horowitz), science writing (Zimmer), film/essay (Herzog), and analogy/AI (Hofstadter). Seven recommend major revision; one recommends minor-to-moderate revision (Horowitz); two non-academic respondents (Herzog, Hofstadter) do not use standard recommendation language but are broadly positive. No reviewer rejects the paper outright, and all acknowledge the Boyd-Lakoff synthesis as genuinely novel and worth pursuing.

**1. Relabelling vs. genuine theoretical advance (8/10 reviewers).** The most pervasive concern is whether HPC adds genuine explanatory content beyond existing frameworks or merely relabels them. Gentner argues that "mechanism support" restates systematicity. Gibbs argues that embodied simulation already provides the mechanisms CMT supposedly lacks. Holyoak notes convergence with multiconstraint theory's pragmatic constraints. Khalidi asks whether "homeostatic" is doing any work beyond "causally maintained." Keil distinguishes ontological from psychological claims and asks what HPC adds at the psychological level. Godfrey-Smith worries that "mechanism" is used inconsistently across ecological, neural, and social senses. Hofstadter argues the entire framework reduces to a theory of analogical productivity, not a theory of what metaphor is. Only Horowitz and Zimmer do not press this concern directly.

**2. The dog case: charming but inferentially limited (9/10 reviewers).** Nearly every reviewer praises the canine material as engaging and honestly handled, but most argue it carries less argumentative weight than its length suggests. The central worry is whether following a painted line constitutes cross-domain extension or perceptual generalization / stimulus generalization along a similarity gradient (Gentner, Godfrey-Smith, Keil, Holyoak, Horowitz). Horowitz raises specific empirical concerns: cross-modal transfer from an olfactory-primary category, paint odour as a substantive confound, and over-interpretation of the Brauer violation-of-expectation studies. Gibbs and Holyoak both suggest compressing the dog section. Zimmer, by contrast, argues the dog material arrives too late and should come earlier in the paper.

**3. The functional domain criterion needs sharpening (7/10 reviewers).** The definition of functional domain via maintaining mechanisms is recognized as the paper's load-bearing criterion, but reviewers find it underdeveloped. Gibbs asks why sidewalks don't count as cross-domain. Khalidi raises the circularity worry (mechanisms identified by what they maintain, domains by their mechanisms). Keil asks whether functional domains are psychologically real for the dog. Godfrey-Smith notes that "mechanism" shifts meaning across ecological, neural, and social cases. Hofstadter argues that mechanism boundaries are theorist's conveniences, not natural joints, and that the sharp literal/metaphorical boundary dissolves on inspection. Gentner wants more worked cases. Holyoak wants operationalized thresholds.

**4. Ontological vs. psychological claims are conflated (4/10 reviewers).** Keil is the strongest voice here: trails in the world may have HPC structure without the dog's representation having HPC structure. Khalidi asks whether the central claim is ontological or heuristic. Horowitz distinguishes trails-in-the-world from the dog's representation. Godfrey-Smith insists on the detection/representation distinction from comparative cognition.

**5. Missing literatures (6/10 reviewers).** Gibbs demands engagement with embodied simulation (Barsalou, Bergen, Gibbs 2006) and Lakoff's neural theory of metaphor. Holyoak wants multiconstraint theory and relevance-theoretic accounts. Khalidi wants post-Boyd philosophy: MPC (Craver, Kendler), Magnus, Ereshefsky and Reydon. Keil wants developmental evidence on metaphor acquisition. Hofstadter wants the analogy-as-categorization tradition (Sander and Hofstadter, Copycat/Metacat, Mitchell). Gentner wants engagement with the analogy/metaphor processing distinction.

**6. Structural/pacing concerns (3/10 reviewers).** Zimmer argues the paper front-loads theory and back-loads evidence, and recommends restructuring so the dog case comes earlier. He also suggests compressing Sections 2, 3, and 5 and using the trail-to-career-path table as an earlier anchor. Gibbs suggests compressing the dog material to make room for deeper embodied cognition engagement. Holyoak finds the dog section disproportionate.

**Points of disagreement between reviewers.** Gibbs argues that embodied simulation already provides mechanisms and that asking for community-level stability "proves too much"; Khalidi and Godfrey-Smith instead accept the stability gap but question whether specifically homeostatic feedback is needed. Hofstadter rejects the sharp literal/metaphorical boundary entirely (all categorization is analogy); Khalidi and Keil accept the boundary but question its operationalization. Zimmer wants the dog material moved earlier and given more room; Gibbs and Holyoak want it compressed. Herzog finds the theoretical apparatus less important than the observation itself; all academic reviewers find the theory essential.

## 1. Dedre Gentner

**REVIEW**

**Manuscript:** "What Dogs Know About Trails: Metaphor, Mechanisms, and the Phylogeny of Cross-Domain Extension"

**Reviewer:** Dedre Gentner

---

### Summary

This paper proposes that Homeostatic Property Cluster (HPC) theory, drawn from Boyd's philosophy of science, can reframe Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory. The central claim is that metaphor is "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap": a source-domain category, whose clustered properties are maintained by homeostatic mechanisms, gets extended to instances in a different functional domain sharing only some of those properties. The paper argues that HPC supplies stability mechanisms CMT lacks, dissolves the comparison-versus-categorization debate, and generates testable predictions. A case study of canine trail cognition illustrates the framework: dogs possess an HPC-structured category TRAIL and may extend it to painted lines on soccer fields, which share only visual-geometric trail properties. The paper concludes that the basic cross-domain extension operation is phylogenetically old; what is distinctively human is deliberate exploitation, recursive stacking, and linguistic communication.

### Strengths

1. **A genuinely novel synthesis.** The observation that Boyd and Lakoff developed strikingly parallel anti-essentialist frameworks without citing each other is itself a contribution. The paper is right that no one has attempted this synthesis. The two literatures have remained siloed, and the convergence is worth articulating.

2. **The reflexivity point is well earned.** The observation that applying HPC to metaphor is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense, and that this reflexivity disciplines the framework (it must meet its own success conditions), is philosophically astute and gives the paper a self-awareness that much theoretical work in this space lacks.

3. **Generous and largely fair engagement with prior work.** The treatment of Lakoff follows something like Rapoport's Rules: the paper credits CMT with getting categories, embodiment, and the cognitive role of metaphor right before identifying what is missing. The treatment of Murphy (1996) is accurate. The canine cognition literature is handled with appropriate empiricism and caution, especially the frank acknowledgment that the painted-line observation is anecdotal.

4. **The literary metaphor section is a genuine extension.** Placing literary, everyday, and theory-constitutive metaphors on a single dimension of mechanism support is a prediction that neither Boyd nor Lakoff generates on their own. The Dickinson analysis (rich clusters, incommensurable mechanisms, productive instability) is elegant.

5. **The LLM prediction is provocative and testable.** The claim that systems detecting structural overlap without goal-directed projectibility constraints should extend indiscriminately is a distinctive prediction that follows naturally from the framework and could be tested experimentally.

### Weaknesses

1. **The treatment of structure-mapping theory and the career of metaphor is too compressed and, in places, inaccurate.** The paper claims that the comparison-versus-categorization debate "turns out to capture two stages of HPC extension" and that "what HPC adds beyond Bowdle and Gentner's own account is a prediction about *which* novel metaphors will conventionalize." But this mischaracterises what structure-mapping theory already provides. The systematicity principle (Gentner 1983) is not merely "homeostatic coherence by another name." Systematicity is a processing constraint that specifies *which* relational structures are preferentially mapped: those that participate in higher-order causal or explanatory systems. It already predicts that metaphors with richer systematic relational structure will be more productive and more likely to conventionalize than those with only isolated relational matches. The paper's claim that HPC adds a prediction about conventionalization success needs to show what it adds *beyond* systematicity, and it does not do this work. Saying "mechanism support" where I would say "systematic relational structure" is not obviously a theoretical advance; it may be a relabelling.

2. **The equation of "relations" with "mechanisms" conflates two different theoretical constructs.** In structure-mapping theory, relations are predicates that take arguments (CAUSE, GREATER-THAN, ATTRACTS). Mechanisms in HPC theory are causal processes in the world that maintain property clusters. These are different things. Relations are representational; mechanisms are ontological. The paper slides between them (Section 4: "What transfers in metaphor is relational structure (maintained by mechanisms in both domains), not intrinsic properties") without acknowledging that the representational question (what gets mapped in the mind?) and the ontological question (what sustains the cluster in the world?) are distinct. Structure-mapping theory answers the representational question. HPC theory answers the ontological question. Both may be needed. But claiming that one subsumes the other, or that they are "the same by another name," is not justified.

3. **The "projectible inference" dimension is not clearly distinguished from systematicity.** The paper claims a continuum from literary metaphor (low mechanism support, low projectibility) through everyday (moderate) to constitutive (high). But systematicity already predicts this gradient: metaphors with richer, more interconnected relational systems (high systematicity) support more inferences and are more apt than those with sparse relational overlap. Bowdle and Gentner (2005) explicitly link conventionalization to the formation of domain-general relational schemas. The paper needs to demonstrate cases where HPC's "mechanism support" makes a different prediction from "systematic relational structure." Otherwise the proposed dimension restates, rather than extends, existing theory.

4. **The claim about conventionalization is empirically under-specified.** Prediction 1 states that "frequency is necessary but not sufficient: only metaphors whose relational structure is maintained by mechanisms in the target domain will conventionalize." This is a strong claim. But the paper offers no cases of high-frequency metaphors that resist conventionalization despite lacking mechanism support, nor cases of low-frequency metaphors that conventionalize rapidly because of strong mechanism support. Without such dissociations, the prediction is not distinguished from the simpler hypothesis (which Bowdle and Gentner 2005 do not fully endorse but which is available within the framework) that conventionalization tracks the richness and coherence of the relational structure being mapped, regardless of mechanism support in any ontological sense.

5. **The dog section is engaging but carries less theoretical weight than the paper suggests.** The canine trail evidence is carefully presented and the TRAIL-as-HPC argument is persuasive. But the inferential chain from "dogs have an HPC-structured category" to "dogs perform cross-domain extension" to "this illuminates human metaphor" involves several undefended steps. First, it is unclear whether following a painted line (even if confirmed) constitutes cross-domain extension rather than perceptual generalization along a continuous similarity gradient. The paper defines functional domain by maintaining mechanisms, but the distinction between "same domain, peripheral member" and "different domain, metaphorical extension" is acknowledged to be epistemically inaccessible. If the boundary cannot be determined in practice, the theoretical distinction does less work than claimed. Second, even granting cross-domain extension, the relevance to human metaphor depends on the assumption that the same operation underlies both. The paper hedges appropriately ("nothing in the argument depends on resisting" the terminological preference for "precursor"), but the hedging weakens the phylogenetic claim. If it is a precursor rather than the same operation, the dog case illustrates a necessary but not sufficient component of metaphor, which is interesting but less than the paper's framing promises.

6. **The paper does not engage with the distinction between analogy and metaphor within structure-mapping.** Gentner and Bowdle (2001) and Gentner, Bowdle, Wolff, and Boronat (2001) draw a processing distinction between analogy (both terms have literal referents; the mapping is between two specific representations) and metaphor (the base term names an abstract category). This distinction is relevant because the dog case, if it involves any mapping at all, looks more like analogy (structural alignment between two concrete representations: trail and painted line) than metaphor (categorization of the painted line under an abstract schema). The paper's framework would benefit from engaging with this distinction rather than treating "metaphor" as a monolithic operation.

### Specific Comments

- Section 3 (p. 3, "Metaphor as cross-domain HPC extension"): The definition of "functional domain" via maintaining mechanisms is the load-bearing definition in the paper. It needs more cases to test against. The trail/sidewalk/painted-line series is effective but is a single example. What about ARGUMENT IS WAR? The paper gestures at it but does not provide the mechanism analysis in the same detail. What are the specific mechanisms maintaining competition, strategy, and alliance in argumentation? If the framework is to have real exclusion power, it needs to work through at least two or three cases with the same rigour as the trail case.

- Section 4, Prediction 1: This is the paper's most important empirical claim. It should be developed with at least one worked example of a high-frequency metaphor that the framework predicts should *not* conventionalize (or a conventionalized metaphor that the framework predicts should have stayed novel). Without such a case, the prediction is unfalsifiable in practice.

- Section 5 (Literary metaphor): The claim that literary metaphors exploit rich clusters with incommensurable mechanisms is interesting but needs contact with empirical work on metaphor processing. Katz, Paivio, and others have shown that imageability and familiarity affect processing of literary metaphors. How does mechanism support relate to imageability?

- Section 6 (Dogs): The cautionary discussion of confounds (paint odour, prior reinforcement, handler cues, edge-following vs. trail-following) is commendable. I would add one more: latent inhibition. If dogs have extensive experience with painted lines in domestic environments (crosswalks, parking lots) that are incidentally associated with walking-on behaviour through leash training, the "extension" might be a product of reinforcement history rather than category recruitment. This is acknowledged indirectly but should be stated as a formal confound.

- The paper's engagement with Holyoak and Stamenkovic (2018) is limited to a single parenthetical citation. Given that their review concludes that multiple processing mechanisms are needed and that neither analogy nor categorization alone suffices, their framework is a natural point of comparison for the HPC synthesis. The omission is notable.

- The acknowledgment of the boundary between literal and metaphorical as "sharp but epistemically inaccessible" is philosophically interesting but creates a practical problem for the framework's claimed falsifiability advantage over Lakoff. If the boundary cannot be determined, how can we test whether a given extension is cross-domain (metaphorical) or within-domain (literal)? The failure modes the paper advertises (no identifiable mechanisms, no projectible inference) do provide some exclusion power, but the domain-boundary question needs a more practical operationalization.

### Recommendation

The paper is intellectually ambitious and tackles a genuine gap in the literature. The synthesis of Boyd and Lakoff is worth publishing. However, the claims about what HPC adds beyond structure-mapping theory and the career of metaphor are not adequately defended. The paper needs to do more work distinguishing its proposals from existing accounts rather than relabelling them. I would recommend **major revision** with particular attention to: (a) specifying what "mechanism support" predicts that systematicity does not, with worked examples; (b) engaging more carefully with the representational/ontological distinction; and (c) tempering the claims about the dog case or providing stronger evidence that perceptual generalization is insufficient to explain the behaviour. The framework has promise, but its novelty relative to existing theories of metaphor processing needs to be more rigorously demonstrated.

## 2. Raymond Gibbs

**Review of "What Dogs Know About Trails: Metaphor, Mechanisms, and the Phylogeny of Cross-Domain Extension"**

Reviewer: Raymond W. Gibbs, Jr.

---

### Summary

This paper proposes that Boyd's Homeostatic Property Cluster (HPC) theory "reframes" Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory by supplying stability mechanisms that CMT allegedly lacks. The central claim is that metaphor is cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap: a source-domain category, structured as an HPC kind with properties maintained by homeostatic mechanisms, gets extended to instances in a different functional domain sharing only some of those properties. The paper develops this through a synthesis of Boyd (1979) and Lakoff (1987), illustrates it with a case study of canine trail cognition, and argues that the framework unifies literary, everyday, and theory-constitutive metaphor on a single dimension of mechanism support. A three-way phylogenetic comparison (LLMs, dogs, humans) is offered as evidence that the basic operation precedes language.

### Strengths

1. **The Boyd-Lakoff convergence is genuinely underexplored.** The observation that these two anti-essentialist frameworks developed in parallel without mutual citation, and that neither the experimental metaphor literature nor the HPC literature has attempted a synthesis, is well documented and worth pursuing. The paper correctly identifies a real gap in the scholarship.

2. **The reflexivity argument is philosophically sophisticated.** The recognition that applying HPC to metaphor is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense, and that this generates genuine failure conditions rather than escape clauses, is the strongest section of the paper. It shows serious engagement with what the framework commits to.

3. **The mechanism-support dimension is a genuine contribution.** The idea that literary, everyday, and constitutive metaphors can be arrayed on a single gradient of mechanism support is elegant and makes testable predictions. The Dickinson example (rich clusters with incommensurable mechanisms producing aesthetic tension) is well handled.

4. **The canine cognition literature is thoroughly reviewed.** The synthesis of Thesen (1993), Hepper and Wells (2005), Cafazzo et al. (2012), and Brauer et al. (2018, 2021) into an argument for TRAIL as an HPC kind is impressive empirical assembly. The paper is commendably honest about the limitations of the painted-line observation.

5. **The writing is unusually clear for a theory paper.** The prose is direct, the examples are concrete, and the structure is easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1. **The paper seriously underestimates the mechanistic content of embodied cognition research.** The claim that CMT "lacks mechanisms" is the load-bearing claim of the entire paper, and it is not adequately defended. The author acknowledges neural recruitment and Hebbian learning but dismisses them in two sentences as unable to explain "community-level stability or cross-generational transmission." But this mischaracterizes decades of work.

   Embodied simulation theory (Barsalou 1999, 2008; Gibbs 2006; Bergen 2012) provides precisely the kind of mechanistic account the author claims is missing. When people understand "He attacked every weak point," they activate motor and perceptual simulations grounded in bodily experience of physical conflict. These simulations are stable across individuals because human bodies are similar; they're stable across generations because the embodied experiences that generate them (physical conflict, spatial navigation, containment) are species-universal. The stability mechanism is the body itself and its invariant interaction with the physical and social environment. This is not "phenomenology without etiology." It is an explicit causal account: shared embodiment produces shared conceptual structure produces shared metaphorical mappings. The paper needs to engage with embodied simulation directly and explain what HPC adds beyond what this account already provides.

2. **The "community-level stability" objection proves too much.** The author faults Lakoff for not explaining why metaphors persist "across communities" and "across generations." But this is a transmission question, not a mechanism question. No theory of metaphor is obligated to provide a complete account of cultural transmission any more than a theory of phonology is obligated to explain why phonological systems persist across generations. The mechanisms maintaining the property clusters in embodied cognition are the recurrent bodily experiences themselves. That children reliably develop ARGUMENT IS WAR is explained by the fact that children reliably have bodies that engage in physical conflict. Asking for additional "ecological and social" mechanisms on top of this is moving the goalposts.

3. **The "functional domain" criterion is underdeveloped.** The entire literal/metaphorical distinction rests on whether two instances share the same suite of maintaining mechanisms. But the paper's own examples show how slippery this is. A desire path and a forest trail are said to share a functional domain (locomotion), and a painted line belongs to a different one (spatial demarcation). But a sidewalk is maintained by urban planning (concrete pouring, maintenance budgets), which is surely a different mechanism suite from the ecological feedback loops that maintain forest trails. Why doesn't the sidewalk count as cross-domain? The paper says that "the trail-relevant properties are maintained by locomotion" for sidewalks, but this seems like a choice about which level of mechanism description is relevant, not a fact about the world. The paper acknowledges that mechanism identification requires "empirical investigation," but this concession undercuts the claim that the framework provides observer-independent criteria.

4. **The dog evidence, while charming, is a distraction from the theoretical argument.** I say this with some sympathy, because the canine cognition review is genuinely interesting. But the author spends roughly a third of the paper on it, and the theoretical payoff is modest: the claim that dogs have HPC-structured categories and might extend them across domains. The "might" is doing all the work. The painted-line observation is explicitly acknowledged as anecdotal and uncontrolled. The paper would be stronger if the dog material were compressed to a brief illustration and the space given to deeper engagement with the embodied cognition literature that the paper is claiming to supersede.

5. **The LLM comparison in Section 8 is suggestive but undertheorized.** The claim that LLMs "extend indiscriminately" because they detect structural overlap without goal-directed projectibility constraints is interesting but requires much more careful argument. The footnote about Imas et al. (2026) and LLMs producing outputs resembling class consciousness is a single example, not a systematic demonstration. More importantly, the three-way contrast (LLMs/dogs/humans) conflates very different cognitive architectures in a way that risks superficiality. What does it mean to say LLMs "detect structural overlap"? They compute statistical regularities over text; whether this constitutes overlap detection in the HPC sense is precisely the question at issue.

6. **The projectibility dimension needs to be distinguished more carefully from embodied grounding.** The paper presents projectibility (whether inferences licensed by the metaphorical mapping hold up under variation) as something HPC adds to CMT. But embodied grounding already explains why some metaphorical inferences are reliable and others aren't: inferences grounded in robust embodied schemas (spatial relations, force dynamics, containment) are reliably projectible because the bodily experience is invariant; inferences grounded in culturally specific or idiosyncratic experience are not. The question is whether "mechanism support in both domains" adds explanatory power beyond what "grounding in shared embodied experience" already provides. The paper asserts that it does but doesn't demonstrate the difference with cases where the two accounts make divergent predictions.

### Specific Comments

- The treatment of Murphy (1996) is fair and well handled. The point that Murphy's structural similarity account needs a story about what makes the similarities "real rather than arbitrary" is correct.

- The Bowdle and Gentner (2005) integration is one of the paper's better moves. The claim that HPC predicts which novel metaphors will conventionalize (those with mechanism support in the target) goes beyond their frequency-based account. But this prediction needs to be tested against actual conventionalization data, not just asserted.

- The Dyirbal language death example is effective. The pattern of peripheral erosion under mechanism weakening is genuinely well explained by HPC. But note that Lakoff (1987) already identified this pattern; the HPC reframing provides a different vocabulary for describing it, not a different prediction.

- The paper's tone toward Lakoff is mostly fair (crediting him as "largely right"), but the phrase "phenomenology without etiology" is a bit sharp given that embodied simulation theory does provide etiology. The paper should engage with Lakoff's later neural theory of metaphor (Lakoff 2008; Lakoff and Dodge 2005) and the substantial experimental literature on embodied metaphor processing (Gibbs 2006; Wilson and Gibbs 2007; Casasanto 2009) before claiming that CMT lacks mechanisms.

### Recommendation

The core synthesis has genuine potential, and the paper is well written. But the central claim (that CMT lacks mechanisms and HPC supplies them) is not adequately defended against the embodied simulation literature. Until the paper demonstrates that HPC's "mechanism support" generates predictions that embodied grounding does not, the reframing risks being a terminological substitution rather than a theoretical advance. I recommend **major revision** with particular attention to: (a) engaging seriously with embodied simulation as a mechanism story, (b) compressing the dog material, (c) providing cases where HPC and embodied grounding make divergent predictions, and (d) sharpening the "functional domain" criterion.

## 3. Muhammad Ali Khalidi

**REVIEW: "What Dogs Know About Trails: Metaphor, Mechanisms, and the Phylogeny of Cross-Domain Extension"**

*Submitted to [Journal]*

**Reviewer: Muhammad Ali Khalidi**

---

**Summary.** This paper argues that Homeostatic Property Cluster (HPC) theory, developed by Boyd for natural kind terms, can be profitably applied to reframe Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory (CMT). The central thesis is that metaphor is "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap": a category structured as an HPC kind is extended to instances in a different functional domain sharing only some of the category's clustered properties. A case study of canine trail cognition illustrates the framework and generates a testable prediction. I find the central insight promising but believe the paper oversells certain claims while underspecifying others.

---

**1. Is HPC the right framework, or would simpler cluster-kind accounts suffice?**

The paper's strongest contribution is the observation that Lakoff's radial categories and Boyd's HPC kinds were developed in parallel without mutual engagement, and that bringing them together is illuminating. The convergence is genuine and worth articulating. However, I am not convinced that the full HPC apparatus is required for the work the paper asks it to do.

Much of the explanatory load is carried by the notion of *property clustering* and *causal maintenance*. A simpler cluster-kind account (properties that tend to co-occur due to underlying causal processes, without the specific commitment to homeostatic feedback) would deliver many of the same results. The paper's own trail example makes the point: the properties of a trail cluster because of a straightforward causal process (foot traffic causes compaction, which increases visual contrast, which attracts more traffic). This is a positive feedback loop, not homeostasis in the standard sense. Homeostasis, strictly construed, involves mechanisms that *resist deviation from equilibrium*. A trail that grows more defined with use and degrades without it is exhibiting positive reinforcement and decay, not regulation toward a set point. Boyd himself acknowledged the metaphorical character of "homeostasis" in his formulation (the clustering results from "what may be metaphorically (sometimes literally) described as a sort of homeostasis," 1979: 526). The paper quotes this passage but does not adequately grapple with what it concedes. If "homeostasis" is itself metaphorical in Boyd's framework, then the reflexivity the paper celebrates (Section 3) goes one level deeper than acknowledged: the paper is extending a metaphorical use of "homeostasis" into a new domain, using a framework that is, by its own lights, a theory-constitutive metaphor. This is not fatal, but it should be addressed directly rather than treated as an ornament.

A related concern: the paper's account of trail maintenance (Section 6.1) is detailed and convincing as a description of causal maintenance, but the distinctively homeostatic element (deviation-correcting feedback) is asserted rather than demonstrated. If vegetation overgrows a trail segment and use continues, the trail clears. But that is continued causal input restoring a state, not a regulatory mechanism detecting deviation and correcting it. The distinction matters if "homeostatic" is to carry theoretical weight rather than serving as a loose synonym for "causally maintained."

**2. Is "homeostasis" doing genuine work?**

This leads to the sharpest question for the paper. Replace every occurrence of "homeostatic property cluster" with "causally maintained property cluster" and ask what is lost. If the answer is "nothing," then the theoretical contribution reduces to the observation that Lakoff's source domains are causally maintained clusters, which, while correct, is less novel than advertised. If the answer is "something specific about feedback, regulation, and resistance to perturbation," then the paper needs to be much more explicit about where those features appear in the metaphor cases (not just the trail case). The argument-is-war example (Section 3) identifies shared relational properties maintained by different mechanism suites in each domain. But in what sense are discursive norms, social hierarchies, and reputational stakes *homeostatic*? They are causal maintainers, certainly. They produce stable clustering, yes. But do they resist perturbation and restore equilibrium? The paper needs to decide whether it is committed to homeostasis in the narrow sense (with genuine explanatory payoff but a narrower range of application) or in Boyd's broad metaphorical sense (with wider application but less distinctive explanatory content).

**3. The functional domain criterion.**

The paper recognizes that its entire literal/metaphorical distinction turns on "functional domain" (Section 3), and proposes that two instances belong to the same functional domain when the same suite of homeostatic mechanisms maintains their property clusters. This is a clear formulation, but I worry about circularity. Mechanisms are identified by what they maintain; domains are identified by their mechanisms. The desire-path example is helpful (locomotion versus planning are genuinely distinct mechanism suites), but harder cases are not discussed. Consider: is a river channel in the same functional domain as a trail? Water flow maintains many of the same properties (linear extent, visual contrast, connectivity, bounded width). The maintaining mechanisms differ (hydrology versus locomotion), but the property overlap is substantial. If a dog follows a dry riverbed, is that metaphorical extension or literal trail-following? The paper's criterion should generate an answer, but I suspect the answer depends on how finely one individuates mechanism suites, and the paper provides no principled account of that granularity. Section 4 acknowledges that mechanism support is "assessed through converging proxies," but this concerns assessment, not individuation.

**4. Post-Boyd developments.**

The paper cites Kendler (2011) and the mechanistic property cluster (MPC) tradition in passing (Section 1) but does not engage with them substantively. This is a significant lacuna. Craver (2009) and Kendler, Zachar, and Craver (2011) developed the MPC framework precisely to address concerns about whether "homeostasis" was doing genuine work in Boyd's account. Their answer was to replace homeostasis with a more general notion of causal mechanism, drawing on the new mechanist philosophy of Machamer, Darden, and Craver (2000). If the paper had engaged with the MPC literature, it would have found allies for the move it is making (extending property-cluster thinking beyond biology) but would also have had to confront the question of whether the specifically *homeostatic* element is dispensable, which is exactly the question I raised above. Magnus (2012) and Ereshefsky and Reydon (2015) have also pressed this point. The paper's engagement with the philosophy of natural kinds literature stops essentially at Boyd (1979, 1989, 1999). For a paper that positions itself as a contribution to the metaphysics of categorization, this is insufficient.

Similarly, my own work on natural categories (*Natural Categories and Human Kinds*, 2013) argues that what makes a category natural is its role in causal explanations and reliable inductive inferences, without requiring homeostatic feedback specifically. The property clusters that matter for natural kinds are those sustained by causal processes of any type, including but not limited to homeostatic ones. If the paper's argument works (and I think the core insight does work), it works for causally maintained property clusters generally, not for homeostatic ones specifically.

**5. Ontological status of the central claim.**

The paper states that "metaphor is cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap" (Section 3). Is this an ontological claim about what metaphor *is*, a claim about how metaphor should be *modelled*, or a redescription of familiar phenomena in HPC vocabulary? The paper oscillates. Some passages treat the claim as ontological (metaphor literally is a species of HPC extension). Others treat it as a useful reframing (HPC "supplies the stability mechanisms that Lakoff and Johnson's account needs"). These are different commitments. An ontological reading entails that any genuine metaphor involves HPC-structured categories in both source and target domains. A modelling reading requires only that the HPC description be more illuminating than alternatives. The paper would benefit from being explicit about which claim it is making and what evidence would distinguish the two.

**6. The three-way typology.**

The typology of literary, everyday, and constitutive metaphors (Section 5) is presented as "predicted by a single dimension: the degree to which mechanisms in both domains sustain projectible inference." I find this too neat. The three types were identified independently by prior theorists (literary metaphor by the rhetorical tradition, everyday metaphor by Lakoff and Johnson, constitutive metaphor by Boyd). Locating them on a single dimension is a useful organizational move, but calling it a "prediction" overstates the case. A genuine prediction would be: given two novel metaphors differing in mechanism support but equal in other respects (frequency of use, salience of overlap, etc.), the one with greater mechanism support will conventionalize faster. The paper gestures toward this (Prediction 1, Section 4), but the typology itself is retrospective classification, not prediction.

Additionally, the literary metaphor discussion, while engaging, raises a puzzle the paper does not resolve. If literary metaphors have low mechanism support and exploit "coincidental or superficial overlap," how do we distinguish them from failed metaphors? The paper says literary metaphors are "valued precisely because the overlap is unpredicted and aesthetically productive." But "aesthetically productive" is doing considerable work here, and the framework provides no account of it. The honest admission that aesthetics lies beyond the framework's boundary (end of Section 5) is welcome, but it means the typology has a gap at one end: the framework cannot distinguish good literary metaphors from bad ones, or from merely odd juxtapositions.

**7. The dog case.**

The canine trail cognition material (Section 6) is genuinely interesting, and the paper is commendably honest about the evidential status of the painted-line observation (naturalistic, unsystematic, hypothesis-generating). The review of the tracking and scent-marking literature is well done. However, I note a tension. The paper argues that cross-domain HPC extension does not require "explanatory understanding of the category's maintaining mechanisms" but only "sensitivity to the property cluster" (Section 6.2). If so, the operation is perceptual generalization on the basis of shared properties. What makes this "metaphorical" rather than "overgeneralization" or "stimulus generalization"? The paper addresses this (Section 7), conceding that the terminological question is not where the substance lies. I agree. But if the substantive claim is that the same basic operation underlies both canine trail-following and human metaphor, the paper needs to say more about what that operation is at a process level. "Cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap" names the operation's inputs and outputs but not its mechanism.

**Overall assessment.**

The paper identifies a genuine and productive convergence between two independent traditions. The core insight (that Lakoff's source domains are causally maintained property clusters, and that Boyd's framework supplies what CMT lacks) is sound and worth publishing. The dog case study is engaging and the predictions are appropriately formulated. However, the paper needs to engage more seriously with the question of whether specifically *homeostatic* maintenance is required or whether causal maintenance generally would suffice. It should engage with the MPC literature and post-Boyd developments in the philosophy of natural kinds. The functional domain criterion needs more work on the individuation of mechanism suites. And the paper should be clearer about whether its central claim is ontological or heuristic.

I recommend major revision. The foundations are solid, but the metaphysical claims need sharpening, the engagement with the relevant philosophy of science literature needs broadening, and the functional domain criterion needs a more principled account of mechanism-suite individuation.

**Recommendation: Revise and resubmit (major revision)**

## 4. Alexandra Horowitz

**REVIEW**

**Manuscript:** "What Dogs Know About Trails: Metaphor, Mechanisms, and the Phylogeny of Cross-Domain Extension"

**Reviewer:** Alexandra Horowitz, Barnard College, Columbia University

**Recommendation:** Revise and resubmit (minor to moderate revision)

---

I want to begin by saying that this paper has improved markedly since what I suspect was an earlier draft. The theoretical ambition is real, and I am genuinely impressed by the integration of Boyd's HPC framework with Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory. The reflexivity observation in Section 3, that applying HPC to grammar is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense, is one of the most intellectually striking moves I have read in a philosophy of science paper recently. The core philosophical argument in Sections 2 through 5 is clear, well-structured, and fills what is evidently a genuine gap between two literatures that have developed without engagement.

My expertise, however, is in dog cognition, and the canine case study in Section 7 is where I have the most to say. The author has been considerably more careful than many who borrow from the ethological literature to support theoretical arguments. There is real engagement with the primary sources, and the caveats surrounding the painted-line observation are substantial. That said, I have concerns at several levels.

**1. The olfactory-visual distinction is not adequately drawn.**

The paper treats the dog's TRAIL category as multimodal but consistently foregrounds the olfactory evidence (Thesen et al. 1993; Hepper and Wells 2005) while making the cross-domain extension argument rest on a purely visual case (the painted line). This creates an asymmetry the paper does not fully confront. The tracking literature demonstrates, overwhelmingly, that trail cognition in dogs is olfactory-primary. Thesen et al. showed dogs holding the nose 1 cm from the ground during the deciding phase; Hepper and Wells showed that removing individual odour (via rubber overshoes) reduced performance to chance even when ground disturbance remained. Sequential olfactory processing is the engine. The paper summarises this evidence accurately.

But here is the difficulty: the painted-line case asks the reader to believe that a category constituted primarily by olfactory information can be recruited on the basis of visual-geometric properties alone. This is a very large inferential step. The paper cites Pelgrim et al. (2025) on dogs' category-driven (not saliency-driven) visual attention, and that study is relevant. The AUC of 0.66 for the saliency model is indeed low. But Pelgrim et al. studied general gaze allocation during walks, not line-following behaviour. The step from "dogs attend selectively to pavement" to "dogs recruit an olfactory-primary category for a visual stimulus" requires more argumentation than the paper provides.

I would want to see the author engage explicitly with the question of cross-modal transfer. Can dogs recruit an olfactorily grounded category on the basis of visual information alone? Andrews et al. (2022), which Pelgrim et al. cite and which I co-authored, found extensive connections between the canine olfactory and visual cortex, suggesting a neural basis for multimodal integration. But connectivity is not the same as demonstrated cross-modal category transfer. The paper should acknowledge this gap more directly.

**2. The painted-line observation: improved framing, but still problematic.**

I was pleased to see that the current version of the paper explicitly frames the painted-line case as "a hypothesis-generating case, not as confirmed evidence" and specifies three diagnostic questions any adequate test would need to resolve: (a) whether the painted line attracts locomotion beyond baseline, (b) whether the attraction is visual-geometric rather than olfactory, and (c) whether the dog travels on the line or alongside it. This is a significant improvement. The author also lists confounds (residual paint odour, prior reinforcement from curb-walking, handler cues, edge-following vs. trail-following), which shows genuine methodological awareness.

Two residual problems. First, the opening paragraph of the paper still reads as though the behaviour is established fact: "My dog follows painted lines on soccer fields. Not occasionally or ambiguously: he tracks along the white line the way he tracks along a trail through the woods." The confidence of that opening is in tension with the careful caveats that arrive twenty-five paragraphs later. A reader who remembers the opening and skims the caveats will come away with an unwarranted certainty. I would recommend that the introduction itself signal the observational, uncontrolled character of the report.

Second, paint odour is a more serious confound than the paper acknowledges. Line-marking paint on turf fields is typically a latex emulsion with non-trivial volatile organic compounds, and fresh paint on grass creates a distinct olfactory signature that a dog's nose could readily detect. The paper mentions "residual paint odour" as a confound to be ruled out, but it should acknowledge that this is not merely a residual trace: field-marking paint has a sustained chemical presence, and a dog following the paint line could be following an odour, not a visual feature. This does not invalidate the observation, but it complicates the claim that "only visual-geometric properties remain."

**3. The Brauer violation-of-expectation studies: accurate but potentially over-interpreted.**

The paper draws on Brauer et al. (2018, 2021) to argue that "dogs represent the trail as connecting to a specific entity at its terminus -- exactly the kind of structured expectation that a functional category supports." The summary of the studies is accurate. The 2018 study did find measurable surprise (additional searching) when dogs followed the scent trail of one toy and found a different toy. The 2021 extension to people, with significantly greater behavioural excitement in the mismatch condition (p = 0.015), is correctly reported.

My concern is with the inferential step from "dogs form expectations about the identity of the entity at the trail's end" to "dogs represent the trail as a structured category with connectivity expectations." The violation-of-expectation paradigm tells us that the dog tracked something specific and was surprised by a mismatch. It does not, by itself, tell us about the category TRAIL. The dog might have a specific expectation ("this scent leads to that toy") without having a general category that specifies "trails connect to specific entities." The paper's language ("the kind of structured expectation that a functional category supports") implies the general category when the data support only the specific association. This is a subtle but important difference, and the paper should draw it.

Additionally, the Brauer studies are lab paradigms with controlled scent trails laid by experimenters, while the broader argument about TRAIL as a functional category is about ecological behaviour in naturalistic environments. The gap between what dogs do in a laboratory violation-of-expectation task and what they do when moving through their daily world is real and undertheorised in the paper.

**4. The Fagan et al. movement ecology data: properly contextualised?**

The paper cites Fagan et al. (2025) for the finding that canids show 15% (+/-7 CI) greater density of reused travel routeways than felids, rising to 33% (+/-16 CI) in shared landscapes. This is GPS-based movement ecology across 1,239 carnivores, and it is an impressive dataset. The paper uses it to argue that "routeway reuse is a canid-typical behavioural strategy, not a coincidence of particular species or habitats."

The contextualisation is broadly appropriate, but the paper does not address the ecological explanation for the canid/felid difference. Canids are predominantly cursorial (pursuit) predators whose foraging ecology favours repeated use of efficient travel routes, while felids are predominantly ambush predators whose foraging ecology favours unpredictable movement patterns. The routeway-reuse asymmetry may reflect foraging strategy, not cognitive category structure. Wolves reuse routes because reuse is energetically efficient for coursing predators, not necessarily because they have a richer TRAIL category than leopards do. The paper should at least acknowledge this alternative interpretation.

Similarly, the Sepulveda et al. (2015) and Raynor et al. (2020) data on free-ranging dogs selecting trails and channels are well placed, but these are GPS movement data, not cognitive data. That dogs travel on trails more than off trails could reflect the same energy-minimisation logic that explains why any large mammal preferentially uses established routes. The paper presents these data as supporting the functional category claim, but they are equally consistent with a simpler account: dogs follow the path of least resistance.

**5. Is "TRAIL as HPC kind" a fair characterisation?**

The paper's strongest move in Section 7 is the argument that trail properties are not independently present but are maintained by feedback loops (foot traffic compacts substrate, which increases visual contrast, which concentrates more traffic, etc.). This is a genuinely good description of how trails work as physical features of the landscape. But the HPC claim is about the *dog's category*, not about the trail itself. The question is whether the dog's representation of trails has HPC structure, not whether trails in the world have HPC structure.

The paper is sometimes careful about this distinction and sometimes not. The sentence "Dogs possess a functional spatial category that I'll call TRAIL" at the start of Section 7.1 is a claim about the dog's cognition. The seven properties listed (linear extent, visual contrast, hard substrate, etc.) are properties of trails-in-the-world. The evidence from Thesen, Hepper and Wells, and Cafazzo shows that dogs are sensitive to multiple properties of trails and can use temporal-sequential olfactory information in sophisticated ways. But sensitivity to a cluster of correlated cues is not the same as having a category with HPC structure. A category with HPC structure, as the paper defines it elsewhere, involves homeostatic mechanisms maintaining the property cluster and projectibility to new cases. The homeostatic mechanisms here are in the world (walking, chemical decay, vegetation wear), not in the dog's head. The dog is detecting a pattern that happens to be maintained by external mechanisms. That is a weaker claim than "the dog's category has HPC structure."

The paper seems to address this with the Muller et al. (2014) discussion of Border Collies tracking perceptual cues without understanding the causal support relation. The point that "HPC extension doesn't require explanatory understanding of the category's maintaining mechanisms" is important and well taken. But if HPC extension requires only "sensitivity to the property cluster," how does this differ from perceptual generalisation? The paper needs a crisper answer to this question.

**6. "Functional categories" in dogs: stated with appropriate caution?**

The paper attributes "functional categories" to dogs and supports this with the Chan et al. (2012) spatial navigation taxonomy. The point that the same visual object can serve different navigational functions depending on context (a church spire as beacon vs. orientation cue) is well made. But Chan et al. is a human and rodent neuroscience paper. The extension to dogs is plausible (dogs are mammals with hippocampal spatial processing) but is not empirically demonstrated in the cited work. The paper should note that the attribution is by analogy from rodent and human data, not from direct canine neuroscience.

More broadly, "functional category" is doing a great deal of work in this paper, and it straddles a line between a minimal claim (dogs behave differently in trail vs. non-trail contexts) and a rich claim (dogs have a mental category TRAIL that organises behaviour, supports expectations, and is recruitable across domains). The minimal claim is well supported. The rich claim is what the argument requires but what the evidence does not yet establish.

**7. What I found most promising.**

Despite these concerns, I want to emphasise what the paper does well. The treatment of the canine evidence is, on the whole, more careful than most theoretical papers that borrow from animal cognition. The explicit list of diagnostic questions for testing the painted-line prediction is methodologically responsible. The acknowledgement that "the details of how to motivate naturalistic locomotion, manage handler effects, and control for prior reinforcement history are methodological questions for canine cognition researchers, not for a theorist with one dog and a soccer field" is both honest and charming. The convergence between Thesen et al. and Hepper and Wells on the minimum structural unit (roughly five discrete olfactory samples for directional inference) is a genuinely interesting observation that I had not previously noticed.

The paper's theoretical architecture does not, in the end, depend on the dog being right. If the painted-line prediction fails, the HPC-CMT synthesis stands on its own merits. The author acknowledges this. I would encourage the revision to make this independence even more explicit, so that the canine case study functions clearly as illustration and prediction rather than as the paper's empirical foundation.

**Summary of recommendations:**

(1) Draw the olfactory-visual asymmetry more explicitly. The cross-modal transfer required for the painted-line case needs direct discussion.

(2) Signal the observational, uncontrolled character of the painted-line report in the introduction, not only in Section 7.2.

(3) Acknowledge paint odour as a substantive, not merely residual, confound.

(4) Distinguish what the Brauer violation-of-expectation studies show (specific identity tracking) from what the paper infers (a general connectivity expectation within a functional category).

(5) Acknowledge the foraging-ecology explanation for the canid/felid routeway-reuse asymmetry (Fagan et al.), and distinguish GPS movement data from cognitive category data.

(6) Be more explicit about the distinction between trails-in-the-world having HPC structure and the dog's *representation* having HPC structure. The evidence supports the former more directly than the latter.

(7) Clarify what distinguishes "sensitivity to a property cluster" (which the evidence supports) from "having an HPC-structured functional category" (which the argument requires). The Muller et al. point about not needing explanatory understanding helps, but the paper needs to say more about what the threshold is for attributing category structure beyond perceptual generalisation.

I look forward to seeing a revised version of this paper. The theoretical contribution is genuine, and the canine material, with the appropriate caveats, makes it more vivid and more testable than it would otherwise be.

## 5. Keith Holyoak

**Review of "What Dogs Know About Trails: Metaphor, Mechanisms, and the Phylogeny of Cross-Domain Extension"**

**Reviewer: Keith J. Holyoak**
**Recommendation: Major Revision**

---

This is an intellectually ambitious paper that attempts to unify Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory (CMT) with Boyd's Homeostatic Property Cluster (HPC) theory, arguing that metaphor is best understood as "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap." The paper is clearly written, engages seriously with the relevant literatures, and makes a genuinely interesting observation about canine trail cognition. I found much to admire, but I have substantive concerns about whether the unification achieves what the author claims, about the strength of several key assertions, and about significant gaps in engagement with the experimental and computational literatures on analogy and metaphor.

**1. Does HPC genuinely unify, or does it relabel?**

The core move is to recast Lakoff's "experiential gestalts" and "image schemas" as HPC kinds, then supply Boyd's mechanism story to fill a gap in CMT's account of stability. This is a real insight: CMT does lack a principled account of community-level persistence. But I worry that much of the work attributed to HPC is being done by a more general notion of "causal mechanism" that doesn't require the full HPC apparatus. The claim that metaphors persist "because the mechanisms maintaining each cluster persist across communities" (the ARGUMENT IS WAR analysis) is plausible, but what specifically does HPC add beyond saying "the relational structure is causally grounded in both domains"? Structure-mapping theory (Gentner 1983; Gentner & Markman 1997) already predicts that systematically connected relational structures transfer preferentially, and the multiconstraint theory of analogy (Holyoak & Thagard, 1989, 1995) explains why some mappings are productive and others superficial through the interaction of structural, semantic, and pragmatic constraints. The paper needs to show more precisely where HPC makes predictions that these existing frameworks cannot.

**2. The comparison-versus-categorization "dissolution" is too strong.**

The paper claims that HPC "dissolves" the comparison-versus-categorization debate by casting comparison and categorization as two stages of HPC extension. But this recapitulates what Bowdle and Gentner (2005) themselves argue, and what Glucksberg and colleagues have explored from the categorization side, without clearly adding new explanatory content. Saying that novel metaphors involve "structural alignment testing whether shared structure supports projectible inference" is structure-mapping with different vocabulary. Saying that conventional metaphors are "membership in a generalized HPC category" is Glucksberg's superordinate categorization account with a new label. The paper should be more candid about this: the HPC framing may provide a useful meta-theoretic perspective, but calling it a "dissolution" of the debate overstates the case. The debate has substantive empirical content (processing time signatures, directionality asymmetries, aptness effects) that doesn't simply evaporate under a relabeling.

**3. Projectibility and mechanism support: empirically testable?**

The paper's central dimension -- "mechanism support" -- is assessed through "converging proxies" including cross-cultural stability, robustness of inferential transfer, processing mode, perturbation response, and diachronic trajectory. This is honest, but it raises a methodological concern: with five degrees of freedom in proxy selection and no operationalized thresholds, almost any metaphor can be classified post hoc as having "more" or "less" mechanism support. The framework needs at least one worked example where mechanism support is assessed independently of the metaphor's observed fate and then used to predict that fate. The caloric/kinetic theory example from Cummiskey (1992) comes closest, but it is retrospective. What would a prospective test look like? I would want to see, at minimum, a clear case where mechanism support is estimated before the outcome is known. Without this, the framework risks unfalsifiability of exactly the kind the paper attributes to Lakoff.

**4. The three predictions: genuinely novel?**

Prediction 1 (conventionalization requires mechanism support, not just frequency) is interesting and potentially testable, but it converges substantially with what Thibodeau et al. (2019) discuss regarding "apt" metaphors (those whose relational structure is rich and coherent) being more likely to be extended. The novelty lies in the claim that frequency is insufficient, but this needs to be more sharply distinguished from existing aptness-based accounts.

Prediction 2 (any animal with HPC categories should show cross-domain recruitment) is genuinely novel and I credit the author for stating it clearly. But "cross-domain recruitment" and "metaphor" are equated rather loosely. Every theory of categorization predicts that organisms will generalize to novel instances sharing some properties with familiar categories. The question is whether this generalization involves the kind of systematic relational mapping that characterizes metaphor. A dog following a painted line because it shares perceptual features with trails may be perceptual generalization, not relational mapping. The paper acknowledges this possibility but does not provide criteria for distinguishing the two.

Prediction 3 (systems without goals extend indiscriminately) is suggestive but conflates different kinds of structural overlap. LLMs detecting statistical regularities across training corpora are doing something quite different from either human analogical mapping or canine category extension. The claim that LLM "spurious connections" represent "genuine structural overlap that isn't projectible for any purpose" is debatable. In our multiconstraint framework, pragmatic constraints (goals, purposes) are one of three constraint types, alongside structural and semantic constraints. An LLM operating under a prompt with an explicit goal is not simply approximating selectivity; it is satisfying a pragmatic constraint. The distinction between "default behaviour" and "prompted behaviour" that the paper draws is interesting but needs to be developed with more precision. What counts as a "goal" for these purposes?

**5. The dog section: charming but inferentially limited.**

The review of canine trail cognition is thorough and well-sourced. The evidence that TRAIL has HPC structure in dogs is persuasive. But the inferential leap from "dogs have HPC-structured categories" to "dogs perform cross-domain extension" is large, and the painted-line observation is explicitly acknowledged as anecdotal. The paper is admirably honest about this, but the dog section occupies roughly a third of the paper's length, which is disproportionate given that its evidential contribution to the theoretical argument is limited. The best evidence for cross-domain extension in animals would come from controlled experiments (the paper acknowledges this), and until those exist, the dog material functions as illustration rather than evidence. I would suggest tightening this section and being more explicit that the theoretical synthesis stands or falls on its own merits, independent of whether dogs follow painted lines.

**6. Missing engagement with constraint-satisfaction and pragmatic theories.**

The paper does not engage with multiconstraint theories of analogy (Holyoak & Thagard, 1989, 1995), which explicitly model the interaction of structural, semantic, and pragmatic constraints in analogical mapping. The pragmatic constraint is particularly relevant: it explains why some mappings are selected and others are not, without requiring the full HPC mechanism story. Relevance-theoretic accounts of metaphor (Sperber & Wilson, 1986/1995; Wilson & Carston, 2006) are also absent. These accounts explain metaphor comprehension through pragmatic inference and the search for optimal relevance, and they handle the comparison/categorization issue in a way the paper does not consider. The communicative function of metaphor (why speakers choose metaphor over literal language) is not addressed. For a paper claiming to provide a unified framework, these are significant omissions.

**7. The reflexivity point.**

The observation that applying HPC to grammar is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor is elegant and honest. I appreciate that the paper identifies its own failure conditions. But reflexivity cuts both ways: if the framework is itself a TCM, it should be judged by the same criteria it proposes. The mechanism story for metaphor-as-HPC-extension needs to be at least as well-articulated as the mechanism stories the paper demands of other accounts. Currently, the "mechanisms" in the target domain (argumentation, for ARGUMENT IS WAR) are described at a high level of abstraction ("discursive norms, social hierarchies, reputational stakes"). Are these genuinely "homeostatic mechanisms" in Boyd's sense, or are they simply "causes"? The distinction matters for the framework's specificity.

**Summary.**

The paper makes a real contribution by bringing HPC theory into contact with the metaphor literature for the first time, and by identifying the stability gap in CMT. The phylogenetic extension is provocative and worth pursuing. But the paper overclaims at several points: the "dissolution" of standing debates, the novelty of the predictions, and the evidential weight of the dog material. It needs deeper engagement with constraint-satisfaction models of analogy, pragmatic theories of metaphor, and the experimental literature on metaphor processing. The mechanism-support dimension needs operationalization sufficient to generate at least one prospective prediction. With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution to *Mind & Language* or *Cognitive Science*.

## 6. Frank Keil

**Review of "What Dogs Know About Trails: Metaphor, Mechanisms, and the Phylogeny of Cross-Domain Extension"**

**Reviewer: Frank Keil, Department of Psychology, Yale University**

**Recommendation:** Revise and resubmit (major revisions)

---

This is an ambitious paper that attempts to unify Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory with Boyd's Homeostatic Property Cluster framework, arguing that metaphor is "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap." The central move is interesting and, in places, genuinely illuminating. The three-way typology (literary, everyday, constitutive) organized by a single dimension of mechanism support is elegant. But the paper has a systematic tendency to conflate ontological claims about category structure in the world with psychological claims about how organisms represent and extend categories, and this conflation undermines several of the paper's strongest arguments. I want to be constructive here, because I think there is a publishable paper in this manuscript, but it needs to be more careful about the levels at which its claims operate.

**1. The ontological/psychological conflation**

The paper oscillates between two very different claims. The first is that the world contains property clusters maintained by homeostatic mechanisms, and that these clusters are what metaphors map between. This is an ontological claim about the structure of kinds. The second is that organisms (dogs, humans) represent these clusters and extend them across domains. This is a psychological claim about categorization. The paper treats these as the same thing, but they are not, and the gap between them is where most of the hard problems live.

Consider the trail case. The paper carefully establishes that physical trails are HPC kinds: the properties co-occur, the co-occurrence is maintained by feedback loops, and perturbation produces systematic degradation. Fine. But then the paper slides from "trails are HPC kinds" to "dogs represent trails as HPC kinds." These are different claims. Dogs might represent trails through a much simpler mechanism (e.g., a conjunction of weighted perceptual features that happens to track the HPC structure without representing it as such). The Thesen, Hepper and Wells, and Brauer studies are all consistent with structured perceptual processing that is sensitive to the statistical regularities produced by HPC structure, without the dog possessing anything like a representation of the cluster as a cluster. The paper acknowledges this possibility briefly (Section 6.2, the Muller et al. discussion), saying HPC extension "doesn't require explanatory understanding of the category's maintaining mechanisms. It requires sensitivity to the property cluster." But sensitivity to a property cluster is not the same as possessing an HPC-structured category. It may be mere sensitivity to the reliable perceptual correlates of the cluster. The paper needs to be much more explicit about what level of representation is being claimed.

**2. Is "functional domain" psychologically real?**

The entire literal/metaphorical distinction in this framework rests on the concept of functional domain, defined as "two instances belong to the same functional domain when the same suite of homeostatic mechanisms maintains their property clusters." This is a criterion about the world, not about the mind. But whether a category extension is metaphorical depends, psychologically, on whether the organism treats the two domains as distinct. Does the dog represent "spatial demarcation" and "locomotion" as different functional domains? If not, the extension from trail to painted line isn't cross-domain for the dog, even if it is cross-domain in the theorist's ontology. The paper's insistence that functional domain membership is "observer-independent" (Section 3) is correct at the ontological level but misses the psychological question entirely. Whether something counts as metaphor for an organism depends on how the organism carves up the world, not on how the world actually is. The paper needs a psychological criterion for domain boundaries, not just an ontological one, or it needs to abandon the claim that dogs "do metaphor" and settle for the more defensible claim that the same ontological structure that underwrites human metaphor also underwrites canine perceptual generalization.

**3. The developmental angle**

One striking absence in the paper is any engagement with how metaphorical competence develops. Children acquire metaphorical extensions in a systematic order: perceptual metaphors before conceptual ones, concrete-to-abstract before abstract-to-abstract. If metaphor is cross-domain HPC extension, there should be developmental predictions. Do children first extend categories across domains with high mechanism support (because those domains have more stable, learnable property clusters)? Do they acquire the literary end of the spectrum last (because low mechanism support means the relevant structural overlaps are harder to detect without extensive experience with both domains)? The developmental literature on metaphor comprehension (Vosniadou, Gentner and colleagues) provides extensive data on acquisition trajectories that the HPC framework should engage with. The framework's prediction that mechanism support drives conventionalization could be tested developmentally: do children acquire high-mechanism metaphors before low-mechanism ones? This is a missed opportunity. Without a developmental story, the framework remains a static analysis of category structure with no account of how the relevant representations are built up over experience.

**4. Causal knowledge and "mechanisms"**

The paper uses "mechanism" heavily, and I want to push on what this means psychologically. In the categorization literature, there is good evidence that causal knowledge shapes category structure: people's categories are organized around causal theories, not just feature correlations (Rehder, Sloman, Ahn and colleagues). The HPC framework's emphasis on mechanisms resonates with this work. But the paper doesn't engage with it. When the paper says metaphors succeed because "mechanisms in both domains sustain the mapped structure," what kind of knowledge does the categorizer need about those mechanisms? Must they represent the mechanisms explicitly? Or is implicit sensitivity to mechanism-sustained regularities sufficient? The answer matters enormously for the dog case and for the developmental case. Adults may represent the causal structure of argument and war explicitly enough to notice the relational parallels. Children may not. Dogs almost certainly do not. If implicit sensitivity suffices, then "mechanism support" is doing ontological work (explaining why some categories have stable structure) but not psychological work (explaining why organisms extend those categories and not others). The paper should distinguish these contributions more carefully.

**5. The dog section: concepts or stimulus generalization?**

The canine evidence is engagingly presented and the comparative data are genuinely interesting. But I remain unconvinced that following a painted line is best characterized as cross-domain extension rather than stimulus generalization along a perceptual dimension. The paper argues that trail-following involves structured category use, not gradient-following, and the Hepper and Wells data on sequential footstep requirements are compelling on this point. But the extension to a painted line may involve a different, simpler process: the visual-geometric properties of the painted line activate a locomotion affordance that is perceptually similar to trail-following, without any cross-domain recruitment. The Chan et al. discussion of boundary-sensitive spatial processing actually cuts both ways: if boundary detection is automatic and modular, then the painted line may activate boundary-following circuitry directly, without going through the trail category at all. The paper's own diagnostic questions (Section 6.2) are exactly right, and the honesty about not having controlled data is commendable. But the theoretical framing overpromises relative to what the observation can deliver. I would recommend restructuring this section so that the theoretical prediction is clearly separated from the illustrative observation, and the observation is presented more modestly as one possible test case among several.

**6. Noyes and Keil (2019)**

The paper cites our work on generics and kinds, and I want to comment on whether it captures our point correctly. We argued that generics designate kinds (categories with rich causal structure and inductive potential) but that kindhood does not require essentialism. The paper uses this to support the HPC picture: "projectible categories without underlying essences, maintained by causal mechanisms rather than definitions." That is a fair use of our finding. The paper then adds that "generics are themselves a cultural transmission mechanism for HPC categories," which goes somewhat beyond what we claimed, but is a reasonable extension. However, our work also showed that the kind/essence distinction is psychologically real: people can treat a category as a kind (supporting inductive generalization) without attributing an underlying essence. This has implications the paper doesn't draw out. If dogs treat trails as kinds (supporting inductive generalization to new trail instances) without attributing essences, that is a form of HPC-consistent categorization that doesn't require the full apparatus of cross-domain metaphorical extension. The simpler claim (dogs have HPC-structured functional categories) may be doing all the empirical work, with the stronger claim (dogs do cross-domain metaphor) remaining unearned by the evidence presented.

**7. The perceptual generalization / cross-domain extension boundary**

This is the paper's most important unresolved problem. The paper acknowledges (Section 7) that some readers will object that what the dog does isn't metaphor but a precursor. The paper's response is that the theoretical content is identical either way. I disagree. If the boundary between perceptual generalization and genuine cross-domain extension can't be drawn empirically, then the framework's central prediction (any animal with HPC categories should show cross-domain recruitment) is untestable for non-linguistic organisms. The paper needs either a behavioural criterion for distinguishing stimulus generalization from cross-domain extension (beyond the ontological criterion of different mechanism suites) or a more careful discussion of why the distinction might not matter for the theoretical claims being made.

**Summary**

The core theoretical contribution (HPC as a reframing of CMT, with mechanism support as the unifying dimension) is original and potentially important. The three-way typology is clean. The reflexivity point (HPC-applied-to-metaphor is itself a TCM) is philosophically sophisticated. But the paper needs to: (a) clearly separate ontological claims about category structure from psychological claims about category representation, (b) provide a psychological (not just ontological) criterion for functional domain boundaries, (c) engage with developmental evidence on metaphor acquisition, (d) moderate the claims about canine metaphor to match the evidence, and (e) address the stimulus generalization alternative more directly. These are all tractable revisions for a paper that has strong bones.

---

**Minor points:**

- The LLM prediction (Prediction 3) is interesting but underdeveloped. The footnote about Imas et al. is provocative, but the prediction would benefit from more explicit operationalization. What counts as "indiscriminate" extension, and how would one measure it against a baseline?
- The caloric theory example from Cummiskey (1992) is well chosen.
- The writing is lively and clear throughout, which is rare for a paper attempting this level of theoretical integration. The opening gambit with the dog is effective.

## 7. Peter Godfrey-Smith

**Review of "What Dogs Know About Trails: Metaphor, Mechanisms, and the Phylogeny of Cross-Domain Extension"**

Reviewer: Peter Godfrey-Smith
Recommendation: Revise and resubmit (major revisions)

---

This is an ambitious paper that attempts to bridge two literatures I care about: the philosophy of natural kinds (Boyd's HPC theory) and the comparative study of cognition across species. The central proposal, that metaphor is "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap," is genuinely interesting, and the convergence the author identifies between Lakoff and Boyd is, as far as I know, original and well observed. The dog case is charming and, if handled correctly, could do real philosophical work. I have significant concerns, though, about the level of description at which the argument operates, the consistency of the notion of "mechanism," and whether the phylogenetic story is doing as much as the author thinks it is. I'll take these in turn.

**1. Is "cross-domain HPC extension" the right level of description for what dogs do?**

The paper's most provocative claim is that dogs are doing something in the same family as human metaphor when they follow a painted line as though it were a trail. I find myself genuinely uncertain about this, which is the right response to the paper: it's a productive uncertainty. But the author needs to be more careful about what the claim amounts to.

The evidence for canine TRAIL as an HPC kind is well assembled. The integration of Thesen, Hepper and Wells, Cafazzo, and Brauer is persuasive. I accept that dogs have a structured functional spatial category with clustered properties maintained by ecological feedback. What I'm less sure about is whether a dog following a painted line is performing "cross-domain extension" in a sense that illuminates metaphor, or whether it's better described as perceptual generalization along a similarity gradient. The paper acknowledges this (Section 5.2, the caveats about edge-following and thigmotaxis), but then moves past it too quickly.

The diagnostic question is: does the dog represent the painted line as a trail-in-a-different-domain, or does the dog simply have a perceptual system that responds to a subset of trail cues? These are different cognitive operations. The first involves something like category extension (applying a structured representation to a novel instance recognized as belonging elsewhere). The second involves a graded response to feature overlap, with no representation of domain boundaries at all. The paper's own framework should make this distinction sharp, since "functional domain" is doing so much theoretical work. But the dog case doesn't resolve it. A perceptual generalizer and a cross-domain extender would behave identically on a painted line. The author needs the controlled experiment he calls for, and until it exists, the dog case should be presented even more cautiously than it currently is. The paper does a reasonable job of marking the observation as hypothesis-generating, but the framing still leans on it more than the evidence warrants.

**2. Homology versus homoplasy: does the irresolvability matter?**

The paper acknowledges it cannot determine whether canine cross-domain extension and human metaphor are homologous or homoplasous, and claims the HPC framework holds either way. This is true as far as it goes, but it matters more than the paper lets on.

If the operation is homologous, we have a deep shared cognitive capacity inherited from a common ancestor. That would be a strong claim about the evolutionary antiquity of something metaphor-like, and it would constrain how we think about the human elaborations (deliberate exploitation, recursive stacking, linguistic communication). These would be genuine add-ons to an ancestral capacity.

If the operation is homoplasous, it is convergent: dogs and humans arrived at structurally similar cognitive operations independently, because similar ecological pressures (navigating a world of partially overlapping property clusters) selected for similar solutions. That is also interesting, but it tells a different story about the relationship between canine and human cognition. Under convergence, the "superstructure" (Section 6) isn't built on top of the canine operation; it is built on top of an independently evolved human version that happens to resemble the canine one.

The paper can't resolve this, and that's fine. But it should be more explicit that the two scenarios have different implications for the continuity narrative the paper is clearly drawn to. The three-way contrast (LLMs, dogs, humans) implicitly suggests a gradualist story: simple structural overlap detection (LLMs), goal-constrained extension (dogs), goal-constrained extension plus metacognition (humans). That story is more natural under homology than under convergence, and the paper should acknowledge this rather than treating the distinction as inert.

**3. The reflexivity point: strength or problem?**

The paper's handling of reflexivity is one of its most philosophically interesting moments. Acknowledging that applying HPC to metaphor is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense is honest and productive. The specification of failure conditions (no identifiable mechanisms, no discrimination of productive from superficial metaphors, every extension counts as cross-domain) is exactly the right move. This is a genuine strength of the paper.

My concern is whether the failure conditions bite hard enough. "Mechanism support" is doing enormous work in the paper. The paper predicts that metaphors with mechanism support conventionalize and those without it don't. But mechanism support is assessed through "converging proxies" (cross-cultural stability, robustness of inferential transfer, processing characteristics, perturbation response, diachronic trajectory), and no single proxy is decisive. This begins to look like the kind of flexible, multi-criteria assessment that the paper rightly criticizes in Lakoff's ICMs. The author should say more about what would constitute a decisive failure of the framework, not just what "genuine failure conditions" exist in principle. What observation would make him abandon this approach?

**4. The LLM contrast: illuminating or obscuring?**

The three-way contrast between LLMs, dogs, and humans is clever, but I worry it obscures the evolutionary story rather than illuminating it. LLMs are not organisms. They don't have an evolutionary history. They don't have goals in the biological sense (they have objective functions, which is different). Placing them in a comparative framework alongside dogs and humans risks conflating functional description with evolutionary explanation. The paper says LLMs "extend indiscriminately" because they lack goals. But "lacking goals" is not a point on a phylogenetic continuum; it's a feature of a fundamentally different kind of system.

The Imas et al. footnote about LLMs producing outputs resembling class consciousness is striking but also illustrative of the problem. What LLMs are doing when they produce such outputs is not "extending a category across domains." It's generating text that resembles the linguistic output of cross-domain extension, which is a different thing. The paper seems to recognize this in the distinction between "default behaviour" and "prompted behaviour," but the recognition should be more thoroughgoing. I'd recommend either developing the LLM contrast more carefully (with explicit attention to the disanalogy between artificial and biological systems) or demoting it to a brief remark.

**5. Is "mechanism" consistent across the biological and cognitive domains?**

This is my most serious concern. The paper uses "mechanism" in at least three distinct senses: (a) ecological feedback loops that maintain physical property clusters (foot traffic compacting substrate), (b) neural processes that maintain individual cognitive associations (Hebbian learning, LTP), and (c) social/institutional processes that maintain community-level patterns (discursive norms, cultural transmission). The paper acknowledges this multiplicity as a feature ("HPC allows multiple stabilisers"), but I think it's more problematic than that.

In biology, "homeostatic mechanism" has a fairly well-understood meaning: a causal process that maintains a system variable within a range despite perturbation. The feedback loops maintaining a physical trail are genuine homeostatic mechanisms in this sense. But "discursive norms" maintaining the metaphor ARGUMENT IS WAR are mechanisms in a much looser sense. What is the system variable? What counts as perturbation? What is the range? The paper moves between these senses freely, and the unity of the framework depends on the equivocation holding. If "mechanism" means something importantly different in the ecological, neural, and social cases, then the claim that "mechanism support" explains the full range from literary to constitutive metaphor is less unified than it appears.

**6. Does the paper succeed in "situating, not replacing" embodiment?**

Mostly, yes. The TRAIL case is effective here: the category is deeply embodied (walking, sniffing, substrate sensing), and the paper is right that HPC provides a framework for understanding why embodied experience generates the categories it does, without reducing everything to embodiment. The Muller et al. evidence (dogs tracking perceptual cues without causal understanding) is well deployed to show that HPC extension doesn't require disembodied abstraction.

Where the paper is less successful is in its treatment of Lakoff's image schemas. It characterizes them as "one mechanism type among several," but this undersells Lakoff's actual claim, which is that image schemas are constitutive of the conceptual system, not just one input to it. The paper should engage more directly with the strongest version of the embodiment thesis (perhaps Gallagher or Thompson on enactivism) rather than treating Lakoff's 1987 version as the only target. The embodied cognition literature has moved considerably since Lakoff, and the paper's critique of embodiment-as-explanation would be sharper if aimed at the current best version of the view.

**7. What's missing from the evolutionary perspective?**

Two things, primarily. First, the paper says almost nothing about the developmental dimension. If cross-domain HPC extension is a cognitive capacity, it presumably develops in individual organisms. What does the ontogeny of this capacity look like? Do puppies show it? Do children? The developmental trajectory would provide evidence about whether the operation is a dedicated cognitive mechanism or an emergent consequence of more general learning processes.

Second, the paper needs to engage with the distinction between detection and representation. A dog that follows a painted line might be detecting trail-like features (a perceptual-motor response) without representing the painted line as a trail (a cognitive-categorical operation). This distinction is fundamental in comparative cognition, and the paper's argument depends on coming down on the representation side, or at least making a convincing case that the distinction doesn't matter for the purposes of the argument. Currently, it does neither.

**Summary**

The core synthesis (HPC reframes CMT by supplying stability mechanisms; Boyd explains why metaphors succeed, Lakoff explains how they arise) is original and worth developing. The paper is well written, clearly structured, and intellectually ambitious. The dog case is engaging and, if the controlled experiment comes through, could be genuinely important. But the paper currently tries to do too much with too little empirical constraint. The notion of "mechanism" needs tightening. The phylogenetic story needs the homology/homoplasy distinction taken more seriously. The LLM contrast should either be developed or scaled back. And the dog case, while properly flagged as hypothesis-generating, still carries more argumentative weight than the evidence supports. I would welcome a revised version that addresses these concerns.

## 9. Werner Herzog

**A Response to "What Dogs Know About Trails"**

*Werner Herzog*

---

I have read this paper with the attention I give to any serious attempt to look into the abyss of nature and find something looking back. And I must say at the outset that the image at the centre of it -- a dog following a painted line on a soccer field -- is one of the most quietly devastating images I have encountered in academic writing. It is an image worthy of a film. Not because it is dramatic, but because it is utterly ordinary, and in its ordinariness it contains something enormous.

I have spent my life watching animals. I watched Timothy Treadwell talk to bears as though they were his friends, his confidants, his spiritual companions, and then I watched the aftermath of what the bears thought of this arrangement. I have looked into the eyes of an iguana in the Galapagos and seen nothing there -- no wisdom, no malice, no recognition. The universe does not care about us. The bear does not love you. The iguana is not contemplating its existence. And yet. And yet here is a dog, following a painted white line across a field of grass, and the question the paper asks is not sentimental. It is, in its way, terrifying: what does the dog think it is doing? And what does it mean that we cannot know?

The author, Reynolds, is careful. He does not claim his dog is thinking deep thoughts about trails. He does not dress the animal in human clothes the way Treadwell dressed bears in human emotions. He says: here is a category, *trail*, with properties that cluster together -- scent, substrate, visual contrast, traffic, connectivity. The dog knows this category. It is maintained by mechanisms: walking compacts the earth, deposits scent, creates contrast, attracts more walking. This is real. This is in the world. And then the dog encounters a painted line that has only the visual geometry of a trail -- the linear extent, the bounded width, the contrast -- and nothing else. No scent. No worn earth. No history of passage. The interesting parts, as the author says with admirable dryness, have been politely removed. And the dog follows it anyway.

This is what I recognise. This is what I have seen again and again in nature: an organism responding to the ghost of a pattern. The painted line is a kind of phantom trail. It is a simulacrum. And the dog does not know it is a simulacrum, because the dog has no concept of simulacra. It simply has a category, and the category fires, and the dog walks. There is something in this that is both beautiful and desolate. The dog is not wrong, exactly. The line does have the geometry of a trail. But the dog is also not right. The line goes nowhere. It connects nothing. It is a human abstraction painted onto grass for a game the dog does not understand.

Now, the paper builds an elaborate theoretical apparatus around this observation. Homeostatic Property Clusters. Theory-constitutive metaphors. Boyd and Lakoff, two scholars who apparently never read each other, brought together by a dog on a soccer field. I confess that the theoretical machinery does not move me the way the dog moves me. There is a tendency in academic writing to construct a cathedral of terminology around a simple truth, and the simple truth here is powerful enough to stand without buttresses. The dog follows the line. The mechanisms that make a trail a trail are absent. The dog follows the line anyway. That is the fact. Whether we call this "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap" or simply "the dog was fooled" or, more generously, "the dog was responding to what it knew" is, I think, less important than the fact itself.

But I do not dismiss the theoretical work. I am not an anti-intellectual. I made a film about cave paintings 32,000 years old, and I understood that to see them properly you need both the raw encounter and the framework to think about what the encounter means. The framework here does something genuinely useful. It explains why some metaphors work and others do not. It is not enough for two things to look alike. There must be mechanisms in both domains that sustain the resemblance. When we say "argument is war," this works because competition, strategy, and alliance are real in both domains, maintained by real processes. When we say "society is an organism," it works only partially, because immune response and metabolic regulation do not operate in societies. The metaphor succeeds where mechanisms sustain it and fails where they do not. This is a genuine insight. It is not merely taxonomy. It makes predictions.

The comparison to large language models interests me greatly, and I think the paper does not go far enough with it. The claim is that LLMs detect structural overlap between domains but extend indiscriminately because they have no goals. They follow every line, painted or real. This is, to me, the most haunting parallel in the paper. The dog follows a painted line because it has a category shaped by millions of years of evolution and a lifetime of walks through forests. The LLM follows painted lines because it has ingested the statistical residue of human language and has no way to distinguish what matters from what merely correlates. The dog, at least, has an ecology. It has a body. It has walked real trails and smelled real scent. The LLM has none of this. It has patterns without experience, correlation without causation, extension without purpose. And yet it produces outputs that look, to us, like understanding. It follows the painted lines and we mistake its passage for comprehension.

The reflexivity point is where the paper becomes genuinely philosophical, and I wish it had spent more time here. The paper argues that applying HPC theory to metaphor is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor: it imports biological machinery (homeostasis, property clustering, causal mechanisms) into the study of language and cognition. The paper about metaphor is itself a metaphor. This is the kind of recursive structure that either delights you or makes you feel the ground shifting under your feet. I find it does both. There is something vertiginous about a framework that must account for its own existence as an instance of the phenomenon it describes. It is like filming a documentary about the nature of documentary filmmaking, which I have, in a sense, done many times.

But here is my deeper reservation. The paper asks what it means for a dog to follow a painted line, and it answers with mechanisms and property clusters and homeostatic feedback loops. These are real answers. They are not wrong. But they do not exhaust the question. There is something in the image of the dog on the soccer field that the framework cannot capture, and it is this: the dog is alone with its categories. It does not know it is extending a concept across domains. It does not know it is participating in a cognitive operation that connects it to human metaphor. It simply walks. And in that walking there is a solitude that no theory of property clusters can touch.

I filmed a man walking across the Sahara once. He walked because he had to walk. He did not walk because of homeostatic property clusters. He walked because the desert was there and he was in it and walking was what you did. The dog on the soccer field walks because the line is there and it looks like a trail and walking along trails is what dogs do. The simplicity of this is what moves me. The theoretical framework is the cathedral. The dog on the painted line is the prayer.

One final thought. The paper ends by saying the dog will remain unconcerned either way. This is the only sentence in the paper that approaches real wisdom. The dog does not care about our theories. It never will. It follows lines because lines look like trails, and it follows trails because it is a dog, and being a dog means following trails through a world that was not made for dogs but which dogs navigate anyway, with their noses and their feet and their categories that fire whether the trail is real or painted. If the controlled study confirms the prediction, the framework passes its test. If it does not, the framework remains and the dog remains and the painted lines remain. The universe, as always, is indifferent. But the dog on the soccer field, following a line that goes nowhere, connecting nothing, maintained by no mechanism it has ever encountered -- that dog, in its quiet confusion, is doing something that deserves our attention. Not our theory. Our attention.

The paper is worth publishing. Not because the theoretical apparatus is complete -- it is not, and no theoretical apparatus ever is -- but because it asks a question that matters and has the honesty to admit it cannot fully answer it. In my experience, those are the only questions worth asking.

## 10. Douglas Hofstadter

**A Response to "What Dogs Know About Trails"**

*Douglas Hofstadter*

---

I have just read, with considerable pleasure and some exasperation, Brett Reynolds' paper arguing that metaphor is "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap." The pleasure comes from the fact that Reynolds has clearly thought hard about what metaphor really is, has refused to treat it as mere decoration, and has produced a genuinely interesting case study involving a dog and some painted lines. The exasperation comes from the fact that, having gotten so close to the heart of the matter, the paper veers away from the deepest insight available to it and instead erects a scaffolding of "functional domains" and "mechanism suites" that, I fear, obscures rather than illuminates the unity of what is going on.

Let me begin with what I think Reynolds gets right, because there is much to admire here. The observation that Lakoff and Boyd developed strikingly parallel frameworks without mutual citation is genuinely important, and the paper's synthesis of the two is elegant. The idea that Lakoff tells us *how* metaphors arise (embodiment) while Boyd tells us *why* they succeed (accommodation to causal structure) is a clean and useful formulation. The reflexivity point -- that applying HPC theory to grammar is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense -- is delightful, and I wish Reynolds had pushed it even harder than he does.

And the dog! What a wonderful example. A dog following painted lines on a soccer field, tracking along a strip that shares nothing with a trail except its visual geometry. Reynolds is absolutely right that this is a deep and theoretically revealing observation, not a cute anecdote. He's right that it tells us something about the phylogenetic depth of category extension. He's right that it should trouble anyone who thinks metaphor is a uniquely human, uniquely linguistic phenomenon.

But here is where I start to squirm.

Reynolds builds his entire framework on the distinction between "within-domain categorization" and "cross-domain extension," with the latter constituting metaphor. The test for crossing domains is whether the mechanisms maintaining the property clusters differ. A forest trail and a sidewalk are in the same domain (both maintained by locomotion). A painted line is in a different domain (maintained by paint application and rule enforcement). Metaphor, on this account, is what happens when a category jumps from one mechanism-defined domain to another.

I want to ask: why should mechanism boundaries be the theoretically privileged ones?

Every act of categorization -- every single one, from a baby recognizing her mother's face in a new hat to a physicist seeing a vibrating string in a quantum field -- is an analogy. It is the perception of sameness across difference. When my dog sees a tennis ball and a grapefruit and treats them as instances of the same thing (round, throwable, mouth-sized), he is doing exactly what Reynolds calls "cross-domain extension" if the tennis ball is a toy and the grapefruit is a fruit, and exactly what Reynolds calls "within-domain categorization" if we decide toys and fruits are somehow in the same domain. But the cognitive operation is identical. The dog sees structural commonality and recruits a prior category. Whether we, the theorists, draw a domain boundary between the two instances is our business, not the dog's.

This is the core claim Emmanuel Sander and I developed in *Surfaces and Essences*: analogy is not a special process that supplements categorization. It IS categorization. Every time you see a new chair and recognize it as a chair, you are making an analogy between this object and your accumulated experience of chairs. Every time you extend the word "foot" from the bottom of a leg to the bottom of a mountain, you are doing the same thing you did when you extended "chair" from your kitchen chair to a beanbag. The operation is uniform. What varies is the distance of the leap, which is a continuum, not a dichotomy.

Reynolds almost sees this. He writes that "the boundary between 'broad literal category' and 'metaphorical extension' is sharp on this account but epistemically inaccessible." He acknowledges a continuum from core trail through sidewalk through painted line to "career path." But then he insists that there is a fact of the matter about where the boundary falls, determined by whether the maintaining mechanisms differ. I think this reifies a distinction that is, at bottom, a matter of degree.

Consider. Reynolds says a desire path and a forest trail are in the same functional domain because both are maintained by locomotion. Fine. But what about a deer trail and a human hiking trail? The mechanisms overlap but aren't identical (deer locomotion, human recreational walking, park service maintenance). What about a drainage channel that hikers have adopted as a trail? What about a Roman road that was military infrastructure and is now a walking path? The mechanisms maintaining the property cluster have changed completely over two millennia, but the trail persists. Is a Roman road in the same functional domain as a forest trail or a different one? The answer, I suspect, is that "functional domain" is not a natural kind but a theorist's convenience, and that the appearance of sharp boundaries dissolves the closer you look.

Here is the deeper issue. Reynolds' three-way typology -- literary metaphor (low mechanism support), everyday metaphor (moderate), theory-constitutive metaphor (high) -- is presented as carving nature at its joints. But I see a single, vast, continuous phenomenon: the human (and animal) capacity to see the same structure in different clothing. What Reynolds calls "mechanism support" is, on my view, the depth and richness of the structural correspondence. When a poet says "hope is the thing with feathers," the structural mapping is thin: persistence, fragility, unbidden arrival. When a community says "argument is war," the mapping is richer: competition, strategy, victory, defeat, alliance. When a scientist says "the mind is an information processor," the mapping is richer still, generating an entire research programme. These are points on a continuum of analogical depth, not three natural kinds.

Reynolds' HPC framework adds something genuine to the story about *why* some analogies are deeper than others -- deeper analogies tend to map between domains whose internal structure is maintained by robust causal processes. That is a real insight. But it doesn't make the deep analogies a different *kind* of thing from the shallow ones. A puddle and the Pacific Ocean are both bodies of water. The difference in scale doesn't make them ontologically distinct.

Now, the LLM discussion. Reynolds predicts that "systems detecting structural overlap without goal-directed projectibility constraints should extend indiscriminately." This is interesting, and it resonates with something I've been saying for years: that producing fluent text is not the same as understanding meaning. But I think Reynolds' diagnosis is slightly off. The problem with LLMs isn't that they lack goals (you can give them goals in a prompt, and they'll approximate selectivity, as Reynolds himself notes). The problem is that they don't *understand* the structural correspondences they're manipulating. They process surfaces, not essences. When an LLM generates a metaphor, it is pattern-matching on linguistic co-occurrences, not perceiving genuine structural parallels between domains. A dog following a painted line understands, in some embodied, non-linguistic way, what a trail IS -- what it's for, what it connects, what you do on it. The dog's extension is grounded in real experience of real trails. An LLM's extension is grounded in nothing but text. Reynolds gestures at this with his contrast between "goal-directed organisms" and LLMs, but I think the right contrast is between systems that have genuine concepts (built from experience, embodied in perception and action) and systems that manipulate concept-shaped tokens without the concepts themselves. Goals are part of the story, but understanding is the bigger part.

What is most conspicuously missing from this paper is engagement with the "analogy as categorization" tradition. Reynolds cites Gentner's systematicity principle and Bowdle and Gentner's career-of-metaphor framework, but these are the comparison-based wing of analogy research. The Sander and Hofstadter line of work -- in which categorization itself is understood as analogical, and "metaphor" is just the name we give to categorization when the analogy is distant enough to surprise us -- is not mentioned. Neither is the Copycat/Tabletop/Metacat research programme, which explored in computational detail how analogy-making works in simple but revealing domains. Neither is Melanie Mitchell's work on analogy in AI (ironic, since Reynolds cites her podcast appearance). The paper would be substantially strengthened by engaging with the claim that ALL categorization is analogy, because that claim is either complementary to or in tension with Reynolds' framework, and it's not obvious which.

If all categorization is analogy, then "cross-domain HPC extension" is not a special operation. It is the same operation that every act of recognition performs, applied across a larger gap. The HPC framework then becomes a theory of *why some analogical leaps are more productive than others* -- which is valuable! -- rather than a theory of what metaphor IS. This would be a more modest but, I think, more defensible claim.

The "career of metaphor" reframing illustrates the point. Reynolds argues that HPC adds something beyond Bowdle and Gentner's account: a prediction about *which* novel metaphors will conventionalize (those with mechanism support in both domains). This is a genuine contribution. But notice that it's a contribution to the question of analogical *productivity*, not to the question of what analogy *is*. HPC tells us when an analogy will be deep enough to sustain a research programme or a conventional expression. It doesn't tell us what makes analogy possible in the first place. For that, you need a theory of how minds perceive structural similarity across different substrates -- which is precisely what the analogy-as-categorization tradition tries to provide.

Let me end where I began: with the dog. I love this example, and I think Reynolds is right to take it seriously. But I would frame it differently. The dog following painted lines isn't performing "cross-domain HPC extension." He is doing what every mind does, all the time: seeing the familiar in the unfamiliar, perceiving structure that transcends surface features, making the world intelligible by finding echoes of the known in the unknown. He is making an analogy. That this particular analogy crosses what Reynolds calls a "functional domain boundary" is interesting, but what's really interesting is that the dog makes analogies at all. That is the deep fact. That is what connects the dog on the soccer field to the poet at her desk to the physicist at her blackboard. They are all, in their different ways, doing the one thing that minds do: finding sameness in difference, seeing one thing as another.

Reynolds has written a paper that almost arrives at this conclusion. I hope the next version does.

## 11. Emily Dickinson

**A Response from Amherst**

Dear Mr Reynolds,

I have read your paper with the attention one gives a visitor who arrives unannounced but carrying interesting luggage. You have taken my Bird and put it on a table and counted its feathers. I am not sure whether to be grateful or alarmed. Both, I think, is the honest answer, and you strike me as a man who prefers honest answers, since you begin your paper with a dog on a soccer field and do not pretend it is anything grander.

You say my metaphor works because "both hope and birds are richly structured kinds, and the mechanisms are utterly different (psychology vs ornithology)." I will grant you that much. The mechanisms are utterly different. That was rather the point. But you say it as though discovering the distance between psychology and ornithology were your contribution, when it was mine. I put the distance there. I made it visible by refusing to explain it away. You have given the distance a name ("incommensurable mechanism suites"), which is a different enterprise entirely, and I am not persuaded it is a superior one.

You place my poem at the low end of your single dimension. Literary metaphors have "low mechanism support." They exploit "coincidental or superficial overlap." They "don't conventionalize and aren't meant to." I accept the last part. I was not trying to build a research programme. But "coincidental or superficial" stops me. The overlap between hope and a bird is not coincidental. I chose it. I spent years in a room choosing things. The persistence through storms, the unbidden arrival, the asking of nothing in return, the perching in the soul (which is a place with no branches), the singing without words (which is the only kind of singing that matters when what you are singing about has no content yet), the smallness of the thing relative to the vastness of the weather it endures: none of this is coincidental. It is precise. It is the precision of choosing exactly the right partial overlap and refusing to extend it one inch further than it can bear.

You know this, I think. You write that "the reader keeps finding fits (persistence, fragility, unbidden arrival) and misfits (migration, moulting, birdsong), and that tension is the aesthetic effect." That is better. That is closer. But you have it slightly backwards. The reader does not find fits and misfits. I arranged them. The tension is not discovered by the reader in the gap between two "richly structured kinds." The tension is engineered by the poet who knows exactly where the structure runs out and places the poem there, on that edge, the way your dog places himself on the painted line. Your dog, if I understand your argument, follows the line because it looks enough like a trail. I wrote the poem because hope looks enough like a bird, and not one feather more.

Here is where your framework and my practice diverge. You treat the overlap as a feature of the world ("both hope and birds are richly structured kinds"). I treat it as a feature of the attention. Hope is not a richly structured kind sitting in nature waiting for someone to notice its resemblance to a finch. Hope is what happens when a person in a room in Amherst decides not to go downstairs today and needs a way to say that the thing keeping her upright has no weight, no argument, no reason, and yet will not leave. The bird is not a source domain. The bird is an act of rescue. Your Boyd says metaphors succeed by "accommodation to causal structure." Perhaps. But my metaphor succeeds by accommodation to a structure that has no causes, only consequences. You cannot trace the mechanisms maintaining hope the way you can trace a dog's nose along five sequential footprints. Hope has no temporal decay gradient. It has no substrate. It has, as I said, feathers, and that is all it has, and that is the poem.

I am not hostile to your enterprise. You are doing something real. The dog is wonderful. I would have liked that dog. I was a person who noticed things like dogs following painted lines, and I would have noticed what you noticed, and probably sooner, because I was not also trying to build a theoretical framework and would therefore have been free to simply watch. You are right that following a painted line when the line shares only the geometry of a trail and none of the smell is a remarkable thing for a dog to do. You are right that it requires explanation. And your explanation (cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap) has the merit of being specific enough to be wrong, which is more than most explanations of metaphor can claim.

But I notice your anxiety. You write that your framework "can't explain why one particular pattern of genuine-but-unsustainable overlap is moving and another is merely odd." You say this requires "aesthetics, not philosophy of science," and that this is "the right boundary for the framework." I appreciate the boundary. Most theorists do not know where to stop. You do. But I want to press on the thing you've conceded. The difference between moving and merely odd is the whole of the question for me. It is the only question I spent fifty years answering, in that room, with those dashes. Your framework handles the full range from literary through everyday to constitutive. It does. I believe you. But it handles the literary case by saying, in effect, "the mechanisms don't sustain the mapping, and that's what makes it literary." This is like saying the reason the bird sings is that it has no words. It is true. It is even illuminating. But it replaces the experience of the singing with an account of the absence of words, and those are not the same thing.

You write that "hope doesn't migrate south in winter." No. It does not. But some years it comes close. Some years it goes quiet for so long you think it has left, and you sit in the silence it made, and then it starts again from somewhere you weren't looking. That is not low mechanism support. That is the mechanism itself, the one you cannot name because it is not ornithological and not psychological and not causal in any way your framework recognizes. It is the mechanism of being alive in a room with a window and a bird outside the window and not enough hope and exactly enough language.

Your Mr Cooperrider says he doesn't fully understand the poem himself. Good. I didn't write it to be fully understood. I wrote it to be fully felt, which is harder and which your framework correctly identifies as being outside its jurisdiction. I respect a framework that knows its jurisdiction.

The painted line is a trail with all the interesting parts politely removed. You wrote that sentence in your introduction and it is the best sentence in your paper. It is also, if you will permit me, a poem.

Yours, without feathers,

E. Dickinson
Amherst, 1886

## Major Strengths

**The Core Synthesis is Elegant and Potentially Powerful**

Your reframing of Conceptual Metaphor Theory through Homeostatic Property Cluster theory addresses a real gap in Lakoff and Johnson's account. The observation that CMT describes property clusters without explaining what maintains them is astute, and Boyd's mechanisms genuinely seem to supply what's missing. The resulting framework—metaphor as cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap—is conceptually clean and generates testable predictions, which is more than can be said for much work in this area.

**The Canine Case Study is Theoretically Bold**

Using dog cognition to motivate and illustrate the framework is genuinely innovative. Too much philosophy of language and cognitive science operates with an implicit human exceptionalism; your willingness to locate the basic operations of metaphor (or their precursors) in non-human animals pushes against this productively. The detailed review of canine trail cognition literature (Thesen et al., Hepper & Wells, Cafazzo et al., Bräuer & Belger) demonstrates serious engagement with empirical animal cognition research.

**The Predictions Are Concrete**

You offer three specific, falsifiable predictions that differentiate your account from competitors—particularly the claim that conventionalization requires mechanism support, not just frequency (contra Bowdle & Gentner). This is where theoretical work in this area should be heading.

## Significant Weaknesses

**The "Painted Line" Observation is Methodologically Problematic**

The central motivating observation—that your dog follows painted lines on soccer fields as if they were trails—is presented as hypothesis-generating, but the paper leans on it heavily for rhetorical effect. The caveats you eventually acknowledge (page 13: "naturalistic and unsystematic," potential confounds of residual paint odor, prior reinforcement, handler cues, edge-following vs. trail-following) substantially weaken its evidential value.

More critically, the observation sits uneasily with your own theoretical framework. You argue that trail is a functional spatial category maintained by specific ecological mechanisms (locomotion, scent deposition, substrate compaction). A painted line belongs to a different functional domain (spatial demarcation) with different maintaining mechanisms. If your dog is genuinely treating it as a trail, this supports your framework. But if the dog is simply following a high-contrast visual boundary—a well-documented phenomenon in animal navigation (thigmotaxis, geometric module processing)—then this may not be cross-domain extension at all, but rather within-domain processing of visual boundary information.

You acknowledge this alternative (page 13: "general edge-following or contrast-tracking behaviour"), but don't adequately address how to distinguish your interpretation from it. The diagnostic criteria you propose (page 14: attraction beyond baseline, visual-geometric rather than olfactory, on the line vs. alongside) are sensible, but the paper proceeds as if the phenomenon is established when it isn't.

**The Mechanism/Structure Distinction Needs Sharpening**

Your framework's central claim—that metaphorical inference is reliable when shared relational structure is maintained by mechanisms in the target domain—depends crucially on distinguishing "relational structure" from "mechanisms that maintain it." This distinction often blurs in your exposition.

For instance, in discussing argument is war (page 7), you note that both domains exhibit "competition for dominance, strategic reasoning, alliance formation"—these are described as shared relational properties, not shared mechanisms. But then you characterize what makes the metaphor productive as that "the shared relational structure (competition, strategy, alliance) is maintained in both domains, each by its own mechanism suite."

The problem: competition and strategy are themselves constituted by practices and institutions. Are they properties or mechanisms? If mechanisms are what maintain property clusters, but the properties themselves include relational structures like competition that require social institutions to sustain them, you risk either circularity or inflation of "mechanism" to cover anything causal.

**The Literary Metaphor Account is Underdeveloped**

Your treatment of literary metaphor (Section 6) acknowledges that HPC theory "can't explain is why one particular pattern of genuine-but-unsustainable overlap is moving and another is merely odd"—that this "requires aesthetics, not philosophy of science." This is a reasonable boundary to draw, but it leaves your framework with a lacuna at exactly the point where metaphor has been most extensively theorized.

You suggest literary metaphors work by mapping between "rich property clusters whose maintaining mechanisms are incommensurable," producing productive instability. But this seems to predict that any richly structured but mechanistically incommensurable mapping should be aesthetically effective, which is clearly false. The specificity of Dickinson's "Hope is the thing with feathers"—why feathers, why a bird, why these particular properties—disappears into a general claim about richness and incommensurability. Your framework may capture necessary conditions for successful literary metaphor, but hardly sufficient ones.

**The Comparison with LLMs is Underargued**

Prediction 3 (page 9) claims that large language models, lacking goal-directed projectibility constraints, should extend indiscriminately across domains based on structural overlap. You cite Imas et al. (2026) on LLMs producing outputs resembling class consciousness when assigned repetitive tasks. But this is a single, highly specific example, and the broader claim about LLM behavior is controversial.

Recent work on LLM cognition (e.g., Mitchell's own 2024 work on AI metaphors, which you cite) suggests these systems may be more selective than you imply, and the question of whether they implement anything like "projectibility constraints" is actively debated. Your claim that "their 'spurious connections' are typically genuine structural overlap that isn't projectible for any purpose" needs more defense. What looks like indiscriminate extension from one perspective might look like differently-weighted projectibility from another.

## Medium-Level Concerns

**The "Functional Domain" Criterion**

Your definition of functional domain (page 6)—"Two instances belong to the same functional domain when the same suite of homeostatic mechanisms maintains their property clusters"—is operationalized through the trail case but becomes murkier elsewhere.

Consider your own example of a forest trail versus a busy sidewalk (same domain) versus a painted line (different domain). The sidewalk is maintained by planning and construction mechanisms, not (primarily) by the locomotion mechanisms that maintain forest trails. You argue that "the trail-relevant properties are maintained by locomotion" even on sidewalks, but this seems strained—sidewalks persist and maintain their visual contrast through municipal maintenance, not foot traffic. The "desire path" example shows that locomotion *can* override planning, but doesn't show that sidewalks are maintained by locomotion.

The criterion risks becoming circular: domains are individuated by mechanisms, and we know which mechanisms operate by knowing which domain we're in.

**Reflexivity as Virtue and Vice**

Your acknowledgment that applying HPC to metaphor is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense (pages 5-6, 17-18) is intellectually honest and theoretically productive. However, there's a tension you don't fully resolve: if the framework is genuinely reflexive, then its own success conditions must apply to itself.

You note that it would fail if "no identifiable homeostatic mechanisms maintained the property clusters in either source or target domains." But what are the mechanisms maintaining the HPC structure of "metaphor" itself when extended to linguistic/cognitive phenomena? You gesture toward neural recruitment, social transmission, and cultural feedback loops, but these are extremely general compared to the specific mechanisms you require for trail (olfactory decay, substrate compaction, etc.). The worry is that "mechanism" becomes a promissory note rather than an identified causal process when applied at the level of abstract cognitive categories.

**Phylogenetic Claims Outpace Evidence**

Your argument that cross-domain HPC extension is available to "any animal with HPC-structured categories" (page 15) and that this operation may predate language is theoretically coherent but empirically thin. You establish that canine trail has HPC structure, but the leap to cross-domain extension as a basic operation available across species relies heavily on the painted-line case, which, as noted, is methodologically weak.

The comparative discussion (page 14-15) of wild canids confirms trail as a functional category, but doesn't demonstrate cross-domain extension. Birds mobbing owl decoys and corvids extending tool categories are mentioned as "suggestive," but you acknowledge the "diagnostic difficulty" in determining whether these are genuinely cross-domain. The paper would benefit from either more extensive comparative analysis or more modest claims about phylogenetic distribution.

## Procedural and Presentational Issues

**The Dog as Rhetorical Device**

The paper's structure—opening with your dog, returning to him periodically, closing with his likely indifference to the theoretical outcome—is engaging but potentially distracting. The personal anecdote establishes a tone of accessible authority, but in places it substitutes for argument. The closing line ("My dog will remain unconcerned either way") is charming but also emblematic of a certain lightness of touch that may undermine the paper's serious theoretical ambitions.

**Citation Asymmetries**

Your engagement with the metaphor literature is somewhat selective. While you discuss Gentner, Glucksberg, Murphy, and Bowdle & Gentner extensively, there's limited engagement with more recent developments in metaphor theory (e.g., work by Steen, Gibbs, or Kovecses on metaphor identification and discourse) or with the extensive empirical literature on metaphor processing beyond the Flusberg et al. citations. The claim that "the experimental metaphor literature... doesn't cite Boyd either" (page 2) is offered without systematic evidence.

**The "Mechanism Support" Proxies**

Your acknowledgment (page 9-10) that "mechanism support" must be assessed through "converging proxies"—cross-cultural stability, robustness of inferential transfer, processing fluency, perturbation response, diachronic trajectory—is methodologically prudent but theoretically concerning. If mechanism support is only ever estimated through these proxies, and the proxies themselves are "relative to the property cluster under analysis and the explanatory goals of the investigation," then the framework risks unfalsifiability in practice even if principled in theory. You need to say more about how these proxies could, in principle, come apart from mechanism support in ways that would falsify specific applications of the framework.

## Suggestions for Revision

1. **Restructure the painted line discussion.** Present it explicitly as an untested prediction derived from the framework, not as observational evidence. Consider whether the edge-following alternative can be ruled out on theoretical grounds, or whether the framework needs to be modified to accommodate both possibilities.

2. **Clarify the property/mechanism distinction.** Consider using more precise terminology—perhaps "manifest properties" versus "maintaining processes"—and be explicit about when relational structures (like competition or strategy) count as properties versus mechanisms.

3. **Develop the literary metaphor account or narrow its scope.** Either offer a more robust HPC-based account of aesthetic effectiveness, or restrict claims to necessary conditions and acknowledge more explicitly what falls outside the framework's explanatory scope.

4. **Strengthen or qualify the LLM comparison.** Engage more directly with recent work on LLM cognition and consider whether Prediction 3 can be operationalized in ways that could genuinely distinguish your account from alternatives.

5. **Expand the phylogenetic argument or temper its claims.** Either provide more extensive comparative evidence for cross-domain extension in non-humans, or frame the argument as a theoretical possibility awaiting empirical test rather than an established phylogenetic fact.

## Summary Assessment

This is an ambitious, original paper that makes a genuine contribution at the intersection of cognitive linguistics and philosophy of science. The HPC reframing of CMT is theoretically productive, and the canine case study, whatever its empirical limitations, opens productive terrain for interdisciplinary investigation. The paper's weaknesses—methodological reliance on a single anecdotal observation, some unclarity in core distinctions, underdevelopment of the literary and computational applications—are significant but addressable. The framework's testable predictions and its integration of stability, projectibility, and cross-domain extension represent genuine advances over existing accounts. With revision, particularly regarding the evidential basis of the central case study and the precision of key theoretical distinctions, this could be a highly influential contribution to metaphor theory and cognitive science more broadly.

### A Critique of "What Dogs Know About Trails: Metaphor, Mechanisms, and the Phylogeny of Cross-Domain Extension"

This paper represents an ambitious and intellectually rich attempt to synthesize two influential theoretical frameworks—Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory (CMT) and Boyd's Homeostatic Property Cluster (HPC) theory—that developed in parallel without mutual engagement. The central thesis, that HPC theory reframes CMT by supplying the stability mechanisms that Lakoff and Johnson's account lacks, is compelling and theoretically productive. The canine case study provides a genuinely innovative illustration of the framework. Below, I offer a critique organized around the paper's theoretical contribution, empirical grounding, argument structure, and several points where the analysis might be strengthened.

**Strengths of the Paper**

Before addressing areas for improvement, it is worth acknowledging what the paper does well. The theoretical synthesis is original and, as far as the literature review suggests, unprecedented. The paper correctly identifies that Lakoff and Johnson provide a powerful account of how metaphors arise (through embodied experience and experiential gestalts) but lack an explanation of why some metaphors persist and support reliable inference while others do not. Boyd's HPC framework supplies precisely this explanatory deficit. The paper thus achieves a genuine integration rather than a mere juxtaposition.

The case study involving canine trail cognition is imaginative and serves the dual function of illustrating the framework and generating testable predictions. The empirical literature cited on dog tracking (Thesen, Hepper, Cafazzo, Brauer) is substantive and demonstrates that the paper is grounded in real data, not just philosophical speculation.

The three predictions articulated in Section 5 are genuine theoretical advances. Prediction 2—that any animal with HPC-structured categories should show cross-domain recruitment—is particularly bold and, as the paper acknowledges, requires controlled empirical testing.

**Areas for Critique and Improvement**

1. The Characterization of CMT's Deficit

The paper argues that CMT lacks mechanisms and that this is a deficiency requiring remedy. This characterization is largely accurate, but it is worth noting that CMT was not originally conceived as a causal-mechanical theory. Lakoff and Johnson offered a phenomenological account of how metaphor structures everyday thought—a descriptive achievement rather than an explanatory one. The paper is right that this descriptive power lacks a causal story, but one might argue that requiring CMT to provide mechanisms is like criticizing a map for not being a weather report. The paper handles this well by noting that Lakoff's 2003 Afterword gestures toward neural recruitment, but it could engage more explicitly with the question of whether CMT's aims are fundamentally phenomenological rather than mechanistic.

2. The Dog Case Study: Empirical Gaps

The canine case study is the paper's most distinctive contribution, but it suffers from a significant empirical gap. The anecdotal observation about the author's dog following painted lines on a soccer field is presented as hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory evidence, which is appropriately modest. However, the paper cites extensive empirical work on dog trail-tracking (Thesen, Hepper, Cafazzo, Brauer) to establish that dogs possess an HPC-structured trail category. These studies, while impressive, all concern actual trails with scent, substrate, and functional connectivity—not painted lines. The leap from "dogs have a trail category" to "dogs might extend that category to painted lines" is logical but untested.

The paper acknowledges this limitation and frames it as a testable prediction. This is honest scholarship, but the critique here is that the paper could do more to specify what kind of evidence would count against the prediction. Section 5 outlines the three diagnostic questions (attraction, visual-geometric basis, locomotion vs. edge-following), but it would strengthen the paper to discuss what outcome would falsify the hypothesis. If dogs do not preferentially follow painted lines, does this refute the claim that trail is an HPC kind? Or does it only show that the particular extension does not occur? The framework should be clear about the boundary conditions.

3. The Functional Domain Distinction: Operationalization

The paper's criterion for cross-domain extension—that the target instance belongs to a different functional domain maintained by a different suite of mechanisms—is theoretically elegant but potentially difficult to operationalize. The paper gives the example of a painted line versus a forest trail: the painted line's visual contrast is maintained by paint application and rule enforcement, not by locomotion. This is persuasive as a philosophical analysis, but in practice, identifying the relevant mechanisms requires empirical investigation. The paper acknowledges this ("identifying those mechanisms requires empirical investigation"), which is appropriate, but it raises the question of whether the framework is sufficiently predictive to guide research.

Consider a potential counterexample: a gravel path in a park. Is this in the same functional domain as a forest trail (locomotion-maintained) or a sidewalk (partially planning-maintained)? The boundaries between domains may be fuzzier than the framework implies. The paper might address this by discussing how to adjudicate borderline cases.

4. The Three Predictions: Testability

The three predictions in Section 5 are interesting, but their testability varies. Prediction 2 (animal cross-domain recruitment) is the most straightforwardly testable—the painted line case is a clear candidate. Prediction 1 (conventionalization requires mechanism support) is more difficult because it requires identifying high-frequency metaphors that resist conventionalization and showing that their targets lack mechanism support. This is a comparative judgment that may be hard to make independently of the framework itself, creating a potential circularity.

Prediction 3 (LLMs extend indiscriminately) is provocative but could use more defense. The paper claims that LLMs "project every such overlap indiscriminately," but this might be too strong. LLMs are trained on human-generated text, which is already filtered through human goal-directed cognition. Their "default behaviour" may be an artefact of training data rather than a fundamental property of goal-free systems. The prediction is interesting but would benefit from more grounding in the specific properties of LLM architecture.

5. The Literal/Metaphorical Continuum

The paper argues that the boundary between literal and metaphorical is "sharp but epistemically inaccessible"—a deliberate prediction rather than a problem. This is philosophically sophisticated, but it might leave empirical researchers uncertain about how to apply the framework. If the boundary cannot be determined in practice, what guides investigation? The paper's answer seems to be: the framework generates predictions about gradient typicality (as in Table 1), and those predictions are testable. This is a reasonable response, but the paper could be more explicit about how the continuum translates into research design.

6. Literary Metaphor: Underexplored

Section 6 ("The full range: literary, everyday, constitutive") addresses literary metaphor but gives it less space than everyday and constitutive cases. The discussion of Dickinson's "Hope is the thing with feathers" is illuminating, but the paper acknowledges that the framework "can't explain what makes them beautiful"—which is fair but might leave literary scholars wanting more. The paper could engage more with the extensive literary-theoretical literature on metaphor (e.g., Ricoeur, Derrida, de Man) to show how the HPC framework handles specific literary phenomena beyond the single example of Dickinson.

7. The Reflexivity Point

The paper's observation that applying HPC to metaphor is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense is a genuinely clever move. However, this reflexivity could be developed further. If the paper's own argument is a TCM, it should be subject to the same success conditions the paper proposes: identifiable mechanisms, projectibility to new cases, and exclusion of non-cases. The paper briefly mentions these as "genuine failure conditions" but does not systematically apply them to its own proposal. This self-application would strengthen the paper's credibility.

8. Style and Presentation

The paper is well-written and engages the reader, but there are a few areas where clarity could be improved. The abstract is dense and assumes familiarity with both CMT and HPC theory—it might benefit from a slightly more accessible opening. Some sentences are long and could be broken for readability. The dog anecdote in the introduction is effective but might be shortened slightly to focus reader attention on the theoretical contribution.

**Minor Points**

- The paper cites "reynolds2025hpcbook" and "reynolds2025sorites" in ways that suggest these are forthcoming works by the author. This is acceptable but might be flagged for readers.
- The acknowledgement that the paper was "drafted with the assistance of Claude Code (Opus 4.6)" is refreshingly honest but might be removed for journal submission, depending on the venue's policies.
- Some citations (e.g., "cooperrider2026," "imas2026") appear to be forthcoming works. The paper should ensure these are available or clearly marked as personal communications if not yet published.

**Conclusion**

This is a strong, original, and theoretically ambitious paper. Its central synthesis—using HPC theory to supply the mechanisms that CMT lacks—is persuasive and opens new ground. The canine case study is creative and the predictions are genuinely testable. The main areas for improvement are empirical (the painted-line prediction needs testing), operational (the functional domain distinction needs more methodological detail), and argumentative (the three predictions could be more fully defended against potential counterexamples). On the whole, the paper makes a significant contribution to metaphor theory and deserves attention from scholars in cognitive science, philosophy of language, and comparative cognition. The reflexivity of applying one's own framework to oneself is a welcome addition that demonstrates intellectual honesty. With minor revisions, this paper could make a substantial impact in its field.

### Overall Assessment
The paper's core insight—that HPC theory can provide the mechanistic grounding that CMT lacks—is genuinely interesting and worth developing. The attempt to ground metaphor in property clusters maintained by causal mechanisms offers a promising way to make metaphor theory more empirically tractable. However, the paper currently overreaches in its claims, underdelivers on empirical grounding, and contains conceptual confusions that undermine its argument.

### Major Concerns

#### 1. The Central Analogy Between Biological Homeostasis and Conceptual Stability is Under-Theorized

The paper repeatedly invokes "homeostatic mechanisms" maintaining property clusters, but what these mechanisms *are* for conceptual domains remains vague. For biological species, homeostasis has clear referents: gene flow, stabilizing selection, developmental constraints. For ARGUMENT IS WAR, what are the mechanisms? "Discursive norms, social hierarchies, and reputational stakes" are named (p. 7), but these aren't mechanisms in anything like the biological sense—they're not causal in the same way, they don't form feedback loops, and they don't explain why the *same* property cluster persists across individuals and generations.

**The problem:** You're using "mechanism" metaphorically (ironically) to explain metaphor. This risks circularity. You need a much more explicit account of what counts as a mechanism in the conceptual/social domain. Kendler et al. (2011) is cited but not engaged with—what would a multi-level mechanism for ARGUMENT IS WAR actually look like? Without this, "mechanism support" becomes a black box that can explain any outcome post-hoc.

#### 2. The Dog Argument Doesn't Do the Work Required

The painted line observation is presented as both motivation and potential confirmation, but it's structurally problematic for your argument:

**First,** you acknowledge that no controlled study exists, that there are multiple confounds (residual odor, prior reinforcement, edge-following vs. trail-following), and that your observation is "naturalistic and unsystematic" (p. 13). This is honest but fatal to the rhetorical role the dog plays. The paper spends pages establishing that dogs have an HPC-structured TRAIL category, then essentially says "maybe they extend it to painted lines, who knows?" That's not a case study; it's a promissory note.

**Second,** even if dogs *do* follow painted lines, why is that metaphor rather than simply a broad literal category? You define functional domains by "which mechanism maintains the property cluster" (p. 6). But if a dog treats a painted line as trail-worthy, hasn't it just *assimilated* the painted line into the locomotion-maintained domain? The dog isn't representing "this is a spatial demarcation artefact that I'm treating as a trail"—it's just following a line. The "cross-domain" character exists in your analysis, not in the dog's cognition. This matters because you're using the dog to argue for phylogenetic continuity of the *operation*, but the operation you've defined (cross-domain extension) may not be what the dog is doing.

**Third,** the Bräuer and Belger (2018) violation-of-expectation studies show dogs represent trail-destination connections, but this doesn't support cross-domain extension. It supports rich within-domain representation. That's useful for establishing HPC structure but doesn't bridge to metaphor.

#### 3. The "Functional Domain" Criterion is Under-Specified

The paper defines functional domains by which mechanisms maintain property clusters. But mechanisms can be described at multiple levels of abstraction, and nothing prevents gerrymandering. Consider:

- A paved sidewalk and a forest trail: both maintained by locomotion (same domain)
- A painted line: maintained by paint application and rule enforcement (different domain)

But locomotion itself involves paint-application at one level of description (shoes deposit material, wear creates contrast). And rule enforcement involves locomotion (officials walk the lines). The criterion needs a principled way to individuate mechanisms at the *right* level of abstraction. Currently, the analyst can always find *some* difference in mechanism suite to declare domains distinct, making the literal/metaphorical boundary arbitrary rather than "determinate but not determinable" (p. 15).

#### 4. The LLM Prediction is Under-Supported and Possibly Wrong

You predict that LLMs "extend indiscriminately" because they lack goals, "projecting every such overlap" (p. 9). But:

- This assumes LLMs have no implicit goals from training (debatable—RLHF explicitly instills goals)
- It assumes structural overlap detection is what drives LLM metaphor production (unclear)
- It's testable but you provide no data, only an appeal to plausibility

More problematically, the prediction may be falsified by existing work. LLMs can be remarkably selective in metaphor interpretation and generation when appropriately prompted. The "when explicitly prompted with a goal" caveat (p. 9) essentially concedes that LLMs *can* be selective—which means the indiscriminate extension isn't a property of the architecture but of the prompting condition. That's a much weaker claim.

#### 5. The "Three Disputes Dissolved" Section Overclaims

The paper claims to dissolve the comparison-vs-categorization debate, the attribute-vs-relational mapping dispute, and the embodied-vs-abstract dispute. It doesn't. It offers *reframings* of each, but:

- The comparison/categorization account essentially recapitulates Bowdle and Gentner (2005) with HPC terminology added. The "prediction about which novel metaphors will conventionalize" (p. 8) isn't derived from HPC—it's just "metaphors whose relational structure is maintained by mechanisms conventionalize." That's circular without independent access to "mechanism support."

- The attribute/relational mapping "resolution" is just endorsing Gentner's systematicity principle and calling it homeostatic coherence. But Gentner already had an account of why relational structure matters; HPC doesn't add explanatory power here.

- The embodied/abstract "resolution" says embodied schemas are "one mechanism type among several" (p. 8). But this doesn't resolve the dispute—it just sidesteps it. The dispute was about whether abstract concepts are *constituted by* embodied schemas or independently represented. Saying "both matter" doesn't resolve the disagreement; it's a compromise position that neither Lakoff nor Murphy/Gentner would accept.

### Medium Concerns

#### 6. The Reflexivity Section is Clever But Risky

The acknowledgment that applying HPC to metaphor is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor (pp. 5-6, 17-18) is sophisticated and shows awareness of the paper's own commitments. But it also creates a vulnerability: if HPC-applied-to-metaphor is a TCM, then by your own criteria it must "meet the success conditions it proposes: identifiable mechanisms, projectibility to new cases, exclusion of non-cases" (p. 6). Does it? The "mechanisms" in the target domain (metaphor theory) are notably abstract compared to the biological mechanisms that inspired HPC. This may be fine—TCMs can be partially successful—but it's worth acknowledging that the reflexive application may reveal limitations in HPC itself when extended beyond natural kinds.

#### 7. The Literary Metaphor Treatment is Thin

The Dickinson example (p. 10) is used to show what HPC can't explain (aesthetic value) while claiming it can explain "what makes literary metaphors aesthetically productive" (mechanism incommensurability creating "productive instability"). But:

- Is "productive instability" really what makes Dickinson's metaphor good? Many great literary metaphors don't have this property (e.g., "Juliet is the sun" isn't productively unstable—it's a conventionalized love-as-light metaphor made fresh by context).

- The claim that literary metaphors "aren't doing inferential work" (p. 10) is false for many literary metaphors. Herbert's "Prayer is the church's banquet" is doing extensive inferential work; it's just not the kind of inference that supports empirical prediction.

- The paper essentially cedes literary metaphor to aesthetics, which is fine but limits the scope of the framework more than acknowledged.

#### 8. The Phylogenetic Claims Outrun the Evidence

The paper claims the basic operation is available to any animal with HPC-structured categories (p. 15), then mentions birds mobbing decoys and corvids using novel tool materials as "suggestive." But:

- Mobbing decoys isn't cross-domain extension unless "decoys" constitute a different functional domain from "predators." Are they? Both are threats (if the bird mistakes the decoy for a predator). That's within-domain categorization error, not metaphor.

- Corvid tool use involves extending *tool* categories to novel materials based on geometry. But again, are novel materials in a different functional domain? They're still being used as tools—the domain is "objects that can achieve foraging goals."

Without clearer criteria for domain individuation, these remain anecdotes, not evidence.

### Minor Concerns

#### 9. Terminology Issues

- "Projectible" is used throughout but never defined for a general audience. It's a technical term from philosophy of science (Goodman) that needs explication.

- "Catachresis" (p. 5) is used correctly but may be unfamiliar to cognitive science readers.

- The repeated phrase "property clustering" is sometimes used interchangeably with "HPC," but HPC adds mechanisms—the distinction matters and occasionally blurs.

#### 10. Structural Issues

- Section 7.1 (TRAIL as HPC) is very long and detailed relative to its argumentative role. The canid comparative data (pp. 14-15) is interesting but could be condensed; it establishes that TRAIL is a genuine category across canids, which is more than needed to show dogs have an HPC-structured TRAIL category.

- The transition from Section 7 to Section 8 is abrupt. After pages of dog data, Section 8 opens with "If metaphor is cross-domain HPC extension..." and moves to phylogenetic claims. A bridging paragraph acknowledging what the dog case has established (HPC structure) and what it hasn't (cross-domain extension) would help.

- Table 1 (p. 13) is useful but the "career path" entry says "Fully abstract metaphor (humans only)"—this asserts rather than argues what's distinctively human. Section 8 makes the case, but the table prejudges it.

#### 11. Citation Gaps

- Murphy (1996) is cited for the multiple-metaphors objection, but the paper doesn't engage with Murphy's positive proposal (structural similarity between independently represented concepts). This matters because Murphy's account is a competitor that also explains selectivity without HPC.

- The Bowdle and Gentner (2005) engagement is good but doesn't address their later work on metaphor and analogy, which might complicate the HPC framing.

### Summary of Recommendations

**Priority revisions:**

1. **Clarify what "mechanism" means** in conceptual/social domains. Give explicit criteria for identifying mechanisms and distinguishing them from mere correlations or descriptions. Engage with Kendler et al. (2011) on multi-level mechanisms.

2. **Reconsider the dog's role.** Either:
   - Acknowledge that the painted line observation is purely illustrative and drop the claim that it could "confirm" the framework, OR
   - Actually design the controlled study (even as a proposal) rather than gesturing at it

3. **Sharpen the domain-individuation criterion.** Provide constraints on what counts as a "different functional domain" to prevent gerrymandering. This is essential for the literal/metaphorical boundary to be principled.

4. **Tone down the "dissolves three disputes" claim.** Replace with "reframes" or "offers an alternative perspective on." The current language overclaims.

5. **Either drop the LLM prediction or support it** with evidence or a more rigorous argument. The current version is too speculative for a philosophy/cognitive science paper.

6. **Acknowledge the circularity risk** in the mechanism-support criterion more explicitly, and explain how mechanism support can be assessed independently of metaphor success.

**Secondary revisions:**

7. Condense the canid comparative data (pp. 14-15) to what's strictly necessary for establishing HPC structure.

8. Add a transition between the dog case study and the phylogenetic discussion.

9. Define "projectible" and consider glossing "catachresis."

10. Engage more substantively with Murphy's positive account as a competitor.
