# Research Notes: Thesen et al. (1993) - Behaviour of Dogs During Olfactory Tracking

## Full Citation

Thesen, A., Steen, J. B., & Døving, K. B. (1993). Behaviour of dogs during olfactory tracking. *Journal of Experimental Biology*, 180, 247–251.

**DOI:** Not visible in PDF

## Core Findings

### Three-Phase Tracking Behaviour

Dogs exhibit three distinct behavioural phases when tracking:

1. **Searching phase**: Dog tries to find the track
2. **Deciding phase**: Dog determines the direction of the track
3. **Tracking phase**: Dog follows the track in the determined direction

**100% success rate**: "In every test the dogs tracked in the correct direction (i.e. in the direction in which the track-maker had moved)" (p. 249).

### Deciding Phase Characteristics

The deciding phase is the most cognitively demanding:

- **Duration**: 3–5 seconds (mean: 4.7±1.1s on grass, 5.3±2.5s on concrete) (p. 250, Table 1)
- **Footprints examined**: 2–5 footprints (mean: 3.1±1.1 on grass, 3.0±0.8 on concrete) (p. 250, Table 1)
- **Movement speed**: Half the speed of other phases (stepping frequency 1.9±0.5 steps/s vs 3.1–3.2 steps/s) (p. 250, Table 1)
- **Sniffing duration**: Three times longer sniffing periods (35.6±15.6 sniffs per period on concrete vs 11.5–13.7 in other phases) (p. 247, Abstract; p. 250, Table 1)

> "The deciding phase lasted 3–5s, while the dogs sniffed at 2–5 footprints. The dogs' ability to determine track direction in this time must rely on accurate methods of sampling air and a remarkable sensitivity for certain substances." (p. 247, Abstract)

> "During the deciding phase the dogs moved at half the speed and their periods of sniffing lasted three times as long as during the other two phases." (p. 247, Abstract)

### Directional Determination Method

> "We assume that the dogs determine the track direction by perceiving differences in the concentration of certain substances deposited by the track-maker. This implies that, in some of our tests, the dogs must have determined a difference in the concentration of scent in the air above two consecutive prints which were made 1s apart, either 3min or 20min earlier." (p. 251)

The paper cites earlier work by Steen and Wilsson (1990): "Studies of German shepherd dogs indicate that these dogs compare the strength of the scent of consecutive footprints and then follow the track in the direction of increasing concentration of scent" (p. 247).

### Olfactory Sensitivity

> "Although the sensitivity of individual olfactory receptors is similar in humans and dogs (Moulton et al. 1960), dogs have many more receptors than do humans (Adrian, 1956). Thus, a dog's detection threshold for acetic acid, which is one constituent of human skin secretions, may be 10^8 times lower than a human's threshold (Neuhaus, 1953)." (p. 251)

## Methodology

### Subjects
- **Species**: Domestic dog (*Canis familiaris*)
- **Breed**: German shepherd
- **Number**: Four male dogs
- **Training**: All trained to follow human tracks in the direction the track-maker had moved
- **Handlers**: Each dog handled by its own trainer

### Experimental Design

**Location**: Airfield near Oslo, Norway

**Testing period**: May–October 1990, between 12:00 and 15:00h

**Conditions**: Dry ground, dry calm days, fair temperatures (15–20°C)

**Grid setup**:
- 10×4 squares painted on ground
- Each square 2m × 2m
- Track-maker took two steps per square at ~1 step/second
- Footprint positions video-recorded for exact localization

**Track conditions**:
- **Grass tracks**: 20 minutes old (10 tests)
- **Concrete tracks**: 3 minutes old (10 tests)
- Age difference chosen based on handlers' experience that dogs could determine direction of tracks at these ages

**Trial procedure**:
1. Dog started at heel ~5m away from track, perpendicular to middle of track
2. Neither dog nor handler present during track-laying
3. No contact with track-maker until after test
4. Five tracks laid in each direction (left-to-right or right-to-left, random order)
5. Reward (wooden stick) left at end of track by track-maker
6. Reward not visible during trial

**Recording equipment**:
- Sony WP 27 microphone fastened above dog's nose
- Radio transmitter (military equipment) on collar
- VHS video camera with time marker (40ms resolution)
- 4–8 familiarization trials before testing

### Measurements

Quantitative variables measured:
- Number of sniffs per second
- Number of sniffs between each respiratory ventilation (breath)
- Number of forepaw steps per second (stepping frequency)
- Number of footprints sniffed at
- Time elapsed between finding track and deciding direction

**Note**: "The number of sniffs per breath could not be determined for trials on grass because of noise caused by the dog's nose touching the grass." (p. 250, Table 1 note)

### Sniffing Consistency

> "The frequency of sniffs was remarkably constant: about 6 per second in all phases. Judging from the sound tracks, the intensity of individual sniffs was uniform." (p. 250)

> "Regardless of phase, the time taken for respiratory inspiration and expiration was always less than 10% of the sniffing period." (p. 250)

### Nose Position During Deciding Phase

> "During this phase the dog's nose was held close to the ground, about 1cm from the concrete, but this was more difficult to judge in trials on grass." (p. 250)

### Error Correction

> "However, they did not always turn in the correct direction when they first found the track. If the initial direction was wrong, the dogs turned abruptly and walked in the opposite direction." (p. 249)

**Prior-trial effect**: "The initial direction chosen by dogs that had already been tested before on the same day was generally (8/9) the same as the correct direction in the previous test, i.e. the direction that had yielded the reward for correct tracking." (p. 249)

## Relevance to TRAIL Argument

### Multi-Property Tracking

This study provides strong evidence that dogs track using **temporal gradients of scent concentration**, not just presence/absence of scent. The ability to determine direction from 2–5 footprints made 1 second apart (sampled 3–20 minutes later) demonstrates:

1. **Temporal representation**: Dogs compare scent concentrations across consecutive footprints to infer temporal sequence
2. **Spatial representation**: Dogs navigate spatially along the track, slowing at choice points
3. **Directional representation**: Dogs extract directional information from scalar (concentration) gradients

The three-phase behaviour pattern suggests dogs have a **structured representation of what a track is**:
- Searching phase: Looking for track presence
- Deciding phase: Determining track direction (inference from gradient)
- Tracking phase: Following established direction

### Evidence for Clustered Properties

The deciding phase reveals dogs integrating multiple cues:
- **Olfactory**: Scent concentration gradients
- **Spatial**: Geometric arrangement of footprints
- **Temporal**: Age/freshness of scent deposits
- **Directional**: Vector information inferred from gradient

> "We suggest that during the deciding phase the dog behaves in a way that gives constant detection variables, as far as possible. These variables include the distance between the source of the scent and the nose, the volume of each sniff and the sniffing frequency." (p. 251)

This controlled sampling behaviour suggests dogs are **actively structuring their perceptual input** to extract track properties, not passively following scent.

### Projectibility Evidence

The 100% success rate across both grass and concrete, and across 3-minute and 20-minute-old tracks, suggests dogs are projecting a general TRAIL category across diverse surface types and temporal scales. The maintenance of constant sniff frequency (6 Hz) across all phases suggests a **stable perceptual strategy** applied to the category.

### HPC Mechanistic Stability

The study demonstrates **robust homeostatic mechanisms** maintaining track-following behaviour:
- Consistent sniff frequency across phases and conditions
- Stereotyped three-phase sequence
- Rapid error correction when initial direction is wrong
- Transfer of directional bias from previous trials (8/9 cases)

## Limitations and Caveats

1. **Small sample size**: Four dogs, 10 tests per condition
   - Some tests excluded due to poor recording quality ("so the number of tests referred to below is not always 10", p. 248)

2. **Pre-selected competent dogs**: "The experiments were not designed to test whether these dogs were able to detect the direction of the track. Rather, we wanted to study their behaviour when they were presented with tracks whose direction we knew (from earlier experience with these dogs) that they were able to detect." (p. 248)
   - Study describes expert performance, not acquisition or variability across skill levels

3. **Limited track ages**: Only two track ages tested (3 min concrete, 20 min grass)
   - Ages chosen based on handlers' experience, not systematic variation

4. **Single track-maker**: No information on whether dogs generalize across different human track-makers

5. **Controlled conditions**: Dry, calm days at moderate temperatures
   - Performance under wind, rain, or temperature extremes not tested

6. **Grass measurement limitations**: Could not measure sniffs per breath on grass due to noise from nose touching grass (p. 250)

7. **Mechanism assumed, not demonstrated**: Study assumes concentration gradient detection but doesn't rule out alternative cues (e.g., visual, thermal)

8. **No cross-surface generalization tests**: Dogs tested on grass and concrete separately, not tested on switching between surfaces mid-track

## Key Quotes

> "A good tracking dog is not only able to find a track, but it can also follow it in the correct direction, i.e. in the direction the maker of the track was moving." (p. 247)

> "This study demonstrates that tracking by dogs is divided into three phases and that different tracking strategies are used in each phase. This is evidently common knowledge among dog handlers, but has not been substantiated before by direct recordings." (p. 250)

> "Once the direction has been found they move faster, suggesting that following the track (the third phase) is a simpler task than determining its direction. Thus, the deciding phase seems to be the most difficult one; it is certainly the most impressive one from a human point of view." (p. 250–251)

> "To a human, this feat may appear unrealistic." (p. 251) [referring to discriminating scent concentrations from footprints made 1 second apart, sampled 3–20 minutes later]

> "We suggest that during the deciding phase the dog behaves in a way that gives constant detection variables, as far as possible." (p. 251)
