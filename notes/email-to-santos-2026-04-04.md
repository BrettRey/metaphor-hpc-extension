Subject: Dog defecation-site choice relative to paths -- a question from outside the field

Dear Professor Santos,

I'm a linguist working on category theory, and one of my projects on metaphor touches on how animals categorize spatial features. This has generated a question I can't answer myself: do dogs choose defecation sites relative to path edges and junctions in systematic ways, and do those choices change when off-path access is constrained?

The reasoning is simple. There's good evidence that dogs treat paths as structured space (edges, junctions, zones), not just as surfaces to walk on. If that structure is behaviourally real, it should show up in site-selection decisions, including elimination. A bounded-path design (varying how easily a dog can step off the path) would separate preference from constraint.

I recently wrote to Erica Feuerbacher with this question, and she suggested your lab and Alexandra Horowitz's as better fits -- "more cognitive labs" was her phrase. That makes sense to me: the question is really about how dogs represent spatial affordances under competing motivations, not just about marking behaviour. I thought the Canine Cognition Center might have the setup or the student interest to piggyback something like this on walks you're already doing.

I'm not an animal behaviour researcher, and I don't have the facilities or expertise to run this study. I'm writing because the question seems worth testing. If the idea is interesting to you or to one of your students, I'd be happy to share the theoretical background and a short protocol sketch. If it's not, that's perfectly understandable, and I'd welcome a suggestion of someone better placed.
