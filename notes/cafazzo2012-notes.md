### Definition of Territory

> "Territorial boundaries were assumed to be the same for all members of the pack because it has been observed that all pack members cooperated for territorial and resources defence against intruders from other neighbouring social packs (Bonanni et al. 2010a). Group territory size measured 22.14 ha and its boundaries were inside the home range. We defined the periphery of the territory as a zone within 50 m of the territorial boundaries. This periphery zone contained about 17% of the total area of territory. All dens, resting and feeding sites were located inside the territory and none in its periphery." (p. 959)

## Methodology

**Species:** Free-ranging domestic dogs (*Canis familiaris*)

**Sample size:** 27 dogs (final statistical analysis)
- 16 adult males
- 5 adult females
- 4 subadult males
- 1 subadult female
- 6 juvenile males
- 5 juvenile females

> "At the start of the research, the dog pack consisted of 27 individuals. Owing to some deaths, births and disappearances of some individuals during the period of observation (see, Cafazzo et al. 2010 for a detailed description), the pack size ranged from 25 to 42 individuals. The final statistical analysis was applied to 27 dogs: six adult males, five adult females, four subadult males, one subadult female, six juvenile males and five juvenile females." (p. 957)

**Observation method:** Direct observation using focal animal sampling (Altmann 1974)

**Duration:** April 2005 to end of May 2006 (approximately 14 months)

> "The study began in Apr. 2005 and lasted until the end of May 2006." (p. 957)

**Total observation time:** 282.53 hours
> "We recorded a total of 782 urinations, 143 ground scratches and 89 defecations during 282.53 h of observations" (p. 959)

**Location:** Suburban environment, south-western outskirts of Rome, Italy (41°50'N, 12°23'W; elevation: about 60 m)

> "The research was carried out in a suburban environment situated in the south-western outskirts of Rome, Italy (41°50'N, 12°23'W; elevation: about 60 m). The study area was about 300 ha and was delimited to the north, west and south by roads with heavy traffic and to the east by cultivated areas." (p. 957)

**Study area characteristics:**
- Total area: ~300 ha
- Territory size: 22.14 ha
- Periphery zone (within 50 m of boundaries): ~17% of territory

> "The south-west sector was urbanized, although not densely populated. The north-east sector was mainly occupied by a nature reserve called 'Tenuta dei Massimi'. The habitat in the reserve consists mainly of open grasslands with interspersed wooded areas" (p. 957)

## Relevance to TRAIL Argument

### Evidence for Trail Discrimination

**1. Spatial concentration at boundaries and crossroads**

The data show that dogs systematically concentrate scent-marking at territorial boundaries and especially at crossroads along those boundaries. This suggests functional categorization of space based on strategic pathways:

> "Also males scent-marked via raised-leg urination mainly at the crossroads along territorial borders (t = 5.00, z = 2.98, n = 16, p = 0.003)." (p. 960)

The crossroads pattern is particularly relevant: crossroads are by definition locations where trails intersect. The preferential marking at these sites suggests dogs recognize these as functionally significant locations within their territory's pathway network.

**2. Boundary trails as movement corridors**

The territorial boundaries themselves appear to function as trails. The paper notes dogs move along these boundaries:

> "We defined the periphery of the territory as a zone within 50 m of the territorial boundaries. This periphery zone contained about 17% of the total area of territory." (p. 959)

The fact that boundaries are narrow linear features (rather than broad zones) and are repeatedly traversed for marking suggests they function as established pathways.

**3. Functional differentiation of denning sites and feeding sites**

Females differentiated between denning sites and feeding sites in their marking behavior:

> "Females scent-marked more frequently at denning sites than at feeding sites and somewhere else (Table 5)" (p. 960)

This functional categorization of locations suggests dogs have structured spatial categories. If denning sites and feeding sites are connected by regular travel routes, those routes would constitute trails in the functional sense.

**4. Strategic vs. non-strategic locations**

The contrast between boundary marking (strategic) and interior marking (less frequent) suggests dogs discriminate between locations based on their functional significance in territorial defense and communication:

> "dogs would mark more frequently along the periphery of the territory than in its interior (prediction 1a); moreover, dogs are expected to mark more frequently at strategic places, i.e. crossroads, where potential intruders are most likely to encounter marks (prediction 1b)." (p. 956)

Both predictions were confirmed, indicating dogs recognize certain spatial features as functionally significant.

### Evidence Against Alternative Interpretations

**Not random spatial distribution:**
The highly non-random spatial distribution of marking (concentrated at boundaries and crossroads rather than distributed throughout the territory) rules out the possibility that dogs are simply marking their entire territory uniformly.

**Not substrate-driven alone:**
While the paper notes some marking occurs on "conspicuous or high substrates (rocks and plants)" (p. 960), the boundary/crossroad concentration cannot be explained purely by substrate availability, as similar substrates would be available throughout the territory.

**Context-dependent behavior:**
The variation in marking rate by social context (agonistic vs. non-agonistic interactions) shows the behavior is functionally organized, not reflexive:

> "Males scent-marked via urination more frequently during agonistic than non-agonistic interactions with both other pack members and dogs from other packs" (p. 961)

## Limitations and Caveats

**1. Single pack studied:**
> "In the current study, we explored the functional significance of different scent marking patterns performed by a pack of free-ranging domestic dogs living in a suburban environment." (p. 956)

Only one pack was observed, limiting generalizability.

**2. Cannot definitively distinguish territorial defense from scent-matching:**

> "whether scents alone (i.e. the scent-fence hypothesis) or the matching between scents and signallers (territory owners; i.e. the scent-matching hypothesis) would keep out intruders needs to be explored." (p. 962)

The authors acknowledge they cannot determine whether the boundary marking functions primarily as a "fence" (odor barrier) or requires matching the scent to known individuals.

**3. Neighbouring pack scent distribution not studied:**

> "Moreover, as we did not study the scent-marking distribution pattern of neighbouring dog packs, we cannot exclude that the concentration of scent marking along territorial boundaries could also be caused by dogs countermarking unfamiliar conspecifics' urine" (p. 962)

Without data on where other packs mark, cannot fully separate boundary demarcation from countermarking behavior.

**4. Small sample of females in some analyses:**

> "Females included in a different representation of age categories than males because only one subadult female was present, and we could not perform a skewed analysis. As this dog pack the dominance rank, the rate of aggressive behaviour and the age of the individuals were correlated (see Cafazzo et al. 2010), we applied a principal components analysis (PCA) to replace the mentioned three original variables" (p. 958)

Statistical analyses for females were limited by smaller sample sizes and age distribution.

**5. Observational constraints:**

> "Data were collected in three different contexts: in the presence of food, when receptive females were courted by males (and food was not present), and in the absence of external sources of competition (i.e. when food was not present and outside the oestrus period). A total of 82,925 s of recording data collection was carried out" (p. 957)

Data collection was restricted to certain contexts, potentially missing marking behavior in other situations.

**6. Cannot exclude all alternative functions:**

> "Finally, our observations suggest that defecation does not play an essential role in olfactory communication among free-ranging dogs and that standing and squat postures are associated with normal excretion. Our results suggest that many of the proposed functions of marking behaviours are not mutually exclusive, and all should be explored through detailed field and laboratory studies." (p. 955, Abstract)

The authors explicitly note that multiple functions may operate simultaneously, and their observational data cannot fully tease these apart.

**7. Definition of "crossroads" not quantified:**

The paper repeatedly refers to crossroads as strategic marking locations but does not provide a formal definition of what counts as a crossroad or how many such locations existed in the territory.

**8. Temporal patterns not fully analyzed:**

While the study spanned 14 months, there is limited analysis of seasonal or temporal variation in marking patterns (though oestrus effects are noted).

## Additional Notes

**On the "property line" function:**
The paper's interpretation that markings form a "property line" (p. 955) aligns with the TRAIL hypothesis if we understand territorial boundaries as linear features that are regularly traversed. The concentration of marking along these boundaries suggests they function as both pathways and borders.

**Comparison to other canids:**
The paper cites extensive literature on scent-marking in wolves, coyotes, and other canids, consistently showing concentration of marking at territorial boundaries and trails. This cross-species pattern suggests the behavior reflects stable functional pressures related to territorial defense and spatial organization.

**Free-ranging vs. owned dogs:**
These are free-ranging dogs with access to a large territory and natural social organization, making them more comparable to wild canids than pet dogs. This strengthens the ecological validity of the findings for understanding canid spatial cognition.
