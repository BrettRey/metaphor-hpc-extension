# Chan, Baumann, Bellgrove & Mattingley 2012

"From objects to landmarks: the function of visual location information in spatial navigation." *Frontiers in Psychology* 3, Article 304. doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2012.00304

---

## Summary

A review article proposing a four-part functional taxonomy of how visual objects serve as landmarks in spatial navigation: (1) beacons, (2) orientation/directional cues, (3) associative cues, and (4) reference frames. Each category is defined by behavioural function and associated with distinct neural substrates. The taxonomy is explicitly function-based, not form-based: "any environmental object can serve as a landmark" (p. 8); what matters is the navigational role the object plays in context.

---

## The four-part taxonomy (Table 1, p. 8)

| Landmark type | Neural correlate | Functional description |
|---|---|---|
| **Beacon** | Striatum | Single object that marks the exact or nearby location of a goal |
| **Orientation cue** | Retrosplenial cortex, anterodorsal thalamic nucleus, pre- and post-subiculum | Visual cue that provides information about one's current heading direction |
| **Associative cue** | Striatum and parahippocampal gyrus | Single object that is associated with navigationally relevant information |
| **Reference frame** | Hippocampus | Environmental geometry (extended surfaces, boundaries, and the intrinsic geometry of discrete object locations) that provides a framework for spatial encoding and localization |

---

## Key findings, organised by relevance to the dog TRAIL argument

### 1. Extended surfaces and boundaries as landmarks (pp. 6-7)

This is the most directly relevant section. Chan et al. argue that "the geometry of an extended surface or boundary can also provide important information for navigation" (p. 6). Critically, this extends the landmark concept beyond discrete objects:

> "In the natural environment, this can refer to the contours of a mountain range or the shoreline, whereas in man-made environments it might refer to the structure of a room or the sides of a large building." (p. 6)

The geometry of extended surfaces and boundaries "can function as a unique landmark by providing a reference frame or schema for the encoding of spatial information" (p. 6).

**Relevance to painted-line case:** A painted line on a soccer field is an extended linear boundary. In Chan et al.'s taxonomy, it could function as a reference frame (providing spatial structure) or, for a dog, as something that triggers TRAIL recruitment because it shares the geometric signature of a trail. The taxonomy explicitly acknowledges that boundaries and extended surfaces are navigational landmarks with their own neural substrate (hippocampus), separate from discrete objects.

### 2. Boundary-specific processing in the hippocampus (p. 7)

**Boundary vector cells:** "This initial discovery led to a model of spatial processing that postulates that place cell firing in the hippocampus reflects the outcome of accumulated activity of multiple cells, termed 'boundary vector cells,' which are tuned to the relative distance and direction of environmental boundaries" (p. 7, citing Burgess et al. 2000; Barry et al. 2006).

Cells with the predicted response characteristics have been found in the subiculum and medial entorhinal cortex (Solstad et al. 2008; Lever et al. 2009).

**Shape of boundaries modulates neural firing:** "It has also been shown that the shape of environmental boundaries modulates the neuronal firing patterns in these brain regions" (p. 7, citing Lever et al. 2009).

**Hippocampal encoding is automatic:** "In a recent fMRI study, having participants simply imagining empty visual scenes with a discrete, 'wall-like' object (i.e., an elongated block) resulted in an increase in hippocampal activity compared with imagining a scene without the object" (p. 7, citing Bird et al. 2010).

**Relevance:** A painted line is a visual boundary (high-contrast linear demarcation). Boundary information appears to be processed automatically by hippocampal circuits. If dogs have analogous boundary-sensitive cells (and rodent evidence strongly suggests they do), a painted line would activate boundary/reference-frame processing even without olfactory or substrate cues. This speaks to Horowitz's challenge: the painted line isn't just "visually salient" in a generic sense. It activates boundary-specific spatial processing.

### 3. Dedicated geometric module for boundary processing (p. 7)

**Cheng's geometric module:** Cheng (1986) showed rats in a rectangular arena used geometric properties of the boundary (not featural cues) to search for hidden food, making systematic "rotational errors" (searching at the geometrically equivalent but wrong corner). "Most interestingly, the rats continued to make rotational errors even when unique featural cues, which disambiguated the four corners, were added to the arena" (p. 7).

This has been replicated across ants, fishes, birds, primates, and humans. Young children (Hermer & Spelke 1994) show the same pattern.

The finding led to "the proposal that there may be a dedicated system -- a 'geometric module' -- in both humans and non-human animals, that automatically encodes geometric boundary information for orientation and navigation" (p. 7, citing Cheng 1986; Wang & Spelke 2002; Lee & Spelke 2010).

**Two dissociable systems (Doeller & Burgess 2008; Doeller et al. 2008):** "Learning trials for boundary-related target objects were associated with increased hippocampal activation, whereas trials concerning discrete proximal objects were associated with increased striatal activity" (p. 7). "Furthermore, in a purely behavioral version of the task, participants learned to use the navigational information provided by the boundary incidentally, even in task manipulations in which encoding of boundary information was neither encouraged nor required" (p. 7).

**Relevance to functional category vs. perceptual generalisation (Reviewer 1/2):** The incidental, automatic nature of boundary encoding suggests it operates below the level of deliberate generalisation. Animals don't "decide" to encode boundaries; the geometric module does so automatically. A painted line, as a linear boundary, would trigger this system. This supports the claim that TRAIL is a functional category grounded in spatial processing architecture, not a post-hoc observer's description.

### 4. Landmark salience depends on function and context, not just perceptual features (pp. 2-3)

**Distinctiveness:** "The more unique an object is within an environment, and the more informative its features are, the more likely it is that it will be used as a landmark" (p. 2, citing Stankiewicz & Kalia 2007).

**Stability:** "In order for objects to provide reliable navigational information, they need to be perceived as having a stable spatial position" (p. 2). In rodents, an object's influence on place cell activity is lost when the animal observes the object has moved (Jeffery 1998; Jeffery & O'Keefe 1999).

**Intrinsic stability and parahippocampal gyrus:** "Objects that were rated by participants as larger and less 'portable' elicited an increase in activity within the parahippocampal gyrus" (p. 2, citing Mullally & Maguire 2011). "It may be that more stable objects automatically evoke landmark-based neural processes" (p. 2).

**Decision-point location matters more than perceptual salience:** "It was spatial positioning that determined whether objects were included in direction giving and map drawing" (p. 3, citing Miller & Carlson 2011). When perceptual salience and spatial position conflict, "objects with stronger perceptual features were later recognized faster irrespective of their spatial positioning. However, it was spatial positioning that determined whether objects were included in direction giving and map drawing" (p. 3).

**Relevance to Godfrey-Smith (observer-relativity):** Chan et al. explicitly argue that landmark function is context-dependent but NOT observer-relative in an arbitrary sense: "The particular navigational function of an object or cue will depend upon the environmental context and the goals and preferences of the individual" (p. 8, citing Steck & Mallot 2000). The same object can serve as a beacon in one context and an orientation cue in another (the church spire example, p. 4). Function is real and measurable (via behaviour and neural correlates) even though it's context-sensitive. This is the HPC point: functional domains are individuated by the mechanisms that maintain them (here: distinct neural circuits for each landmark type).

### 5. Beacons as the most primitive landmark type (p. 4)

"Beacon-based navigation appears to be one of the most basic forms of landmark-based navigation. It develops before other spatial strategies, and may be used in preference to other strategies due to its computational simplicity" (p. 4, citing Alyan & Jander 1994; Alyan 1996; Hardt & Nadel 2009).

Neural substrate: dorsal striatum (Packard & McGaugh 1992; McDonald & White 1994). Metabolic activity associated with beacon-based navigation is increased in the caudate nucleus; increased hippocampal activity found for allocentric strategies (Miranda et al. 2006).

**Relevance to Keil (explanatory understanding):** Beacon navigation is the simplest form: it "involves monitoring the location of the self with respect to a single cue, irrespective of other environmental information" (p. 4). No explanatory understanding is required. If a painted line functions as a beacon-like landmark (marking a linear path to follow), the dog needs no understanding of why it's there. The perceptual overlap with TRAIL properties suffices to recruit trail-following behaviour. HPC categories don't require that the categoriser understand the mechanisms; they require that the mechanisms exist and that the organism's perceptual system is tuned to the relevant properties.

### 6. Associative cues: learned action-object associations (pp. 5-6)

Objects at decision points acquire associative information linking them to specific directional responses (e.g., "turn right at the post office"). "Participants' performance was as good as in the trained condition if the new combination of buildings at an intersection was associated with the same directional movement during training. Conversely, performance was poorer if the new combination of buildings at an intersection contained buildings associated with a direction that conflicted" (p. 6, citing Mallot & Gillner 2000).

Neural substrate: dorsal striatum + parahippocampal gyrus.

**Relevance:** Dogs may form associative cue relations with trails: the trail itself becomes associated with "keep moving forward" or "follow this." The painted line could trigger this associative response even without the full trail property cluster.

### 7. Neural dissociations: four systems, not one (pp. 4-8)

The taxonomy is grounded in neural dissociations:

- **Beacons** --> striatum (p. 4, p. 8)
- **Orientation cues** --> retrosplenial cortex, anterodorsal thalamic nucleus, lateral mammillary nuclei, pre- and post-subiculum (p. 5, p. 8)
- **Associative cues** --> striatum + parahippocampal gyrus (p. 6, p. 8)
- **Reference frames** --> hippocampus (p. 7, p. 8)

**Head-direction cells:** "Specialized neurons that code specifically for an animal's orientation" found "throughout the limbic system of the rodent brain including the anterodorsal thalamic nucleus, lateral mammillary nuclei, pre- and post-subiculum, and the retrosplenial cortex" (p. 5, citing Yoder et al. 2011). "Head-direction cells are strongly influenced by discrete visual cues, whereas geometric cues only provide a weak influence for the computation of heading vectors" (p. 5, citing Knight et al. 2011).

**Topographical disorientation subtypes:** Clinical evidence confirms functional dissociations. "Landmark agnosia" (parahippocampal damage) = can't recognize salient landmarks (Takahashi & Kawamura 2002). "Heading disorientation" (retrosplenial/posterior-medial lesions) = can recognise landmarks but can't extract directional information from them (Barrash 1998; Aguirre & D'Esposito 1999).

## Addressing the four reviewer challenges

### Challenge 1 (Multiple reviewers): Functional category vs. perceptual generalisation

Chan et al. provide strong support for distinguishing functional categories from mere perceptual generalisation:

1. **Neural dissociations.** Four distinct neural systems process four different landmark functions. This is not a single perceptual generalisation system; it's functionally organised. If a painted line activates boundary-specific hippocampal processing (as the evidence on geometric modules predicts), that's not "mere" perceptual generalisation but engagement of a dedicated spatial-functional system.

2. **Automatic, incidental encoding.** Boundary information is encoded "incidentally, even in task manipulations in which encoding of boundary information was neither encouraged nor required" (p. 7). This is architecture, not decision-making.

3. **The same object can serve different functions.** The church spire is a beacon when the church is the goal, an orientation cue when used for heading direction, an associative cue when linked to "turn right here" (p. 4). Function is not read off percept; it's determined by the navigational context. A functional category assigns different roles to the same percept depending on task demands. Perceptual generalisation can't explain this.

4. **Decision-point location trumps perceptual salience** for navigational function (p. 3). If perceptual generalisation were the whole story, more salient objects would always be better landmarks; but it's position in the navigational structure that determines landmark status.

### Challenge 2 (Godfrey-Smith): Observer-relative functional domains?

Chan et al.'s taxonomy is function-based but NOT observer-relative in a problematic sense:

1. The functions are **behaviourally measurable** (reaction times, navigation accuracy, error patterns) and **neurally instantiated** (distinct circuits for each type). You can determine which function an object serves by measuring the animal's behaviour and brain activity, not by asking the researcher's opinion.

2. Functions are **context-dependent but constrained.** "The particular navigational function of an object or cue will depend upon the environmental context and the goals and preferences of the individual" (p. 8). This is real context-sensitivity, not observer projection. The constraints come from the animal's navigational system (what information is available and what neural circuits are engaged), not from the experimenter's theoretical framework.

3. The "geometric module" operates automatically across species (ants, fish, birds, rodents, primates, humans). The boundary-processing function isn't something researchers impose; it's something animals do, measurably and consistently.

### Challenge 3 (Horowitz): Simpler explanations for painted-line following

Chan et al. actually make the "simpler explanation" challenge more precise while also providing tools to rebut it:

**Edge-following (thigmotaxis):** Chan et al.'s taxonomy distinguishes boundary-as-reference-frame from edge-following. Edge-following means walking *alongside* a boundary; trail-following means walking *on/along* a linear feature. If the dog walks on the painted line (not beside it), that's trail-following, not edge-following.

**Visual salience / contrast-tracking:** Chan et al. show that visual salience alone doesn't determine landmark function. Perceptually salient objects attract attention but don't automatically become navigational landmarks unless they occupy the right position in the navigational structure (p. 3). A painted line is salient, but mere salience would predict looking at it, not following it. Following is a navigational behaviour, implicating the spatial processing systems Chan et al. describe.

**Boundary processing as a dedicated system:** If a painted line triggers the hippocampal boundary-processing system (as the geometric module literature predicts), then "contrast-tracking" undersells what's happening. The organism is engaging dedicated spatial-navigational architecture, not just tracking a visual edge.

**What would disambiguate:** If the dog follows a painted line but NOT a high-contrast non-linear mark (e.g., a painted circle or scattered paint splotches of equal contrast), that eliminates contrast-tracking and implicates the linear-extent property specifically. This is a testable prediction.

### Challenge 4 (Keil): Explanatory understanding vs. perceptual overlap

Chan et al.'s evidence strongly supports perceptual overlap as sufficient:

1. **Beacon navigation requires no understanding.** It "involves monitoring the location of the self with respect to a single cue, irrespective of other environmental information" (p. 4). No explanatory understanding of why the beacon marks the goal. This is the simplest form of landmark use and develops earliest.

2. **Geometric module is automatic and pre-attentive.** Rats use geometric boundary information even when featural cues are available and would be more informative (Cheng 1986). Young children do the same (Hermer & Spelke 1994). The system operates on geometric properties, not on understanding of what boundaries are for.

3. **Boundary encoding is incidental.** Participants encode boundary information even when not instructed to do so (Doeller & Burgess 2008). No explanatory understanding needed; the spatial system picks up geometric structure automatically.

4. **HPC categories don't require the categoriser to understand the mechanisms.** Chan et al.'s landmark taxonomy is about what the organism's spatial system does with visual information, not about what the organism understands about navigation. The dog doesn't need to know that painted lines are spatial demarcations; it needs only a spatial system that treats linear high-contrast boundaries as navigational features. HPC theory says the category is constituted by the property cluster and its maintaining mechanisms, not by the categoriser's understanding of those mechanisms.

## Connections to existing grist

1. **Geometric module + TRAIL:** Cheng's geometric module processes boundary geometry automatically. A painted line IS a boundary. If the dog's geometric-module equivalent encodes the painted line as a linear boundary, this gives the painted-line response a neural grounding beyond "it looks like a trail." The dog's spatial system is architecturally primed to encode linear boundaries.

2. **Boundary vector cells + Reddy et al. 2021:** Chan et al.'s discussion of boundary vector cells (tuned to distance and direction of environmental boundaries) connects to Reddy et al.'s sector search model (dogs maintain a noisy estimate of trail heading). Both describe geometric, predictive spatial processing -- not point-by-point stimulus response.

3. **Function-based taxonomy + HPC:** Chan et al.'s insistence that landmark function (not form) determines category membership is structurally identical to the HPC claim about TRAIL. The same visual feature (a line) functions as a trail for the dog because the dog's navigational system assigns it trail-like function based on geometric properties. The function is real (neurally instantiated, behaviourally measurable) even though it's context-dependent.

4. **Salience vs. function + Godfrey-Smith:** The finding that spatial position matters more than perceptual salience for navigational function (p. 3) directly addresses observer-relativity concerns. Functional domains are individuated by neural circuits and behavioural measures, not by the researcher's interpretation.

5. **Associative cues + Cafazzo et al. 2012:** Free-ranging dogs' preferential marking at trail crossroads (decision points) parallels Chan et al.'s finding that objects at decision points acquire special navigational status. Crossroads are decision points in a trail network; dogs treat them differently from trail segments. This is functional categorisation of trail topology.

## Limitations and cautions

1. Chan et al.'s evidence is from rodents and humans, not dogs specifically. The extension to canines is reasonable (shared mammalian neural architecture, especially hippocampal and striatal systems) but not directly demonstrated by this paper.

2. The "geometric module" claim is debated. Learmonth et al. (2002) and others have shown that adults can override geometric information with featural cues; the modularity claim has been weakened (see Cheng & Newcombe 2005 for a review). The paper acknowledges this: "these studies do not imply that children and animals cannot utilize featural cues" (p. 7).

3. The paper does not discuss trails or linear features specifically. The boundary/surface discussion is about walls, room geometry, and building contours. Applying the reference-frame category to a painted line on open ground requires an inferential step: the line functions as a boundary without being an enclosure.

4. No discussion of olfactory landmarks (the paper is about visual information in spatial navigation). The multi-modal integration that characterizes real trail-following (visual + olfactory + substrate) is outside the paper's scope.
