# Philosophy of Science Notes for HPC Reframing of CMT

## 1) Hartry Field (1973), "Theory Change and the Indeterminacy of Reference"

### Core argument (2-3 sentences)
Field argues that scientific revolutions can produce genuine referential indeterminacy: earlier terms may not determinately pick out one later-accepted entity/property. His main case is Newtonian `mass`, where there is no fact of the matter whether Newton referred to proper mass or relativistic mass. To preserve semantic evaluation across theory change, he introduces partial denotation and denotational refinement.

### HPC-relevant claims (support/challenge)
- Partial reference / referential indeterminacy: Yes, this is the central thesis.
- How theoretical terms fix reference: Against simple one-shot determinate reference; term tokens can partially denote multiple candidates.
- Mechanism, maintenance, stability of reference: Stability is modeled as historical denotational refinement (single or multiple), not all-or-nothing continuity/failure.
- Success conditions for metaphor/analogy in science: Not directly about metaphor, but gives a formal semantics that later metaphor-based accounts can use when reference is underdetermined.

### Quotable passages (with page numbers)
- "[S]cientific revolutions show that many scientific terms are referentially indeterminate - there is no fact of the matter as to what they denote..." (p. 462)
- "There is no basis whatever for deciding between these two possibilities.... [T]here is no fact of the matter as to whether the word `mass`... denoted relativistic mass or proper mass." (p. 468)
- "A term that partially denotes more than one quantity is called referentially indeterminate...." (p. 475)
- "[I]f I am right in thinking that denotational refinement is a fairly common feature of scientific revolutions... many of our current scientific terms are referentially indeterminate." (p. 480)
- "[P]artial denotation and partial signification... are perfectly objective relations between words and extralinguistic objects..." (p. 481)

### Key references cited to track down
- W. V. O. Quine, *Word and Object* (1960)
- W. V. O. Quine, *Ontological Relativity and Other Essays* (1969)
- Thomas Kuhn, *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962)
- Hartry Field, "Tarski's Theory of Truth" (1972)

### Connection to HPC reframing argument
Field supplies the formal core for your "partial overlap" claim: terms need not map to essences, yet can retain objective, projectible contact with reality via partial denotation and refinement. That directly supports an HPC-style view of metaphor extension as structured but non-essentialist cross-domain mapping.

## 2) D. Cummiskey (1992), "Reference Failure and Scientific Realism: A Response to the Meta-Induction"

### Core argument (2-3 sentences)
Cummiskey argues that pure causal theories of reference cannot explain reference failure for theoretical terms and therefore trivialize realism. He proposes a hybrid causal-plus-descriptive account using Boyd's theory-constitutive metaphors: metaphors constrain what could count as successful reference while remaining open-ended enough for scientific development. This account explains reference failure, partial reference, and realist progress as accommodation of language to causal structure.

### HPC-relevant claims (support/challenge)
- Partial reference / referential indeterminacy: Yes; explicitly endorses Boyd + Field on partial reference and bifurcating kinds.
- How theoretical terms fix reference: Via hybrid constraints: causal contact plus metaphorically structured descriptive constraints.
- Mechanism, maintenance, stability of reference: Strongly yes; emphasizes "fruitful articulation" of explanatory mechanisms over time as the stability criterion.
- Success conditions for metaphor/analogy in science: Explicitly yes; metaphor succeeds when subsequent inquiry finds causally relevant similarities and can articulate them.

### Quotable passages (with page numbers)
- "[O]nly a hybrid causal and description theory of reference can adequately account for cases of reference failure for theoretical terms." (p. 22)
- "[I]f the account of reference fixing is restricted to what... prompted the use of a term, then theoretical terms cannot fail to refer.... [T]he referential claim of scientific realism would become trivial." (p. 27)
- "Whether we have indeed succeeded in referring... will depend on whether... the new research program proves to be fruitful...." (p. 29)
- "Cases of partial reference and bifurcating natural kinds do not undermine scientific realism but instead lend support to... accommodation of language to the causal structure of the world." (p. 38)
- "[W]e are justified in believing that a theoretical term successfully refers when four necessary conditions are met..." (quasi-perception, theory-constitutive metaphor, phenomenal success, fruitful articulation) (p. 39)

### Key references cited to track down
- Richard Boyd, "Metaphor and Theory Change" (1979)
- Hartry Field, "Theory Change and the Indeterminacy of Reference" (1973)
- Michael Devitt, *Realism and Truth* (1984)
- Hilary Putnam, *Meaning and the Moral Sciences* (1978)
- Larry Laudan, "A Confutation of Convergent Realism" (1981)
- Barent Enc, "Reference of Theoretical Terms" (1976)
- Ernan McMullin, realism/scientific methodology work (esp. 1984 discussion cited)
- Ian Hacking, *Representing and Intervening* (1983)
- C. Hardin & A. Rosenberg (1982) on aether/caloric cases

### Connection to HPC reframing argument
This is the strongest direct support for your thesis. It operationalizes Boyd's line that metaphor is not just embodied projection; it is a research-program scaffold whose success depends on world-involving causal accommodation and ongoing articulation. That provides exactly the stability/maintenance component CMT typically under-specifies.

## 3) Dylan Glynn (2010), "Testing the hypothesis: Objectivity and verification in usage-based Cognitive Semantics"

### Core argument (2-3 sentences)
Glynn argues that even highly subjective semantic analysis can be made testable through operationalization, inter-coder checks, and confirmatory statistics. His corpus study of *bother* finds clustered usage tendencies rather than discrete lexical senses. The theoretical upshot is a multidimensional, gradient conception of meaning.

### HPC-relevant claims (support/challenge)
- Partial reference / referential indeterminacy: Not a reference-theory paper; no direct treatment of referential indeterminacy.
- How theoretical terms fix reference: Not addressed.
- Mechanism, maintenance, stability of reference: Indirectly relevant methodologically; provides procedures for stabilizing and testing semantic claims over analysts/datasets.
- Success conditions for metaphor/analogy in science: Indirectly relevant; argues cognitive-semantic work (including metaphor research) should be empirically testable.

### Quotable passages (with page numbers)
- "[T]o demonstrate theoretically that discreet senses and discreet linguistic forms do not exist." (p. 239)
- "Operationalisation enables quantification; quantification enables verification and thus the testing of hypotheses." (p. 242)
- "It is not objectivity that quantitative analysis offers us, but a better and more varied way of verifying the results." (p. 242)
- "[T]he different senses identified represent tendencies, not discreet categories. Lakoff (1987...) assumes distinct lexical senses and Langacker (1994) ... argues that senses... are necessarily discreet. The results here suggest that such positions are erroneous." (p. 262)
- "[C]ognitive models, and metaphors should consider adopting quantitative corpus-driven methods." (p. 264)

### Key references cited to track down
- George Lakoff, *Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things* (1987)
- Ronald Langacker, "The limits of continuity: Discreetness in Cognitive Semantics" (1994)
- Dirk Geeraerts, work on vagueness/polysemy (esp. 1993)
- Adam Kilgarriff, "I don't believe in word senses" (1997)
- Leonard Talmy on introspection/methodology (2007, 2008)

### Connection to HPC reframing argument
Glynn does not provide metaphysics of reference, but it strengthens your methodological flank: if metaphor-based semantic claims are gradient and cluster-structured, they should be evaluated with reproducible evidence rather than treated as fixed discrete schemas. That is congenial to an HPC framing (clustered tendencies + stabilizing practices) and can help you show what CMT lacks in explicit stability machinery.

## Cross-paper takeaway for your HPC/CMT thesis
- Field gives the semantic architecture for partial reference and indeterminacy under theory change.
- Cummiskey (via Boyd) gives the causal-mechanistic and developmental account of when metaphor-guided theorizing succeeds or fails.
- Glynn gives empirical methodology for testing cluster/gradient semantic structure rather than assuming discrete sense inventories.
- Together, they support: metaphor as cross-domain HPC extension under partial overlap, with success tied to causal accommodation plus stabilizing articulation practices.
