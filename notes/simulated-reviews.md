# Simulated Reviews: "What Dogs Know About Trails"

These are simulated referee reports for the draft paper "What Dogs Know About Trails: Metaphor, Mechanisms, and the Phylogeny of Cross-Domain Extension," generated in February 2026 by Claude Opus 4.6. Each review adopts the perspective, expertise, and likely concerns of a specific scholar or writer. They are not actual reviews and should be read as structured adversarial feedback from divergent disciplinary standpoints.

## Dedre Gentner, Northwestern University

This is a genuinely ambitious paper that attempts to bridge two literatures -- Boyd's philosophy-of-science work on homeostatic property clusters and the experimental metaphor literature -- that have, as the author correctly observes, developed without mutual engagement. The dog case is memorable and the paper is well written. I have, however, serious reservations about whether "HPC extension" does the explanatory work the author claims.

**Strengths.** The observation that Lakoff (1987) and Boyd (1979) converge on anti-essentialist property-cluster accounts without citing each other is genuinely illuminating (Section 1, para. 4). The identification of a stability gap in CMT -- "Lakoff has phenomenology without etiology" (Section 2, final paragraph) -- is well put and largely correct. The reflexivity point in Section 3, that applying HPC to grammar is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense, is the most intellectually interesting move in the paper. I also appreciate that the paper takes Murphy (1996) seriously rather than treating him as a foil.

**Central concern: Does "HPC extension" add explanatory power beyond structure-mapping?** Section 4 claims that the comparison-versus-categorisation debate "turns out to capture two stages of HPC extension," with novel metaphors as mechanism-transfer tests and conventional metaphors as HPC category membership. But what is "testing whether source-domain mechanisms can apply to the target" if not structural alignment? The author writes that my systematicity principle is "homeostatic coherence by another name" (Section 4, para. 6). If that is true, then HPC extension is structure-mapping by another name, not a resolution of anything. The resolution of a theoretical debate requires showing that the new framework generates predictions that neither predecessor does. What prediction does HPC extension make that structure-mapping does not?

I can see one candidate answer: the claim that metaphors conventionalise when "mechanisms in the source domain predict properties in the target domain" and fail when "the overlap is superficial and no mechanisms sustain it" (Section 4, para. 1). This is promising. But the paper doesn't operationalise "mechanism" clearly enough to make it testable. In the ARGUMENT IS WAR case (Section 4, para. 2), the author lists "military institutions, training, logistics" as mechanisms maintaining the war cluster, and "discursive norms, social hierarchies, reputational stakes" as maintaining the argument cluster. These are very different things. What is the shared mechanism? If the answer is "competition for dominance" or "strategic reasoning," those are relational commonalities -- exactly what structure-mapping already captures. The HPC vocabulary adds machinery but I am not yet convinced it adds explanatory leverage.

**The dog case (Section 5).** This is the most original contribution. The ethological evidence is carefully assembled and the painted-line observation is striking. But I want to press on whether the dog's behaviour is genuinely *metaphorical* or simply *broad categorisation*. The author acknowledges the boundary is "sharp on this account ... but epistemically inaccessible" (Section 6, para. 2). If we cannot tell where literal categorisation ends and metaphorical extension begins, the claim that the dog is doing something metaphorical has no empirical bite. The author's own continuum table (Section 5.2) places the painted line at "marginal / metaphorical," which is precisely the zone where the framework cannot discriminate. I would find the argument stronger if the author committed to one reading (this is broad categorisation enabled by HPC structure, and that is all metaphor is) rather than hedging between categorisation and something more.

**Treatment of the career of metaphor.** The author correctly reports our finding that "repeated exposure to novel metaphors shifts processing from simile-preference to metaphor-preference" (Section 6, para. 4, citing Bowdle and Gentner 2005: 197). The claim that HPC adds an explanation of *why* some metaphors conventionalise and others do not (Section 6, para. 5) is the strongest novel contribution. But the caloric/kinetic example (borrowed from Cummiskey 1992) is a case of scientific metaphor, not everyday language. Can the author demonstrate the same discriminative power for ordinary conceptual metaphors? Why does ARGUMENT IS WAR conventionalise but ARGUMENT IS BREAKFAST not? Saying "no shared mechanisms" is not an explanation unless the author specifies what would count as a mechanism independently of whether the metaphor succeeds. Otherwise the account is circular: productive metaphors have shared mechanisms; we know the mechanisms are shared because the metaphor is productive.

**What is missing.** The paper does not engage with alignment-based evidence that would challenge its claims. For instance, it does not discuss the role of progressive alignment in metaphor comprehension (Gentner and colleagues), or structural consistency constraints, or the experimental evidence that directionality effects in metaphor interpretation track relational asymmetries rather than mechanism asymmetries. The claim that "what transfers in metaphor is relational structure -- mechanisms -- not intrinsic properties" (Section 4, para. 6) is presented as an HPC prediction but was in fact established by structure-mapping theory in 1983. Acknowledging priority more carefully would strengthen the paper's credibility.

**Recommendation.** Revise and resubmit. The central insight -- that HPC theory can ground the stability of metaphorical mappings in a way that CMT cannot -- is worth developing. But the paper must (1) identify at least one clear prediction that HPC extension makes and structure-mapping does not, (2) operationalise "mechanism" independently of metaphor success to avoid circularity, and (3) address whether the dog case demonstrates metaphor or just broad perceptual categorisation. The reflexivity argument in Section 3 deserves more development; it may in fact be the most durable contribution of the paper.

## Raymond W. Gibbs, Jr., University of California, Santa Cruz

This is an ambitious and genuinely interesting paper that attempts to bridge two literatures that have indeed developed without mutual engagement: Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory and Boyd's Homeostatic Property Cluster theory from philosophy of science. The central proposal, that metaphor is "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap" (Section 4), is provocative and worth engaging with seriously. But I have substantial concerns about how the paper handles embodiment, and I think the author has made a move in Section 4 that looks like integration but is actually demotion.

**Strengths**

The observation that Lakoff (1987) and Boyd (1979) developed structurally parallel anti-essentialist frameworks without citing each other is genuinely striking, and the author is right that nobody has articulated the connection. The reflexivity point in Section 3 (that applying HPC to grammar is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense) is elegant and well-argued. I also appreciate the author's adherence to Rapoport's Rules throughout: the characterisation of what Lakoff got right in Section 2 ("All of this is insightful and largely right") is fair and accurate before the critique begins.

The dog TRAIL case study in Section 5 is the paper's most original contribution. The convergent evidence from Thesen et al. (1993), Hepper and Wells (2005), and Cafazzo et al. (2012) is carefully assembled, and the painted-line observation is a vivid diagnostic. The continuum table in Section 5.2, from core TRAIL through painted line to "career path," is genuinely illuminating. The point that what makes the painted line theoretically important is that it "strips away nearly every deflationary alternative simultaneously" (Section 5.2) is well taken.

**The embodiment problem**

My central objection is to the paper's treatment of embodiment. In Section 4, the author writes: "The embodied-versus-abstract dispute (Lakoff versus Murphy/Gentner) is resolved by recognising that embodied schemas are one mechanism type among several." This sentence does enormous theoretical work and receives almost no argument. Embodied experience is not "one mechanism type among several." It is the constitutive ground of conceptual structure. Image schemas do not merely "stabilise" categories the way, say, institutional norms stabilise social categories. They arise from the body's mode of functioning in the world and make abstract thought possible in the first place. There is a difference between a mechanism that maintains a category and a mechanism that makes a category possible. The author conflates these.

The paper's abstract states that the reframing "relocat[es] the explanation from embodied cognition to causal-ecological structure." But embodied cognition IS causal-ecological structure. When I move through space, my body's interaction with the environment produces the SOURCE-PATH-GOAL schema. That is a causal-ecological process. The author writes as though "embodied" and "causal-ecological" were rival explanations, but the embodiment thesis has always been that bodily experience provides epistemic access to the world's causal structure. The paper acknowledges this in passing ("Lakoff was right that bodily experience matters") but never grapples with the deeper claim that embodied experience is what makes cross-domain mapping possible, not merely one factor contributing to it.

**Does the dog case help or hurt?**

Here the paper is in a genuinely interesting bind. The author wants the dog case to show that "the basic operation underlying metaphor is phylogenetically old" (Abstract) and that "any animal with HPC-structured categories should show cross-domain recruitment" (Section 6). But notice what the dog actually does: it follows a painted line because its bodily experience of trails (locomotion along linear features, repeated sensorimotor engagement with the path substrate) has produced a category grounded in embodied interaction. The dog's TRAIL category is not an abstract structure floating free of the body. It is constituted by the dog's particular mode of locomotion, its olfactory apparatus, its postural orientation. The dog extends TRAIL to painted lines precisely because the visual-geometric properties that trigger the extension are properties the dog's body has learned to detect through repeated embodied engagement with real trails.

This actually supports the embodiment thesis rather than undermining it. What the dog case shows is that embodied experience can produce cross-domain extension without language, without deliberate reflection, without cultural scaffolding. That is interesting and worth arguing. But it is not evidence that we should "relocate the explanation from embodied cognition to causal-ecological structure." It is evidence that embodied cognition operates at the phylogenetic level.

**The "why these mappings?" question**

One of the central achievements of CMT is explaining why specific source domains map to specific targets: we understand ARGUMENT in terms of WAR (not in terms of BREAKFAST) because our embodied experience of physical conflict provides structured knowledge that maps systematically onto argumentation. The author claims HPC answers this question through "shared mechanisms" (Section 4): "The metaphor is productive because shared mechanisms license inferences." But what are these "shared mechanisms" between argument and war? The author lists "competition for dominance, strategic and tactical reasoning, alliance formation, victory and defeat." These are themselves metaphorically described. The mechanisms that connect argument and war are not causal-ecological processes in the way that trail compaction is. They are experiential correlations grounded in embodied social interaction. The paper has not escaped embodiment; it has smuggled it back in under the label "mechanism."

**Stability: a real contribution**

I want to acknowledge that the author's point about stability mechanisms in Section 2 is well taken. Lakoff's framework does lack an explicit account of what maintains metaphorical mappings across speakers and generations. The observation that "neural recruitment is a learning mechanism, not a maintenance mechanism" is sharp. HPC may indeed supply something CMT needs here. But the right move is to integrate HPC's stability story with the embodiment thesis, not to replace one with the other.

**Recommendations**

I would encourage a revised version that treats embodied experience as a constitutive mechanism (perhaps the primary one for concrete-to-abstract mappings) rather than reducing it to "one mechanism type among several." The dog case is powerful precisely because it is an embodied case. The author should lean into this rather than using the dog to argue against embodiment. Section 6's discussion of what is "distinctively human" would benefit from engaging with the extensive experimental literature on embodied simulation during metaphor processing (e.g., Wilson & Gibbs 2007; Gibbs 2006), which shows that embodied experience is not merely a developmental precondition for metaphor but is actively recruited during online comprehension.

The paper has a genuine contribution to make. It should make it without demoting the explanatory framework that made the contribution visible.

*Recommendation:* Major revision.

## Muhammad Ali Khalidi

**Summary.** This paper argues that Homeostatic Property Cluster (HPC) theory supplies the stability mechanisms missing from Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory (CMT), and proposes that metaphor be reconceived as "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap." A case study of canine trail cognition illustrates the idea: dogs extend a functional spatial category, TRAIL, to painted lines on soccer fields that share only visual-geometric properties with actual trails. The paper contends this operation is phylogenetically old and that distinctively human metaphor adds deliberate exploitation, recursive stacking, and linguistic communication.

I found much to admire. The identification of the non-engagement between Boyd and Lakoff is genuinely illuminating, and the paper is right that these two traditions converge on anti-essentialism without mutual citation. The synthesis of the canine ethological literature in Section 5 is impressively thorough, and the escalating evidential structure (from trail-following through defecation-avoidance to painted-line following) is well constructed. The reflexivity point in Section 3 -- that applying HPC to grammar is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense -- is philosophically sophisticated and worth developing further. The paper makes a real contribution in bringing these literatures together.

I have, however, several concerns.

**1. The sharp-boundary claim is doing no work and may be doing harm.** In Section 3, the author writes that Boyd "is right that HPC kinds resist sharp extensional characterisation, but wrong about the source of the resistance. The boundaries are sharp -- maintained by mechanisms at determinate thresholds -- but epistemically inaccessible because the relevant thresholds can't be finitely specified" (Section 3). This is then echoed in Section 6: "The boundary between 'broad literal category' and 'metaphorical extension' is sharp on this account... It's determinate but not determinable in practice."

I think this is both philosophically questionable and unnecessary for the paper's argument. The author presents no independent argument for sharp boundaries here, only a parenthetical citation to two unpublished manuscripts (Reynolds 2025sorites; Reynolds 2025hpcbook: Ch. 5). Without access to those arguments, the claim reads as a stipulation. More importantly, the author concedes that this "divergence from Boyd doesn't affect the argument that follows, which depends on property clustering and mechanisms, not on the metaphysics of boundary vagueness." If it doesn't affect the argument, why include it?

I would go further: the paper's own evidence tells against sharp boundaries. The continuum table in Section 5.2, running from core TRAIL through painted line to "career path," is more naturally read as exhibiting genuinely vague boundaries than as exhibiting sharp but epistemically inaccessible ones. The author acknowledges that "the point at which it crosses from literal to metaphorical can't be specified independently of investigative context" (Section 4). This sounds like ontological context-dependence, not epistemic limitation. If the boundary's location depends on the investigative context, that is a feature of the kind itself (whose identity, as Boyd notes, is indexed to explanatory purpose), not a feature of our ignorance about a fixed boundary.

The paper would be stronger if it adopted a position closer to Boyd's own: genuine extensional indeterminacy as a feature of HPC kinds, not a deficiency. This would also cohere better with the field-relativity of "cutting nature at its joints" that the paper endorses in Section 7.

**2. Is "cross-domain HPC extension" a genuine theoretical advance?** The central claim is that metaphor is "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap." This is presented as a unification of Boyd and Lakoff, but I worry it may be less than it appears. The key move is saying that source domains are HPC kinds and that metaphorical mappings extend these kinds to new domains. But HPC theory already says that kind membership tolerates partial instantiation (Boyd's point 6 in the 1979 paper). And metaphor theory already says metaphors map relational structure across domains. Is the synthesis more than the conjunction of these two claims? The paper needs to identify a prediction or explanation that the conjunction yields but that neither framework yields alone. The dissolution of the comparison-versus-categorisation debate (Section 4) comes closest to this, but it needs more development. In what precise way does invoking "HPC stabilisation trajectory" explain the conventionalisation of metaphor better than Bowdle and Gentner's own account?

**3. Is TRAIL a natural kind?** The paper's argument requires that the dog's TRAIL category is an HPC kind in some philosophically robust sense. The ethological evidence is impressive, but I am not persuaded the paper has distinguished TRAIL from a broad perceptual category. The properties listed in Section 5.1 (linear extent, visual contrast, hard substrate, concentrated scent, frequent locomotion, connectivity, bounded width) are causally maintained, yes, but by the physical process of trail creation and use -- not by mechanisms internal to the category itself. One might say trails are simply a type of geographical feature, and the dog has a perceptual capacity to detect them. The HPC apparatus may be more machinery than needed. I would like to see the author address what explanatory work "HPC kind" does for TRAIL that "perceptual category with causally correlated cues" does not.

**4. Metaphysics versus epistemology.** The paper sometimes elides the distinction between the metaphysics of category boundaries and the epistemology of classification in ways that weaken the argument. In Section 6, the "sharp but epistemically inaccessible" boundary between literal and metaphorical is presented as "a prediction, not a problem." But this makes the sharpness claim unfalsifiable: any failure to locate the boundary confirms it is merely epistemically inaccessible. A genuinely vague boundary, by contrast, makes a different prediction: there are cases where there is no fact of the matter about whether an extension is literal or metaphorical. The painted-line case might be one such case. The paper would benefit from taking this possibility seriously rather than treating indeterminacy as always epistemic.

**Minor points.** The paper is well written and engagingly argued. The dog opening is effective. I would flag that the Barja et al. (2005) wolf data in Section 5.3, while interesting, introduces a complication the paper doesn't fully address: wolves mark *on* trails, pet dogs avoid marking on trails. The paper notes both behaviours require trail/non-trail discrimination, which is fair, but the reversal of the behavioural rule deserves more attention. Does the HPC framework predict or explain why the *same* category generates opposite behavioural outputs in different populations?

**Recommendation.** Revise and resubmit. The core idea -- that HPC theory provides the mechanistic grounding CMT lacks -- is promising and potentially significant. The sharp-boundary claim should be either properly argued or removed; the theoretical advance needs sharper articulation; and the status of TRAIL as an HPC kind (rather than a perceptual category) requires further defence.

## Alexandra Horowitz, Barnard College, Columbia University

I found much to admire in this paper. The core philosophical project -- connecting Boyd's HPC framework to Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory -- is original, clearly argued, and fills a genuine lacuna in the literature. The reflexivity observation (Section 3, final paragraph) is genuinely arresting. And the paper is uncommonly well written for a philosophy of science submission. However, as someone who has spent decades studying dog cognition and who knows the ethological literature cited in Sections 5.1--5.3 quite well, I have serious reservations about the canine case study that forms the paper's empirical backbone.

**The painted-line observation is inadequate as evidence.** The paper opens with it (Section 1) and returns to it as the "theoretically important case" (Section 5.2): "my dog follows painted lines on soccer fields. Not occasionally or ambiguously: she tracks along the white line the way she tracks along a trail through the woods." This is an anecdote about one dog, reported by the dog's owner, with no systematic documentation. There is no video analysis, no control condition, no measurement of the dog's trajectory relative to the line, no assessment of how frequently the behaviour occurs, no comparison across dogs. The author acknowledges none of these limitations. Anyone who has walked dogs in parks knows that dogs do all manner of things that look purposeful and systematic to their owners but dissolve under controlled observation. I do not doubt that the author has seen his dog walking along painted lines. But the inferential weight the paper places on this observation -- it is called "the clincher" in the planning notes and carries the entire cross-domain extension argument -- far exceeds what a single uncontrolled observation can bear.

**Simpler explanations are dismissed too quickly.** Section 5.2 addresses the edge-following alternative in a single sentence: "But edge-following involves walking alongside a boundary, not on it. If the dog tracks along the line itself, that's 'this is a trail, follow it.'" This is unsatisfying. First, thigmotaxis in rodents is not the only relevant comparison; dogs are known to orient along high-contrast visual features for reasons having nothing to do with trail cognition. Second, how precisely is "along" versus "on" being measured? A five-centimetre painted line and a dog's gait make the distinction between tracking the line and tracking the edge of the line operationally meaningless without instrumentation. Third, visual salience itself is unaddressed. A white line on a green field is a high-contrast, high-salience stimulus. Dogs are drawn to novel and salient environmental features. The possibility that the dog is simply investigating a visually prominent stimulus, not categorising it as a trail, is never considered.

**The leap from "dogs follow trails" to "dogs have a functional category TRAIL" is not warranted by the cited literature.** Thesen, Steen, and Doving (1993), Hepper and Wells (2005), and Cafazzo, Natoli, and Valsecchi (2012) are all solid studies, and the paper's summaries of them are largely accurate (though I note that Section 5.1 says dogs "examine 3--5 footprints" during the deciding phase, while Thesen et al. actually report "2--5 footprints" on p. 250 -- a minor point, but precision matters when the argument rests on convergence across studies). What these studies show is that dogs are sophisticated olfactory trackers who use sequential temporal information to determine trail direction and who mark certain spatial locations preferentially. None of these studies demonstrate or claim to demonstrate that dogs possess a "functional spatial category" in any cognitively robust sense. The paper treats behavioural regularities as evidence for category representation without arguing for the inference. The seven properties listed in Section 5.1 (linear extent, visual contrast, hard substrate, concentrated scent, frequent locomotion, connectivity, bounded width) are properties of trails as described by a human observer. The paper provides no evidence that dogs represent these properties as a cluster. The three-phase behaviour reported by Thesen et al. shows that dogs process olfactory information in structured stages; it does not show that they have a concept TRAIL of which these stages are diagnostic.

**The Cafazzo et al. and Barja et al. studies are over-interpreted.** The paper claims that "crossroads are topological nodes where trails intersect" and that "the preferential marking at these sites shows that dogs don't just use trails -- they recognise and exploit the functional topology of the trail network" (Section 5.1). But Cafazzo et al. studied territorial boundary marking, not trail-following. Boundaries and trails are different things. A territorial boundary is a spatial zone defined by intergroup dynamics; a trail is a path defined by repeated locomotion. The paper conflates them. Similarly, Barja et al. (2005) studied wolf scat placement on roads and trails in the context of territorial defence. The "zone-dependent strategic behaviour" reported (Section 5.3) is strategic scent-marking, not trail categorisation. That wolves mark differently near dens than at the territorial periphery tells us about territorial cognition, not about whether wolves have a concept TRAIL.

**What would strengthen the paper.** The theoretical architecture (Sections 2--4, 6--7) is strong enough to stand without the canine case study. But if the authors wish to retain it, three things are needed. First, systematic documentation of the painted-line behaviour: video of multiple dogs, trajectory analysis, comparison with behaviour on actual trails, controls for visual salience and novelty. Second, a more careful discussion of what "functional category" means in non-linguistic animals and what evidence would be required to attribute one. The current argument infers category representation from behavioural regularity without engaging with the substantial literature on this inferential problem in comparative cognition (see, among others, Penn, Holyoak & Povinelli 2008 on the "relational reinterpretation" problem). Third, the paper needs to take seriously the possibility that the painted-line behaviour, if confirmed, admits of simpler explanations -- visual salience, edge-following, generalisation gradient from actual trails -- that do not require attributing cross-domain category extension to the dog. The principle of parsimony is not a bureaucratic obstacle; it is a methodological commitment that protects us from seeing in our dogs what we wish to find.

I would welcome this paper after revision, with the canine sections substantially reworked.

**Summary of recommendations:**

1. The painted-line observation requires systematic empirical documentation before it can carry the argumentative weight currently assigned to it.
2. Alternative explanations (visual salience, contrast-tracking, generalisation gradient) need substantive engagement, not single-sentence dismissal.
3. The inference from behavioural regularity to functional category representation needs explicit justification, engaging with the comparative cognition literature on this problem.
4. The uses of Cafazzo et al. (2012) and Barja et al. (2005) conflate territorial boundary marking with trail categorisation; these need to be disentangled.
5. The minor factual error (3--5 footprints versus 2--5 footprints in Thesen et al.) should be corrected.

## Keith J. Holyoak, UCLA

This is an ambitious and genuinely novel paper. The core move -- bridging Boyd's HPC theory with Lakoff and Johnson's CMT through a unified account of metaphor as "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap" -- fills what the author correctly identifies as a real lacuna. I was not previously aware of Boyd's work when Stamenkovic and I wrote our 2018 review, and in retrospect the omission was significant. The paper is well-written, the canine case study is memorable, and the author's willingness to take seriously the phylogenetic implications of the framework is commendable. That said, I have substantial reservations about whether the proposal generates genuinely new predictions beyond what existing multi-constraint models already deliver.

**Strengths**

The identification of the Boyd-Lakoff gap is the paper's strongest contribution. Section 2 diagnoses a real problem: CMT describes property clusters without theorising the mechanisms that maintain them. The observation that "Lakoff has phenomenology without etiology" (end of Section 2) is sharp and, I think, correct. The Dyirbal language-death evidence is effective here -- gradient stability loss from edges inward is exactly the kind of phenomenon that demands a mechanistic explanation.

The reflexivity point in Section 3 -- that applying HPC to grammar is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor in Boyd's sense -- is philosophically sophisticated and gives the paper an intellectual depth that most work in this area lacks.

The canine trail-cognition evidence in Section 5 is carefully marshalled. The convergence between Thesen et al. (1993) on the deciding phase (3-5 footprints examined) and Hepper and Wells (2005) on the five-footstep threshold for directional inference is a genuinely interesting empirical observation. The painted-line case is vivid and does strip away several deflationary alternatives.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Does "HPC extension" add explanatory power beyond existing multi-constraint models?** This is my central concern. In our 2018 review, Stamenkovic and I concluded that metaphor comprehension involves multiple mechanisms -- categorisation for simple conventional metaphors, analogy for complex novel ones -- integrated under pragmatic and contextual constraints. The paper acknowledges this review (Section 1) and claims that HPC "fills both gaps" (end of Section 4). But the specific predictions I can extract from the HPC framework look remarkably similar to what multi-constraint theory already delivers. When the author writes that "metaphorical inference works when mechanisms in the source domain predict properties in the target domain" and "fails when the overlap is superficial and no mechanisms sustain it" (Section 4, paragraph 1), this is very close to the structure-mapping account's systematicity principle plus aptness constraints. What does HPC add beyond a philosophical vocabulary for describing the same constraint satisfaction?

2. **Experimental predictions.** The paper is almost entirely theoretical. Where are the novel, testable predictions that distinguish HPC extension from existing accounts? The author claims that HPC provides "exclusion power" (Section 4 and Section 7) -- if you cannot identify shared mechanisms, the metaphor is superficial. But this is precisely what the systematicity principle already predicts: mappings without interconnected relational structure fail. The author needs to specify at least two or three experimental paradigms where HPC extension makes different predictions from multi-constraint theory. As it stands, I cannot identify a single case where the two frameworks diverge empirically.

3. **Section 4's reframing of three debates.** The dissolution of the comparison-versus-categorisation debate as "two stages of HPC extension" is plausible but not new. Bowdle and Gentner (2005) already proposed precisely this trajectory, and the author acknowledges as much. What HPC adds, the author claims, is an explanation of *why* some metaphors conventionalise and others do not: "when the relational structure (mechanisms) can be maintained in the target domain." But "mechanism maintainability" is not operationalised anywhere in the paper. Without an independent measure of mechanism maintainability, this is circular: productive metaphors are productive because their mechanisms are maintainable, and we know the mechanisms are maintainable because the metaphors are productive.

4. **The dog case: evidence or illustration?** The painted-line observation is compelling as a vivid example, but its evidential status is unclear. First, it appears to be a single anecdotal observation of the author's own dog. Second, the alternative the author dismisses -- general edge-following or contrast-tracking behaviour -- deserves more serious engagement. Thigmotaxis-like behaviour is widespread in mammals and does not require positing cross-domain category extension. The author asserts that "edge-following involves walking alongside a boundary, not on it" (Section 5.2), but this distinction needs empirical verification, not stipulation. Is the dog walking *on* the line or *along* it? How wide is the line relative to the dog's gait? Third, even granting that the dog is extending its TRAIL category to the painted line, the jump from "broad literal generalisation" to "metaphorical extension" depends on a functional-domain boundary that the paper defines but does not independently justify. Why is a painted line in a "different functional domain" from a trail, rather than simply a low-typicality trail? The HPC framework itself predicts gradient typicality -- so where does the gradient cross from literal to metaphorical? The author concedes this boundary is "epistemically inaccessible" (Section 6), which is honest, but it means the dog case cannot serve as evidence for cross-domain extension specifically. It is consistent with the theory but does not test it.

5. **Persistence and death of metaphors.** The paper claims that HPC explains why some metaphors persist and others die (Section 4). This is an interesting claim, but to test it we would need independent measures of mechanism strength in source and target domains, assessed before we know the metaphor's fate. Otherwise we are doing post hoc explanation, not prediction. The caloric/kinetic comparison from Cummiskey (1992) is instructive but drawn from the history of science, not from experimental metaphor research.

**Recommendation**

I recommend major revision. The paper identifies a genuine theoretical gap and proposes an interesting framework, but it needs to (a) articulate specific experimental predictions that distinguish HPC extension from multi-constraint theory, (b) provide an operationalisation of "mechanism maintainability" that is not circular, and (c) either strengthen the dog case with controlled observation or present it honestly as motivating illustration rather than evidence. The philosophical scaffolding is strong; the empirical scaffolding is not yet built.

## Frank C. Keil, Yale University

This is an ambitious and frequently illuminating paper. The central idea -- that Boyd's HPC framework supplies the stability mechanisms missing from Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory -- is genuinely novel and, in my view, largely correct. Sections 2 through 4 constitute a strong philosophical contribution. The identification of the white space between the CMT and HPC literatures is well documented: the fact that Lakoff (1987) never cites Boyd, and that the experimental metaphor literature (Flusberg, Thibodeau, Holyoak & Stamenkovic) never engages with theory-constitutive metaphors, is itself worth establishing in print. The paper's reframing of the comparison-versus-categorisation debate (Section 4, paragraph beginning "The comparison-versus-categorisation debate") as two stages of a single HPC stabilisation trajectory is elegant, and the integration of Cummiskey (1992) on mechanism articulability as a discriminator between productive and superficial metaphors is well handled.

I have five concerns, two of them substantial.

**1. The central evidential question: Do dogs form HPC-structured categories, or is the paper assuming what it needs to prove?**

The paper's Argument B depends on the claim that canine TRAIL cognition has "all the hallmarks of an HPC kind" (Section 5.3). But the evidence presented -- from Thesen et al. (1993), Hepper and Wells (2005), and Cafazzo et al. (2012) -- establishes that dogs have a multi-property spatial representation supporting directional inference, not that this representation has the specific causal-explanatory structure that HPC theory requires. The convergence across studies is impressive: the minimum structural unit of 3--5 discrete odour samples for directional inference (Section 5.1) rules out simple chemotaxis. But an HPC kind is not merely a multi-property cluster; it is a cluster whose coherence is maintained by homeostatic mechanisms and whose identity depends on those mechanisms. What the paper establishes is that the properties of trails co-occur because of ecological processes (walking deposits odour, compaction creates visual contrast, etc.). That is a story about the *world*, not about the *dog's representation*. The question is whether the dog's TRAIL category is structured around explanatory relations among properties, or whether it is a perceptual prototype sustained by feature correlation without any grasp of the mechanisms linking the features. This distinction matters enormously for the paper's argument, because if the dog's categorisation is purely perceptual, then the painted-line extension (Section 5.2) is stimulus generalisation, not cross-domain HPC extension.

**2. Does "cross-domain HPC extension" require explanatory understanding?**

The paper characterises the painted-line case as metaphorical because the dog "has recruited its TRAIL category to assimilate an object whose geometric profile fits but whose ecological role doesn't" (Section 5.2). The key claim is that the painted line "belongs to a different functional domain (spatial demarcation, not locomotion)." But functional domain membership is being assigned by the theorist, not by the dog. The dog doesn't know that painted lines are spatial demarcation artefacts. For the dog, the line is simply a high-contrast linear strip that matches the perceptual profile of trails. The cross-domain character of the extension exists in the observer's functional ontology, not necessarily in the animal's. This is a version of the problem that has long concerned me in my work on the development of explanatory understanding: perceptual similarity can drive categorisation without any grasp of the causal or functional distinctions that make the categorisation theoretically interesting. If the painted-line behaviour is driven entirely by perceptual overlap with no sensitivity to the functional role of the object, then calling it "metaphorical" stretches that term beyond its useful range. The paper needs to address this directly. What would distinguish genuine cross-domain HPC extension from perceptual generalisation within a single broadly-defined category?

**3. Use of Noyes and Keil (2019)**

The paper cites our work correctly on the central point: "Kindhood and essentialism are separable" (Section 6). The claim that generics function as a cultural transmission mechanism for HPC categories, with the feedback loop between kind assumption and generic use constituting a homeostatic stabiliser, is a reasonable extension of what we argued. I would note, however, that our findings showed that property content determines the causal interpretation participants adopt (biological properties triggered essentialism; cultural properties did not). This is relevant to the paper's argument in a way that goes unexploited: it suggests that HPC extension should be sensitive to the *type* of mechanism involved, not just to property overlap. The paper treats mechanism support somewhat generically. Are the mechanisms maintaining the dog's TRAIL category of the same type as those maintaining human conceptual metaphors? If not, the unification may be less tight than the paper claims.

**4. The developmental question**

The paper never asks when TRAIL cognition develops. If TRAIL is an HPC-structured category maintained by homeostatic mechanisms, there should be a developmental trajectory: puppies should initially respond to individual trail properties (scent, visual contrast) and only later integrate them into a coherent category supporting cross-domain extension. Wagner et al. (2014) is cited in the notes (but not in the draft) as showing elimination-zone separation by one month of age. Does trail-following behaviour develop on a similar timeline? The developmental data would bear directly on whether TRAIL is a learned HPC category (as the paper's framework predicts) or a fixed action pattern triggered by perceptual releasers (which would undermine the HPC interpretation). This is a significant omission.

**5. Projectibility (Section 5)**

The paper uses "projectibility" in two different senses without clearly distinguishing them. In Section 5.1, it appears in the empirical sense (dogs generalise across substrate and temporal variation, "confirming projectibility"). In Section 5.3, it appears in something closer to Goodman's philosophical sense (the category "is projectible: dogs generalise across surfaces, temporal scales, and contexts"). Goodman's projectibility is a property of predicates in inductive inferences, not a property of animal behaviour. The paper should either define its usage explicitly or adopt a different term for the empirical generalisation. As it stands, the philosophical connotations of "projectibility" do unearned work, making the dog's behaviour sound more like principled inductive reasoning than the evidence warrants.

**Summary recommendation:** Revise and resubmit. The theoretical contribution (Sections 2--4) is strong and publishable. The canine case study (Section 5) needs to confront the perceptual-generalisation alternative more rigorously, provide clearer criteria for distinguishing HPC extension from stimulus generalisation, and address the developmental trajectory. The paper should also clarify whether it is claiming that dogs have explanatory understanding of TRAIL structure or merely that their behaviour is consistent with an HPC-structured representation. These are importantly different claims, and the paper oscillates between them.

## Peter Godfrey-Smith

This is an ambitious and genuinely interesting paper. The core idea -- that HPC theory can supply the stability mechanisms that Conceptual Metaphor Theory lacks, and that the resulting framework ("cross-domain HPC extension") predicts metaphor-like behaviour in non-linguistic animals -- is novel and worth developing. The integration of Boyd and Lakoff, two literatures that have indeed operated in ignorance of each other, is a real contribution. The dog material is vivid and well-documented. I have serious reservations, however, about the phylogenetic argument in Section 6, about the paper's use of the word "metaphor" for what the dog does, and about the individuation of "functional domains." These need substantial revision.

**1. The phylogenetic claim (Section 6): homology or homoplasy?**

The paper asserts that "the basic operation underlying metaphor is phylogenetically old" and that "any animal whose categories have HPC structure should be capable of the basic operation" (Section 6, paragraph 1). This is too quick. There are two quite different possibilities here, and the paper doesn't distinguish them.

First, it could be that the capacity for cross-domain category extension is homologous across mammals (or some broader clade) -- inherited from a common ancestor that already possessed HPC-structured categories and the machinery to extend them across domains. Second, it could be that this capacity has evolved convergently in different lineages: dogs and humans both do something that looks like cross-domain extension, but the underlying cognitive architecture differs. The paper's own evidence is consistent with both. Dogs may have a perceptual-motor system that generalises on geometric features, while humans may have a genuinely domain-general analogical capacity. Both produce behaviour the paper calls "cross-domain HPC extension," but they could be homoplasies, not homologies.

The paper needs to specify what kind of evidence would distinguish these possibilities. Comparative work across more distantly related taxa would help. If octopuses or corvids show the same pattern, the options narrow differently than if only close mammalian relatives do. As it stands, the claim that dogs and humans share "the same basic operation" is underdetermined by the evidence presented.

**2. Is the universal quantifier warranted?**

The paper states: "any animal with HPC-structured categories should show cross-domain recruitment" (Section 1, final paragraph; repeated in Section 6). This is too strong. Having HPC-structured categories is one thing; extending them across functional domains is another. The extension requires, at minimum, (a) that the animal's recognition system is sufficiently sensitive to partial property overlap, (b) that the animal's behavioural repertoire is flexible enough to apply source-domain behaviour in a novel domain, and (c) that the functional domains are separable in the animal's cognitive architecture in the first place. A bacterium might have something like HPC-structured categories (chemical gradients that cluster reliably), but nobody would predict cross-domain recruitment from this.

The paper gestures toward conditions -- "perceptual overlap triggers recruitment" -- but doesn't spell out the minimal cognitive prerequisites. I'd want to know: What kind of representational system is required? Is a perceptual similarity space sufficient, or does the animal need something more structured? Where, on the continuum from chemotaxis to deliberate analogical reasoning, does the "basic operation" sit? The paper would benefit from being explicit about these boundary conditions rather than offering a universal quantifier.

**3. The individuation of functional domains**

In Section 5.2, the paper claims that the painted line "belongs to a different functional domain from TRAIL. It's a spatial demarcation artefact, not a locomotion-supporting ecological feature." This is doing essential work in the argument: without it, the dog's behaviour is just broad generalisation within a single category, not cross-domain extension. But who individuates these domains?

The dog, presumably, doesn't represent the painted line as belonging to "spatial demarcation." It represents it as a linear high-contrast strip. Whether this counts as a different "functional domain" from trails is a judgment the human observer is making. The paper needs a principled, observer-independent criterion for domain individuation, or it needs to acknowledge that the metaphor/generalisation boundary is partly a matter of our descriptive framework rather than the animal's cognitive architecture. This matters because the entire argument turns on the distinction between "broad literal category" and "metaphorical extension," and the paper itself admits the boundary is "epistemically inaccessible" (Section 6, paragraph 2). If we can't determine the boundary, the claim that the dog is doing something metaphorical rather than something merely general loses its grip.

**4. Is "metaphor" the right word?**

The paper is admirably explicit about what it means by metaphor: "cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap" (Section 4). But this definition strips out nearly everything that makes metaphor interesting as a cognitive and communicative phenomenon. Metaphor, in its central cases, involves some awareness (however dim) that one is seeing X as Y, that the mapping is non-literal, that there is a tension between vehicle and tenor. The dog has none of this. The paper acknowledges this in Section 6 -- "The dog following a painted line doesn't know it's making a metaphorical extension and can't communicate that fact to other dogs" -- but then maintains that the dog is doing metaphor anyway.

I think a more cautious formulation would serve the paper better. What the dog does might be a *precursor* to metaphor, or a *component operation* that metaphor exploits, without itself being metaphor. The paper's own graduated scheme (Section 6: basic operation, deliberate exploitation, recursive stacking, linguistic communication) already provides the vocabulary for this more cautious claim. Calling the dog's behaviour "metaphorical" risks stretching the term until it covers any case of stimulus generalisation, which would drain it of theoretical content.

**5. The evolutionary sequence: gradualism or just-so story?**

Section 6 proposes a four-stage sequence: basic cross-domain recruitment, deliberate exploitation, recursive stacking, and linguistic communication. I'm sympathetic to gradualism about cognitive evolution, and this sequence is not implausible. But as stated, it's a just-so story. There is no evidence presented for the transitions between stages. Why would deliberate exploitation evolve? Under what selection pressures? Does recursive stacking require language, or can it precede it? The paper asserts these stages without theorising the transitions.

This is a familiar problem in evolutionary psychology, and I don't think it disqualifies the paper. But the claims should be presented more modestly -- as a framework for further investigation rather than an established evolutionary trajectory. Something like: "If the basic operation is phylogenetically old, then the evolutionary elaboration of metaphor must have involved transitions roughly like these, though the selective pressures and mechanisms of transition remain to be investigated."

**Strengths worth emphasising.** The paper's integration of Boyd and Lakoff is genuinely novel and illuminating. The point that Lakoff has "phenomenology without etiology" (Section 2, final paragraph) is well put. The use of Cummiskey's caloric/kinetic distinction (Section 4) as a discriminator between productive and superficial metaphors is elegant. The canine evidence in Section 5 is impressively assembled, especially the convergence between Thesen et al. and Hepper and Wells on the 3-5 footstep minimum unit.

**Recommendation.** Major revision. The philosophical integration (Sections 2-4) is strong. The phylogenetic and terminological claims (Sections 5-6) need more careful framing: distinguish homology from homoplasy, provide criteria for domain individuation, and consider whether "precursor to metaphor" is more defensible than "metaphor." The evolutionary sequence needs to be presented as speculative scaffolding rather than established fact.

## 9. Elicit.com AI Review

*Source: Elicit.com automated review, February 2026. Not a simulated persona but an AI-generated peer review with no disciplinary allegiance.*

This is a sharp and genuinely original paper. The core move -- reframing conceptual metaphor as cross-domain HPC extension -- is well-motivated and clearly executed. The dog case study is the paper's strongest asset: it grounds what could be an abstract philosophical argument in concrete ethological data. Here are my substantive comments, organized by section.

### The central argument

The claim that Lakoff provides phenomenology without etiology while Boyd provides success conditions without cognitive grounding is precise and, I think, correct. The synthesis is natural once you see it, which is the mark of a good theoretical unification. The key insight -- that metaphor works when *mechanisms* transfer, not just properties -- gives the framework genuine exclusion power. ARGUMENT IS WAR works because shared mechanisms (strategic reasoning, competition for dominance) sustain inference. LANGUAGE IS ROCK doesn't because no mechanisms transfer. That's a real prediction.

The painted-line case is well chosen because it strips away confounds systematically. No scent, no substrate difference, no energy advantage, no social following. If the dog tracks *along* the line (not alongside it), the inference to TRAIL-category recruitment is strong. One question: do you have video or systematic observational data on this behaviour, or is it anecdotal from your own dog? The paper's rhetoric treats it as established, but the evidentiary status is unclear. If it's a single-dog observation, you should say so explicitly and frame it as a hypothesis-generating case rather than a confirmed phenomenon. The ethological literature you cite (Thesen, Hepper & Wells, Cafazzo) is solid, but none of it involves painted lines. That gap matters.

### Section 2: Source domains without mechanisms

The critique of Lakoff is effective. The Dyirbal collapse example is well deployed -- gradient stability loss demanding mechanistic explanation is exactly the kind of challenge that CMT can't answer. The Murphy (1996) point about incoherence from multiple metaphors is a genuine problem, and your dissolution via HPC (different metaphors pick out different mechanism subsets of the same target concept) is clean.

One underdeveloped point: you claim Lakoff has "phenomenology without etiology" and that neural recruitment ("neurons that fire together wire together") is a learning mechanism, not a maintenance mechanism. This is a good distinction, but it's too quick. Hebbian learning *does* maintain associations through continued co-activation -- that's literally what long-term potentiation does. The stronger version of your argument is that neural recruitment explains individual-level maintenance but not community-level stability or cross-generational transmission. The mechanisms you need are ecological and social, not just neural. Sharpen this.

### Section 3: HPC and theory-constitutive metaphor

The Boyd exposition is clear. The clarification about boundary vagueness is important but risks derailing the reader into a separate metaphysical debate. You assert that boundaries are "sharp but epistemically inaccessible" and cite your own preprint. For this paper's purposes, you could trim this to a footnote. The main argument doesn't depend on whether boundary indeterminacy is ontological or epistemic -- you say as much yourself ("This divergence from Boyd doesn't affect the argument that follows"). If it doesn't affect the argument, don't give it a full paragraph in the main text.

The reflexivity point -- that applying HPC to grammar is itself a theory-constitutive metaphor -- is genuinely interesting and could be developed further rather than left as a paragraph. It demonstrates the framework's self-consistency. But it also raises a question you don't address: if HPC-as-applied-to-metaphor is itself a TCM, what are the failure conditions? Under what circumstances would you conclude that the HPC framing of metaphor has failed? You owe the reader an answer to this, especially since you criticize Lakoff for lack of falsifiability.

### Section 4: Metaphor as cross-domain HPC extension

This is the theoretical core and it mostly works. Three comments:

1. The dissolution of the comparison-vs-categorisation debate (Gentner vs Glucksberg) via "two stages of HPC extension" is elegant but needs more argument. You assert that novel metaphors are comparison (structural alignment) and conventional metaphors are categorisation (HPC membership). Bowdle and Gentner (2005) showed the processing shift experimentally, but their framework doesn't invoke HPC. You need to show that HPC adds explanatory value beyond what Bowdle and Gentner already provide. What does your account predict that theirs doesn't?

2. The ARGUMENT IS WAR example is illustrative but could be sharper. You list "competition for dominance, strategic reasoning, alliance formation, victory and defeat" as shared mechanisms. But these are shared *properties*, not mechanisms. The mechanisms in war are military institutions, training, logistics. The mechanisms in argument are discursive norms, social hierarchies, reputational stakes. What's shared is the *relational structure* -- competition, strategy, alliance -- which is maintained by different mechanisms in each domain. Be precise about whether you're claiming shared mechanisms or shared relational structure maintained by domain-specific mechanisms. The latter is more defensible and more interesting.

3. The Cummiskey (1992) illustration of caloric vs kinetic theory is excellent and should perhaps come earlier -- it's the clearest demonstration of mechanism articulability as the success criterion.

### Section 5: The dog case study

5.1 is the strongest subsection. The convergence between Thesen (2--5 footprints in the deciding phase) and Hepper & Wells (five sequential footsteps for direction) is striking and well-presented. The Cafazzo data on crossroad marking adds the topological dimension nicely.

5.2 is where the argument is most vulnerable. The continuum table is clear, but the painted-line case bears all the theoretical weight and has the least empirical support. You acknowledge that edge-following is an alternative explanation and dismiss it in one sentence ("edge-following involves walking alongside a boundary, not on it"). This needs more work. Is there systematic evidence that dogs track *on* painted lines rather than alongside them? If not, the central empirical claim is under-supported. I'd recommend either (a) collecting systematic data on this behaviour, or (b) explicitly framing it as an empirical prediction of the theory rather than an established fact.

5.3 on wild canids is solid. The Barja et al. wolf data showing zone-dependent marking strategies (den-protection vs territorial signalling) is compelling evidence for functional trail discrimination. The three-way gradient (den area to access trail to periphery) is exactly what HPC predicts.

### Section 6: What's distinctively human

The three-part characterisation (deliberate exploitation, recursive stacking, linguistic communication) is clear and plausible. But the claims about what dogs *can't* do are negative claims that need more careful handling. "There's no evidence that dogs stack extensions" is not the same as "dogs can't stack extensions." The absence-of-evidence point should be explicit.

The Bowdle and Gentner (2005) integration is smooth. The HPC explanation of why some metaphors conventionalise (mechanism support in the target domain) and others don't is a genuine addition.

### Structural and stylistic notes

- The paper is well-written throughout. The opening anecdote is effective.
- The abstract tries to do too much. Consider trimming to the core theoretical move (HPC reframes CMT) and its main consequence (metaphor is phylogenetically old).
- Section 4 is too long relative to its role. Consider splitting: one section for the reframing itself, one for its consequences.
- You cite your own work (Reynolds 2025a, 2025b) several times. Make sure the cited claims are at least briefly argued for in this paper rather than entirely offloaded.

### The big question you don't address

What is a "functional domain"? The entire framework hinges on the distinction between within-domain extension (literal, peripheral categorisation) and cross-domain extension (metaphorical). But you never define what makes two things belong to different functional domains. A sidewalk and a trail are in the same domain (locomotion). A painted line and a trail are in different domains (spatial demarcation vs locomotion). But why? What criterion distinguishes these? Without a principled account of functional domains, the literal/metaphorical boundary is stipulative rather than theoretically grounded. This is the paper's most significant gap.
