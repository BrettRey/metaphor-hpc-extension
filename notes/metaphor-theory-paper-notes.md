# Metaphor Theory Papers: Structured Notes for HPC Reframing of CMT

## 1) Murphy (1996) - *On metaphoric representation* (Cognition 60:173-204)

### Core argument (2-3 sentences)
Murphy evaluates two versions of conceptual metaphoric representation (a strong version and a weak version) associated with Lakoff and Johnson. He argues the strong version is theoretically incoherent and that the weak version is at best plausible but empirically under-supported. He proposes that much of the evidence can be handled by structural similarity (especially relational similarity) without positing that one domain literally structures another.

### HPC-reframing-relevant claims
- Partial mapping vs wholesale transfer: Supports partial mapping. Murphy repeatedly argues that only selected, salient relational correspondences project; whole-domain transfer is implausible.
- Comparison vs categorization: Mostly supports metaphor-as-comparison/relational alignment, not metaphor-as-categorization.
- What makes metaphors succeed/fail: Success depends on coherent relational correspondences; failure arises when theories predict uncontrolled inheritance or require ad hoc interpretation.
- Mechanism/maintenance/stability: Challenges CMT for lacking a clear causal learning/maintenance model and for relying too heavily on linguistic evidence alone.

### Quotable passages
> "It is argued that the first interpretation is not fully coherent." (Murphy, 1996, p. 173)

> "Thus, I conclude that the strong view of metaphoric representation is not theoretically coherent and should be rejected." (Murphy, 1996, p. 182)

> "It is worth reemphasizing that it is the structure of the concepts, rather than superficial properties, that are similar on this account." (Murphy, 1996, p. 196)

> "Second, the empirical base for the theory must be expanded beyond linguistic phenomena." (Murphy, 1996, p. 200)

### Key references to track down
- Lakoff, G., & Johnson, M. (1980). *Metaphors We Live By*.
- Lakoff, G. (1987). *Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things*.
- Lakoff, G. (1993). Conceptual metaphor chapter in *Metaphor and Thought*.
- Kovecses, Z. (1986). *Metaphors of Anger, Pride, and Love*.
- Gentner, D. (1983). Structure-mapping theory.
- Falkenhainer, Forbus, & Gentner (1989). SME model.
- Markman & Gentner (1993). Structural alignment in similarity.

### Connection to the HPC reframing argument
Murphy strongly supports your anti-wholesale-transfer move: metaphor works via constrained relational overlap, not total projection from source to target. He also provides a direct opening for HPC: if CMT lacks explicit causal maintenance mechanisms, HPC can supply the missing stabilizing story (how some cross-domain mappings become persistent and projectible while others remain local or fail).

## 2) Gentner (1983) - *Structure-mapping: A theoretical framework for analogy* (Cognitive Science 7:155-170)

### Core argument (2-3 sentences)
Gentner argues analogy is not based on overall feature overlap but on relational structure mapping between base and target domains. The theory prioritizes relations over attributes and introduces the systematicity principle: interconnected higher-order relational systems are preferred over isolated matches. This yields explicit rules that distinguish analogy from literal similarity, abstraction, and mere appearance matches.

### HPC-reframing-relevant claims
- Partial mapping vs wholesale transfer: Strongly supports partial mapping; analogy preserves selected relational systems and discards many object attributes.
- Comparison vs categorization: Supports metaphor-as-comparison for many metaphors (especially relational metaphors), while acknowledging attribute-based metaphor cases.
- What makes metaphors succeed/fail: Success depends on relational coherence and higher-order constraint structure; non-systematic matches are weak.
- Mechanism/maintenance/stability: Provides a cognitive/computational mechanism for mapping, but not a social-temporal stabilization account.

### Quotable passages
> "The strength of an analogical match does not seem to depend on the overall degree of featural overlap." (Gentner, 1983, p. 156)

> "Many (perhaps most) metaphors are predominantly relational comparisons, and are thus essentially analogies." (Gentner, 1983, p. 163)

> "The structure-mapping theory describes the implicit interpretation rules of analogy... analogy is characterized by the mapping of relations between objects, rather than attributes of objects, from base to target." (Gentner, 1983, p. 168)

### Key references to track down
- Tversky, A. (1977). Features of similarity.
- Ortony, A. (1979). Beyond literal similarity.
- Lakoff & Johnson (1980). *Metaphors We Live By*.
- Gentner & Gentner (1983). Flowing waters/teeming crowds.
- Gick & Holyoak (1980, 1983). Analogical problem solving/transfer.
- Winston (1980, 1981). Learning and reasoning by analogy.

### Connection to the HPC reframing argument
This is foundational for your "cross-domain HPC extension on partial overlap" claim. Gentner gives the mapping constraint machinery (relational selectivity + systematicity), which helps explain why only certain property clusters carry over. HPC can then extend this by adding why some mapped structures become stably maintained kinds/categories over time.

## 3) Bowdle & Gentner (2005) - *The career of metaphor* (Psychological Review 112:193-216)

### Core argument (2-3 sentences)
Bowdle and Gentner propose that metaphor processing changes with conventionalization: novel metaphors are processed as comparisons, whereas conventional metaphors increasingly behave like categorizations. They unify comparison and categorization by treating metaphor as initially structure-mapping-based, with repeated use yielding lexicalized abstract relational categories (polysemy). Their experiments are presented as evidence for this processing shift and associated form preferences (simile/metaphor).

### HPC-reframing-relevant claims
- Partial mapping vs wholesale transfer: Supports constrained relational mapping and selective projection from base to target.
- Comparison vs categorization: Explicitly distinguishes both and proposes a diachronic shift between them.
- What makes metaphors succeed/fail: Metaphors stabilize when repeated alignments induce coherent abstract schemas; dead metaphors are endpoint cases.
- Mechanism/maintenance/stability: Strongest of the four papers on stabilization; conventionalization and lexicalization function as maintenance mechanisms.

### Quotable passages
> "This hypothesis-the career of metaphor-postulates a shift in mode of mapping from comparison to categorization as metaphors are conventionalized." (Bowdle & Gentner, 2005, p. 193)

> "Novel metaphors are therefore interpreted as comparisons... In contrast, conventional metaphors... may therefore be interpreted either as comparisons... or as categorizations." (Bowdle & Gentner, 2005, p. 199)

> "As metaphors become increasingly conventional, there will be a shift in mode of alignment from comparison to categorization." (Bowdle & Gentner, 2005, p. 199)

> "Our results provide converging evidence for the claim that there is a shift in mode of alignment from comparison to categorization as metaphors are conventionalized." (Bowdle & Gentner, 2005, p. 208)

### Key references to track down
- Gentner (1983). Structure-mapping theory.
- Falkenhainer, Forbus, & Gentner (1989). SME.
- Glucksberg & Keysar (1990). Metaphor as categorization.
- Glucksberg, McGlone, & Manfredi (1997). Interactive property attribution.
- Kintsch (2000). Predication model.
- Giora (1997). Conventionality/salience.
- Turner & Katz (1997). Sense creation/retrieval.

### Connection to the HPC reframing argument
This paper is especially useful for your "stability mechanisms CMT lacks" thesis. It gives a concrete stabilization pathway: repeated cross-domain alignments -> abstract relational schema induction -> lexicalized, reusable categories. In HPC terms, this looks like local homeostasis of a metaphorical property cluster through usage history.

## 4) Holyoak & Stamenkovic (2018) - *Metaphor comprehension: A critical review of theories and evidence* (Psychological Bulletin 144:641-671)

### Core argument (2-3 sentences)
This review critically evaluates analogy, categorization, and conceptual-mapping approaches to metaphor using behavioral and neuro evidence. It concludes simple nominal metaphors are usually handled as category statements, while structurally richer metaphors often require analogy-like processes. It also argues evidence is weak for strong conceptual-mapping claims (especially mandatory online activation of deep mappings in conventional language) and calls for integrated multi-mechanism accounts.

### HPC-reframing-relevant claims
- Partial mapping vs wholesale transfer: Supports selective/conditional mapping; rejects strong always-on deep mapping claims.
- Comparison vs categorization: Strongly distinguishes them by task/metaphor complexity (simple vs complex forms).
- What makes metaphors succeed/fail: Aptness is often a better predictor than conventionality for processing ease/form preference; complexity matters.
- Mechanism/maintenance/stability: Emphasizes plural mechanisms and notes conventionalization effects, but challenges a simple analogy->categorization career story as universally supported.

### Quotable passages
> "Simple metaphors are generally interpreted as category statements, but more complex metaphors are likely to be treated as analogies." (Holyoak & Stamenkovic, 2018, p. 641)

> "In summary, the findings from this line of investigation call the career-of-metaphor hypothesis into serious question... there seems to be no compelling evidence that such differences involve a shift from analogy to categorization." (Holyoak & Stamenkovic, 2018, p. 652)

> "In the running debate between the analogy and categorization positions, we find a clear advantage for the latter [for simple nominal metaphors]." (Holyoak & Stamenkovic, 2018, p. 656)

> "Without analogy, it is possible to go a considerable distance toward understanding metaphor, but that road ends well short of the intended destination." (Holyoak & Stamenkovic, 2018, p. 657)

### Key references to track down
- Glucksberg & Keysar (1990). Categorization account.
- Kintsch (2000, 2001). Predication/contextual shading.
- Bowdle & Gentner (2005). Career of metaphor.
- Gentner, Bowdle, Wolff, & Boronat (2001). Mapping-consistency effects.
- Keysar et al. (2000). Consistency effects for novel metaphors.
- Lakoff & Johnson (1980, 1999). Conceptual mapping/embodiment.
- Desai et al. (2011); Cardillo et al. (2012). Neural evidence relevant to conventionalization claims.

### Connection to the HPC reframing argument
This review supports a plural, mechanism-sensitive HPC reframing: no single process explains all metaphors. For your argument, it strengthens the claim that stability and success are conditional (aptness, complexity, conventionalization history), which is exactly the kind of heterogeneity an HPC account should model rather than suppress.

## Cross-paper synthesis for the HPC reframing thesis
- Shared support for your key move: metaphor interpretation is constrained and partial, not wholesale transfer from source domain.
- Strongest support for an explicit stability story comes from Bowdle & Gentner (conventionalization/lexicalization); Murphy explicitly says CMT-style claims need stronger causal/psychological modeling.
- Holyoak & Stamenkovic temper the strongest career claim and push a mixed-process view, which fits HPC: stability should be treated as graded and mechanism-dependent, not universal.
- Net implication for your paper: frame metaphor as cross-domain HPC extension where projection succeeds when relational overlap is coherent and socially/cognitively stabilized over time; fails when overlap is thin, non-systematic, or unstabilized.
