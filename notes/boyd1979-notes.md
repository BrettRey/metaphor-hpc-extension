# Boyd (1979): Metaphor and Theory Change

**Full citation:** Boyd, Richard. 1979. Metaphor and theory change: What is "metaphor" a metaphor for? In Andrew Ortony (ed.), *Metaphor and Thought*, 481–532. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.

---

## Core Argument

Boyd argues that **theory-constitutive metaphors** (TCMs) play an essential, irreplaceable role in scientific theory construction by introducing terminology for natural kinds whose properties are not yet fully understood. Unlike literary metaphors (which convey pre-existing knowledge) or pedagogical metaphors (which explain established theories), TCMs accomplish **nondefinitional reference fixing** through accommodation of language to the causal structure of the world. Metaphors succeed not by explicit similarity/analogy specification, but by providing **epistemic access** to natural kinds through an open-ended process of discovery that refines both terminology and theoretical understanding over time.

---

## Key Theoretical Claims

### 1. Theory-Constitutive Metaphors Defined

**What they are:**
- Metaphorical expressions that constitute "an irreplaceable part of the linguistic machinery of a scientific theory" for expressing theoretical claims with no adequate literal paraphrase (p. 482)
- Used to introduce theoretical terminology where none previously existed (p. 482)
- Cases where "metaphorical expressions use in expressing theoretical claims for which no adequate literal paraphrase is known" (p. 486)

**Canonical examples from cognitive psychology:**
- Thought as "information processing," brain as "computer"
- Mental states as "preprogrammed" vs. acquired through learning
- Memory as having "storage," "indexing," "retrieval"
- Consciousness as "feedback"
- Learning as response of a "self-organizing machine" (pp. 486–487)

**Key distinction:** TCMs are **conceits**, not metaphors in the literary sense. They extend "not through one literary work, but through the work of a generation or more of scientists" and become "the property of the entire scientific community" (pp. 487–488).

### 2. Accommodation to Causal Structure

**The central mechanism (p. 483):**
> "More precisely, what I shall argue is that the use of metaphor is one of many devices available to the scientific community to accomplish the task of *accommodation of language to the causal structure of the world*. By this I mean the task of introducing terminology, and modifying usage of existing terminology, so that linguistic categories are available which describe the causally and explanatorily significant features of the world."

**Why accommodation matters:**
- Language must "cut the world at its joints" – but the "joint" metaphor itself is misleading because it suggests joints are context-free
- The relevant notion of "joint" is context- or discipline-relative (p. 483)
- Accommodation is necessary because we cannot specify natural kind definitions a priori; we must discover causal structure empirically

**Connection to HPC kinds (pp. 483–484):**
Boyd explicitly connects TCMs to his own earlier work on natural kind terms and homeostatic property cluster kinds. He argues that:
- Natural kind terms refer to kinds whose "real essences consist of complex relational properties, rather than features or internal constitution" (p. 484)
- Metaphor serves as "a nondefinitional mode of reference fixing which is especially well suited to the introduction of terms referring to kinds whose real essences consist of complex relational properties" (p. 484)

### 3. Catachresis (Filling Vocabulary Gaps)

**Black's term:** Catachresis = "the use of metaphor to remedy gaps in vocabulary" (p. 481)

**Boyd's position:**
- Agrees that TCMs fill vocabulary gaps but argues this is NOT a "substitute for formal comparison" – it has "its own distinctive capacities and merits" (p. 482)
- TCMs possess "several (though not all) of the characteristics which Black attributes to interaction metaphors; in particular, their success does not depend on their conveying quite specific respects of similarity or analogy" (p. 482)
- The inability to specify similarity/analogy is NOT a defect – it reflects "the necessity of obtaining a *priori* between scientific language and a messy and complex world" (p. 483)

### 4. Inductive Open-Endedness

**The programmatic character of TCMs (pp. 488–489):**
- TCMs display "inductive open-endedness" – they invite exploration of similarities between primary and secondary subjects, including features "not yet discovered, or not yet fully understood" (p. 488)
- The reader is invited to explore similarities "including features not yet discovered, or not yet fully understood. This programmatic research-orienting feature of theory-constitutive metaphors explains the ways in which such metaphors both resemble and differ from ordinary interaction metaphors" (pp. 488–489)
- TCMs are "invitations to future research" – their function is to put researchers "on the track of these respects of similarity or analogy" (p. 489)

**Contrast with literary metaphors:**
- Literary metaphors work by "inductive open-endedness" in the sense that the reader applies current understanding to discover implications
- TCMs work by inductive open-endedness in the sense that they **"invite the discovery of new features of the world delineated in terms of those – perhaps as yet undiscovered – similarities and analogies"** (p. 489)

**Key quotation (p. 490):**
> "Theory-constitutive metaphors, in other words, represent one strategy for the accommodation of language to as yet undiscovered causal features of the world."

### 5. What Makes Metaphors Succeed or Fail?

**The epistemic-access account of success:**

Boyd develops a sophisticated account where metaphor success depends on whether the metaphorical term provides **epistemic access** to natural kinds. Success is NOT about:
- Speaker intentions (p. 508)
- Explicit definitional paraphrase
- Fixed similarity/analogy specifications

**Success conditions (pp. 519–520):**
> "Reference is continuous if the term in question continues to provide epistemic access to the same kind, or if appropriate episodes of denotational refinement take place. Similarly, several roughly simultaneous uses of the same term are coreferential if they are embodied in patterns of usage which afford epistemic access to the same kind(s)."

**For TCMs specifically (p. 520):**
Metaphors succeed when:
1. There exists "a relatively small number of kinds to which their constituent terms afford epistemic access"
2. Each constituent metaphorical expression "affords epistemic access to at most a small number of kinds"
3. In cases where metaphorical expressions refer, "all or most of their constituent metaphors... have the same referent (or approximately the same set of partial denotata) in each of its applications within the relevant theoretical context"

**Why TCMs can't be fully explicated (p. 525):**
> "I have argued that there is no *a priori* reason to suppose that a theory-constitutive metaphor will forever escape complete explication, but should we expect that such an explication will typically be the fate of a theory-constitutive metaphor if things go well scientifically[?]"

Boyd's answer: Sometimes yes, sometimes no – depends on whether the phenomenon referred to is itself an HPC kind (see section 6 below).

### 6. Mechanism, Maintenance, and Stability

**The HPC connection (explicit in final section):**

Boyd argues that many TCMs **refer to homeostatic property cluster phenomena** and therefore resist complete literal explication not as a defect but as an accurate reflection of the structure of natural kinds.

**Ten features of HPC natural kind terms (pp. 526–527, numbered in original):**

1. **Family of clustered properties:** There is a family F of properties contingently clustered in nature
2. **Homeostatic co-occurrence:** Their co-occurrence is "the result of what may be metaphorically (sometimes literally) described as a sort of *homeostasis*" – mechanisms favor presence of some properties given others, or maintain presence together
3. **Causal importance:** "The homeostatic clustering of the properties in F is causally important: there are (theoretically or practically) important effects which are produced by a conjoint occurrence of (many of) the properties in F together with (some or all of) the underlying mechanisms in question"
4. **Kind term application:** There is a kind term *t* applied to things in which the homeostatic clustering of most properties in F occurs
5. **Natural definition:** "It is an analytic definition; rather all or most of the homeostatic cluster F together with some or all of the mechanisms that underlie it provide the natural definition of *t*"
6. **Imperfect homeostasis:** "Homeostasis is nomologically possible or actual: some thing may display some but not all of the properties in F; some but not all of the relevant underlying homeostatic mechanisms may be present"
7. **Relative importance varies:** The relative importance of properties and mechanisms in determining kind membership "is a theoretical rather than a conceptual issue"
8. **Extensional vagueness:** "There will be many cases of extensional vagueness that are not resolvable even given all the relevant facts and all the true theories"
9. **Causal importance of clustering:** The clustering F + mechanisms together "is such that the kind or property denoted by *t* is a natural kind reference important for scientific explanation or for the formulation of successful inductive inferences"
10. **No refinement preserves naturalness:** No refinement replacing *t* with a more extensionally precise term "will preserve the naturalness of the kind referred to"

**The historicity claim (p. 527):**
> "The homeostatic property cluster which serves to define *t* is not individuated extensionally. Instead, the property cluster is an individuated like a (type or token) historical object or process: certain changes over time (or in space) in the property cluster or in the underlying homeostatic mechanisms preserve the identity of the defining cluster."

This means **"*t* continues to have the same definition"** even as properties vary over time/space – historicity is essential to the naturalness of HPC kinds.

**Application to metaphor (pp. 528–529):**

When TCMs refer to HPC phenomena:
- The metaphor **cannot be replaced** by literal definition without loss, because the phenomenon itself resists precise characterization
- The "vagueness" of the metaphor is NOT imprecision but **accurate reflection** of the causal structure being described
- "Vagueness in extension is by no means always indicative of any imprecision at all. For some kinds, 'vagueness' in the application of the associated terminology is precisely indicative of *precision* in the accommodation of language to the actual structure of the world" (p. 530)

**Key quotation (p. 529):**
> "Where a theory-constitutive metaphor (or any other expression) refers to such a phenomenon, there is no reason in general to believe that such an explication is even possible. The properties that constitute the homeostatic property cluster may not even be finite in number and they may vary significantly from time to time or place to place, so that a finite characterization of them (much less a cognitively tractable characterization) need not be possible."

This is the **anti-empiricist punch line**: we cannot have the "sorts of definitions empiricists envisioned as essential for scientific investigation" (p. 529) precisely because natural kinds are HPC phenomena, not essentialist categories.

## Quotable Passages (with page numbers)

### On accommodation

**p. 483:**
> "the use of metaphor is one of many devices available to the scientific community to accomplish the task of *accommodation of language to the causal structure of the world*. By this I mean the task of introducing terminology, and modifying usage of existing terminology, so that linguistic categories are available which describe the causally and explanatorily significant features of the world."

**p. 490:**
> "Theory-constitutive metaphors, in other words, represent one strategy for the accommodation of language to as yet undiscovered causal features of the world."

**p. 510:**
> "What I am calling 'accommodation' is a response to an epistemological problem, whose discovery represents the principal epistemological achievement of early empiricism: inductive generalization is reliable (or, at any rate, is nonaccidentally reliable) only if the categories in terms of which generalizations are formulated correspond in the right way to the causal powers of things in the material world."

### On open-endedness and discovery

**pp. 488–489:**
> "The function of such metaphors is to put us on the track of these respects of similarity or analogy; indeed, the employment of terms in such metaphors may best be understood as contributing to features of the world delineated in terms of those – perhaps as yet undiscovered – similarities and analogies. Thus, it is hardly surprising that, at least for a time, it is not known exactly what the relevant respects of similarity or analogy are; many have yet to be discovered or understood."

**p. 489:**
> "Precisely because theory-constitutive metaphors are invitations to future research, and because that research is aimed at uncovering the theoretically important similarities between the primary and secondary subjects of the metaphors, the explication of these similarities and analogies is the routine business of scientific researchers, rather than of some specialized body of commentators."

### On epistemic access

**p. 503:**
> "The central task of a theory of reference is to explain the role of language in the acquisition, assessment, improvement, and communication of knowledge, especially the role of language in making possible social cooperation and rational deliberation within these activities. *What is to be explained is our (collective) capacity to successfully detect and discover facts about the world*."

**pp. 504–505:**
> "The accommodation of our language and conceptual categories to the causal structure of the world ('cutting the world at its joints') is essential in order that tolerance be possible. Since – in the absence of perfect causal knowledge – such accommodation cannot be accomplished by explicit and conventional definitions, nondefinitional procedures for accommodating language to the world are essential to knowledge. Since knowledge gathering is the essential core of reference, *the processes of linguistic accommodation are essential components of reference*."

**p. 517:**
> "This sort of linguistically mediated epistemic success – *which necessarily includes modification of linguistic usage to accommodate language to newly discovered causal features of the world* – is the very core of reference."

### On HPC kinds and metaphor

**p. 526 (property cluster homeostasis):**
> "Their co-occurrence is, at least typically, the result of what may be metaphorically (sometimes literally) described as a sort of *homeostasis*. Either the presence of some of the properties in F (under appropriate conditions) to favor the presence of the others, or there are underlying mechanisms or processes which tend to maintain the presence of the properties in F, or both."

**p. 528:**
> "Homeostatic property cluster definitions represent a special kind of deference to the world: instead of being possible *conceptual* phenomena whose content is dictated by the causal structure of the world, they are themselves naturalistically and (perhaps) historically individuated causal phenomena *in the world*."

**p. 530:**
> "Vagueness in extension is by no means always indicative of any imprecision at all. For some kinds, 'vagueness' in the application of the associated terminology is precisely indicative of *precision* in the accommodation of language to the actual structure of the world. If, as I have suggested, many theory-constitutive metaphors refer to homeostatic property cluster phenomena, then we have an additional reason for rejecting the empiricist conception of precision for those cases."

### On reference continuity and change

**p. 504:**
> "Reference has an essential dynamic and dialectical aspect. Changes in language use – when they reflect the dialectics of accommodation – do not represent changes of reference in any philosophically puzzling sense of that term. Instead, such dialectical changes of reference are characteristic of referential continuity and represent perfectly ordinary vehicles for the reporting of new discoveries."

**pp. 516–517:**
> "The important fact is that they provided substantial and sustained epistemic access to a sufficiently small number of kinds that their use resulted in sustained increases in knowledge (and eventually in the discovery that crucial distinctions had to be drawn between those kinds to which they afford epistemic access). This sort of linguistically mediated epistemic success – *which necessarily includes modification of linguistic usage to accommodate language to newly discovered causal features of the world* – is the very core of reference."

### On why TCMs resist literal paraphrase

**p. 496:**
> "The introduction of theoretical terminology generally requires just the features that theory-constitutive metaphors provide. One way of providing a tentative and preliminary indication of the properties of presumed kinds – and of disambiguating terms referring to presumed kinds of the same general sort – is by open-ended analogy to kinds whose properties are in some respects better understood. One way of expressing such analogies is by metaphorical use of terms referring to those better understood kinds. Theory-constitutive metaphors, then, simply represent one strategy among many for the preliminary stages of theory construction."

## Connection to HPC Reframing of CMT

### What Boyd gives us that Lakoff lacks:

**1. Causal grounding vs. embodied schemas**

**Boyd's position:**
- Metaphors succeed by accommodating to **independently existing causal structure** in the world (p. 510: "the categories in terms of which generalizations are formulated correspond in the right way to the causal powers of things in the material world")
- Reference is fixed through **epistemic access** to natural kinds, which are individuated by homeostatic mechanisms, not conceptual schemas
- The world constrains which metaphors work: "we accommodate *our* language to the structure of a theory-independent world" (p. 532)

**Lakoff's position (implied contrast):**
- Metaphors express embodied image schemas (CONTAINER, PATH, etc.)
- Grounding is in bodily experience, not world structure
- No clear account of why some metaphors succeed and others fail beyond experiential fit

**HPC reframing:** Metaphors that endure do so because they **track homeostatic property clusters** in the world. Embodiment may shape *which* metaphors we try, but causal structure determines which ones support reliable inference. The "container" schema works for categories not because containment is embodied, but because many categories exhibit HPC structure (discrete boundaries maintained by homeostatic mechanisms).

**2. Stability mechanisms**

**Boyd's contribution:**
- Explicit 10-point characterization of HPC kinds (pp. 526–527), including:
  - Homeostatic mechanisms that maintain property clustering (#2)
  - Causal importance of the clustering (#3, #9)
  - Historical individuation – clusters can change over time while preserving identity (#11)
  - Imperfect homeostasis – not all members have all properties (#6)

**What Lakoff lacks:**
- No account of what **maintains** conceptual structure over time
- No explanation of why metaphorical mappings are stable across speakers/communities
- No mechanism for property clustering beyond experiential correlation

**HPC reframing:** Conceptual metaphors endure because they map between **two HPC kinds** that share overlapping mechanisms. ARGUMENT IS WAR succeeds not just because we experience arguments as adversarial, but because both argument-kinds and war-kinds exhibit:
- Competition for dominance (shared mechanism)
- Strategy/tactics (shared mechanism)
- Victory/defeat endpoints (shared mechanism)
- Alliance formation (shared in some subtypes)

The metaphor **projects reliably** because these mechanisms maintain similar property clusters in both domains.

**3. Open-ended discovery vs. fixed mappings**

**Boyd (pp. 488–489):**
- TCMs are "invitations to future research"
- Success depends on discovering **not-yet-understood** similarities
- Metaphors refine over time as causal structure is better understood
- No expectation of complete literal paraphrase for HPC phenomena

**Lakoff:**
- Conceptual metaphors have relatively **fixed mapping schemas** (TIME IS SPACE: temporal → spatial)
- Emphasis on systematicity and entailments that follow from established mappings
- Less account of how metaphors evolve with empirical discovery

**HPC reframing:** Productive metaphors exhibit **inductive open-endedness** precisely because they connect HPC kinds. As we discover more about the causal mechanisms in either domain, we discover new respects of similarity. MIND IS MACHINE was initially crude (hydraulic metaphors), became more sophisticated (computer metaphors), and continues to evolve as we learn more about both cognitive mechanisms and computational architecture. The metaphor is **projectible** because both domains are stabilized by mechanisms, not fixed definitions.

**4. Field-relative projectibility**

**Boyd (pp. 514–515, 531–532):**
- "Cutting nature at its joints" is **context-specific**: joints depend on the "linguistic community" and the "practical or theoretical projects that the relevant linguistic community undertakes" (p. 532)
- "Jade" denotes a commercial/gemological kind (legitimate for that field) even though jadeite and nephrite are chemically distinct
- The "naturalness" of a kind is relative to investigative purposes: "what natural kinds causally determine the phenomena that they have been able to predict successfully" (p. 512)

**Connection to HPC book's field-relative projectibility:**
This is **exactly** the principle developed in Ch 6–7 of the HPC book: projectibility is indexed to analytical purpose. Boyd provides the **metaphor** version of the same insight. Computer metaphors in psychology cut cognition at different joints than neuroscience does – both are legitimate for their respective explanatory projects.

**Lakoff:** No systematic treatment of field-relativity. Metaphors are presented as cognitively universal (with cultural variation in specifics).

**5. Precision through vagueness**

**Boyd's insight (p. 530):**
> "Vagueness in extension is by no means always indicative of any imprecision at all. For some kinds, 'vagueness' in the application of the associated terminology is precisely indicative of *precision* in the accommodation of language to the actual structure of the world."

**HPC reframing for CMT:**
- Lakoff treats metaphorical "highlighting and hiding" as conceptual selectivity
- Boyd reframes this: metaphors are **accurate** when they reflect HPC structure, which resists sharp boundaries
- The "vagueness" of ARGUMENT IS WAR (some arguments more war-like than others, no sharp boundary) is not imprecision – it's **fidelity to the HPC structure** of both argument-kinds and war-kinds

**6. Why metaphor is necessary (not just convenient)**

**Boyd (pp. 495–496):**
> "The introduction of theoretical terms does require, however, some tentative or preliminary indication of the properties of the presumed kinds in question. Any such terminology must possess a sort of programmatic open-endedness... Theory-constitutive metaphors, then, simply represent one strategy among many for the preliminary stages of theory construction."

Metaphor is **necessary** when:
- We need to refer to natural kinds before fully understanding their properties
- The kinds in question are HPC phenomena (no complete literal definition possible)
- We need open-ended terms that support discovery of further properties

**Lakoff:** Metaphor is pervasive in thought but not clearly *necessary* – could potentially be eliminated in favor of literal language (though Lakoff doubts this in practice).

**HPC reframing:** Metaphor is **constitutive** of thought about HPC kinds because:
1. HPC kinds resist complete literal specification (point 6 of Boyd's 10-point characterization)
2. We need to refer to and reason about such kinds before fully understanding them
3. Cross-domain mapping between HPC kinds allows property discovery through analogy

**7. Success/failure conditions**

**Boyd's epistemic-access account (pp. 519–520):**
Metaphors succeed when constituent terms afford epistemic access to a small number of kinds across different uses. Failure modes:
- Term affords access to too many unrelated kinds (ambiguity)
- No underlying similarity in causal structure (metaphor doesn't project)
- Community standards of evidence conflict (partial denotation issues)

**Lakoff:** Success/failure determined by experiential grounding and systematic mapping, but no clear **causal** explanation of why some metaphors support inference and others don't.

**HPC reframing:** Metaphors succeed when:
1. **Both domains are HPC kinds** (have homeostatic mechanisms)
2. **Mechanisms partially overlap** (shared causal structure)
3. **The overlap is causally important** for the investigative purposes at hand

Example: LANGUAGE IS ORGANISM succeeds (both have:
- Growth/development mechanisms
- Functional adaptation
- Historical lineages
- Death/extinction
)

LANGUAGE IS ROCK fails (no shared mechanisms).

## Synthesis: Boyd + HPC → CMT Reframing

**The argument:**

1. **Boyd:** Theory-constitutive metaphors succeed by accommodating language to causal structure; they refer to natural kinds (often HPC kinds) through open-ended epistemic access rather than definition.

2. **HPC theory:** Natural kinds are individuated by homeostatic mechanisms that maintain property clusters over time; these kinds resist essentialist definition but support reliable induction.

3. **CMT reframing:** Conceptual metaphors are **cross-domain HPC extensions**:
   - Source and target domains are both HPC kinds
   - Metaphor maps between domains that share **overlapping mechanisms**
   - Metaphorical entailments are **projectible** because mechanisms in the source domain predict properties in the target domain
   - Metaphors are **stable** across communities because mechanisms maintain property clusters in both domains
   - Metaphors exhibit **open-endedness** because HPC kinds support discovery of novel properties

**What this adds to Lakoff:**
- **Causal grounding:** Mechanisms, not just embodied schemas
- **Stability explanation:** Why metaphors persist (homeostasis)
- **Projectibility account:** Why metaphorical inferences are reliable (causal structure)
- **Failure conditions:** Why some metaphors don't work (no shared mechanisms)
- **Field-relativity:** Same phenomenon, different metaphors for different purposes (syntax vs. phonology vs. semantics may use different LANGUAGE IS X metaphors legitimately)

**What Lakoff lacks:**
- No account of **mechanisms** that maintain conceptual structure
- No explanation of **why** embodied schemas are stable across speakers
- No clear **success conditions** beyond experiential fit
- No treatment of **field-relative** projectibility

**The Boyd+HPC synthesis provides:**
A naturalistic, causally grounded account of metaphor that explains both:
1. **Cognitive ubiquity** (we reason about HPC kinds constantly; metaphor is necessary for reference)
2. **Inferential reliability** (metaphors project because they track shared causal mechanisms)

This is THE paper for establishing that Boyd already had the causal/mechanistic account that CMT lacks.

## Methodological Notes

**Boyd's epistemic-access account of reference (summary):**
- Reference is fundamentally an epistemological notion (p. 503)
- The referential relation is constituted by **causal connections** between term users and world features, sustained by "epistemically relevant causal connections both between users of the term and examples of its referent" (p. 503)
- Reference involves "social coordination of epistemic access" (p. 505)
- Epistemic access = the capacity of a linguistic community to detect and discover facts about the referent

**This is a causal theory of reference, but NOT Kripke's:**
- Kripke: reference fixed by dubbing/causal chains
- Boyd: reference fixed by **ongoing epistemic access** (discovery processes)
- Boyd's account is **dynamic and dialectical** (p. 504): changes in usage reflecting accommodation don't represent changes of reference but "referential continuity"

**For the HPC book:** This shows Boyd's position is deeply anti-essentialist even in 1979. Reference doesn't require definitions (necessary & sufficient conditions) – it requires mechanisms that provide epistemic access to causally unified kinds.
