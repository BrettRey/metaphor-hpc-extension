# Notes on Noyes & Keil 2019 and Lakoff & Johnson 1980

## HPC Reframing Argument

**Central claim:** Boyd says metaphors succeed by accommodating to causal structure in the world; Lakoff says they express embodied schemas. HPC adds stability mechanisms that CMT lacks. Metaphor = cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap.

## Paper 1: Noyes & Keil 2019 - "Generics designate kinds but not always essences"

### Core Argument

Generics (e.g., "men like sports") primarily designate that categories are **kinds** (richly structured categories with high inductive potential), NOT that they are essential (natural kinds with internal causal properties). The **kind assumption** and **essence assumption** are separable. Generics induce essentialism only when paired with cues (e.g., biological properties) that reasonably prompt essentialist explanation.

**Three-sentence summary:** People use generics to signal that a category supports rich inductive inferences (the "kind assumption"), but this does not require essentialist beliefs about internal natural causes (the "essence assumption"). Across four studies (n=739), generics consistently increased belief that categories are kinds, but only contingently increased essentialism when property content supported biological explanations. Social categories can be richly structured kinds without being essential.

### Key Claims Relevant to HPC Reframing of CMT

#### Non-essentialist kinds
- **Strongly supports.** Noyes & Keil directly argue that "kindhood does not imply essentialism, and essentialism is not necessary to kindhood" (p. 2).
- Kinds are heterogeneous in causal structures: tigers, oak trees, chairs, money, artists, Christians all vary in whether they are natural/social and whether they have essences (p. 2).
- Generics designate rich causal structure and high inductive potential but don't specify what TYPE of causal structure (p. 2).

#### Metaphor-as-comparison vs metaphor-as-categorization
- **Indirect support.** The paper distinguishes between:
  - **Kind assumption:** belief in rich causal structure, high inductive potential, principled property relationships (p. 1)
  - **Essence assumption:** belief category is natural kind with internal causal properties (p. 1)
- This parallels the distinction between recognizing structural similarities (comparison) vs. treating something AS a member of a category (categorization).
- Relevant quote: "Essentialism usually implies that something is a kind because essentialism usually assumes the presence of rich causal structure. But kindhood and rich structure do not imply that something is essential because kinds are heterogeneous in their causal structures" (p. 2).

#### Maintenance of category stability
- **Weak support, but suggestive.** The paper doesn't address maintenance mechanisms directly, but:
  - Generics are described as "a subtle and ordinary linguistic cue by which the kind assumption can be transmitted within a culture" (p. 5).
  - "The kind assumption induced generics, and generics induced the kind assumption" (p. 5) — a feedback loop that could stabilize category structure.
  - Cultural transmission role: "generics are a mechanism of cultural transmission" (p. 5).

#### Partial mapping, boundaries, failure conditions
- **Yes, explicitly discussed:**
  - **Partial properties:** "Even if the properties are meaningful, rich, and principled, generics do not induce essentialism on their own. Rich and principled properties can support diverse causal representations, including causal representations that are not essential" (p. 2).
  - **Property content matters:** Study 2 shows cultural properties generically stated do NOT induce essentialism (d = -0.30), while biological properties DO (d = 0.70) (p. 3).
  - **Boundaries:** Study 3 shows that with neutral properties, generics increase kind beliefs across animals (d=0.52), artifacts (d=0.42), and humans (d=1.14), but barely affect essence beliefs (all p>0.09) (pp. 3-4).
  - **Failure conditions implicitly present:** When property content doesn't support a particular causal interpretation, that interpretation fails to arise.

### Quotable Passages (with page numbers)

   > "Kindhood does not imply essentialism, and essentialism is not necessary to kindhood. 2. **On diverse kinds:**
   > "There are diverse kinds in the world, including tigers, oak trees, chairs, money, artists, and Christians. They vary considerably in their underlying causal structure, including whether they are natural or social (or both) and whether they have or lack an essence." (p. 2)

3. **On generics and flexibility:**
   > "Participants can entertain diverse causal structures in the presence of generics: Hearing that biological properties generalize to a category (e.g., 'men grow beards') prompts participants to infer essential structure, but hearing neutral or social properties ('women are underpaid') generalized prompts other causal beliefs." (Abstract, p. 1)

4. **On cultural transmission:**
   > "The kind assumption induced generics, and generics induced the kind assumption. Thus, generics are a subtle and ordinary linguistic cue by which the kind assumption can be transmitted within a culture." (p. 5)

5. **On social kinds:**
   > "Talking about social categories with generics, and even talking about social categories explicitly as kinds, does not induce essentialism at default" (p. 5)

6. **On property content:**
   > "Cultural generics had opposite effects on the kind assumption and essence assumption, b = −1.39, SE = 0.25, P < 0.001, whereas biological generics had the same effect on both the kind assumption and essence assumption, b = −0.27, SE = 0.25, P = 0.295." (p. 3)

7. **On flexibility in reasoning:**
   > "Thus, our participants did not appear to be systematically biased in the presence of generics, but were versatile reasoners: As there should be, there are diverse causal structures in the world, and diverse causal structures can generate coherent and inductively significant kinds." (p. 4)

### Connection to HPC Reframing

**This paper is GOLD for the HPC reframing argument because:**

1. **Empirically demonstrates** that people can and do recognize non-essential kinds (Study 2: cultural properties → kind assumption WITHOUT essence assumption).

2. **Property clusters without essences:** The kind assumption tracks "rich causal structure" and "principled relationships" but is agnostic about WHETHER those principles are biological, social, cultural, etc.

3. **Mechanism-neutral inductive potential:** Generics signal that properties generalize reliably, but the mechanisms maintaining that reliability can vary (biological inheritance, social transmission, functional constraints, etc.).

4. **Parallel to Boyd's HPC:** Boyd says categories succeed when they track causal structure in the world. Noyes & Keil show people recognize this causal structure (kind assumption) separately from whether the causes are internal/essential (essence assumption).

5. **Lakoff's embodiment + Boyd's causal accommodation:** Generics work because they signal BOTH that there's reliable structure (Boyd: accommodates to causal reality) AND that the structure matters for our purposes (Lakoff: experientially grounded). Property content determines WHICH causal story we tell.

6. **Stability mechanisms implicit:** Cultural transmission of generics (p. 5) could be one HPC stabilizer. The fact that generics "induce the kind assumption" creates a feedback loop: using generics makes people expect rich structure, which licenses further generic use.

## Paper 2: Lakoff & Johnson 1980 - *Metaphors We Live By* (with 2003 Afterword)

### Core Argument (Original 1980)

Metaphor is not mere linguistic ornamentation but a fundamental mechanism of human thought. Our ordinary conceptual system is "fundamentally metaphorical in nature" (p. 4). We understand and experience one domain (target) in terms of another (source) via systematic mappings. These mappings are grounded in embodied experience and structure not just language but thought and action.

**Three-sentence summary (1980):** The human conceptual system is fundamentally metaphorical: we systematically understand abstract domains (argument, time, love) in terms of concrete experiential domains (war, space, journey). These conceptual metaphors structure not just how we talk but how we think and act, and they are grounded in bodily and cultural experience. Metaphors work through partial structural mappings, highlighting some aspects while hiding others, and are organized into coherent systems.

**Three-sentence summary (2003 Afterword):** Conceptual metaphor is a neural phenomenon grounded in embodied experience: primary metaphors (e.g., Affection Is Warmth) arise from conflated experiences in childhood, with neural connections recruited between co-active brain regions. Complex metaphors are composed of primary metaphors plus cultural frames. Metaphorical mappings allow source-domain sensory-motor inferences to structure abstract target-domain reasoning, and this is inevitable given the architecture of the embodied brain.

### Key Claims Relevant to HPC Reframing of CMT

#### Non-essentialist kinds?
- **Implicit support, via partial structure.** Lakoff & Johnson emphasize that metaphorical structuring is PARTIAL: "part of a metaphorical concept does not and cannot fit" (p. 13). This opens the door to categories being PARTIALLY structured by multiple metaphors, none of which exhaustively defines the category.
- Relevant: "If it were total, one concept would actually be the other, not merely be understood in terms of it" (p. 13).
- Love is structured by MULTIPLE metaphors (Love Is A Journey, Love Is A Patient, Love Is War, etc.), none essential (p. 86).

#### Comparison vs categorization?
- **Strongly addresses, favors categorization.** Traditional view: metaphor is based on pre-existing similarity (comparison). Lakoff & Johnson: metaphor CREATES perceived similarity via cross-domain correlation in experience.
- 2003 Afterword: "Metaphor is, in general, not based on similarity... Instead, it is typically based on cross-domain correlations in our experience, which give rise to the perceived similarities between the two domains within the metaphor" (pp. 244-245).
- Example: "the persistent use of a metaphor may create perceived similarities, as when a love relationship, conceived of metaphorically as a partnership, goes awry when responsibilities and benefits are not shared equally" (p. 245).
- **This is categorization:** treating love AS a journey (acting accordingly) rather than noting that love is LIKE a journey.

#### Maintenance mechanisms?
- **YES, extensively developed in 2003 Afterword:**
  1. **Neural recruitment:** "The neural maps are learned via neural recruitment, the long-term potentiation of neurons connected to the source and target neural ensembles that are coactive during Johnson's period of conflation" (p. 256).
  2. **Primary metaphors are stable:** "This neural learning mechanism produces a stable, conventional system of primary metaphors that tend to remain in place indefinitely within the conceptual system and are independent of language" (p. 256).
  3. **Embodied grounding stabilizes:** Universal primary experiences → universal primary metaphors (p. 259).
  4. **Cultural transmission:** Metaphors are learned and transmitted socially (implicit throughout; see discussion of cultural coherence, pp. 21-24).
  5. **Inference preservation:** "Image-schema structure and image-schematic inferences seemed to be 'preserved' by metaphors" (p. 253) — structural constraints maintain coherence.

#### Partial mapping, boundaries, failure?
- **YES, central theme:**
  1. **Partial structuring:** "The point here is that the metaphorical structuring involved here is partial, not total... part of a metaphorical concept does not and cannot fit" (p. 13).
  2. **Highlighting and hiding:** Ch. 3 is titled "Metaphorical Systematicity: Highlighting and Hiding" — metaphors necessarily focus on some aspects while obscuring others (pp. 10-13).
  3. **What doesn't map:** 2003 Afterword addresses this explicitly: "Mappings tend to be partial... A source domain element is not mapped if it would produce an inference that would contradict the internal structure of the target" (p. 254).
  4. **Neural mechanism for blocking bad mappings:** "If a metaphorical link would result in a contradiction in the target domain, it will not be learned. Neurally, contradictions are mutual inhibitions. Any would-be link that would lead to a contradiction with the inherent structure of the target domain will be inhibited; thus it will never be learned" (p. 259).
  5. **Multiple metaphors, partial fit:** Love is structured by Journey, Patient, War metaphors — each highlights different aspects, none exhaustive (p. 86).

### Quotable Passages (with page numbers)

#### Original 1980 Text

1. **On conceptual metaphor (not linguistic):**
   > "The most important claim we have made so far is that metaphor is not just a matter of language, that is, of mere words. We shall argue that, on the contrary, human thought processes are largely metaphorical. This is what we mean when we say that the human conceptual system is metaphorically structured and defined." (pp. 5-6)

2. **On partial structuring:**
   > "It is important to see that the metaphorical structuring involved here is partial, not total. If it were total, one concept would actually be the other, not merely be understood in terms of it. For example, time isn't really money. If you spend your time trying to do something and it doesn't work, you can't get your time back. There are no time banks... Thus, part of a metaphorical concept does not and cannot fit." (p. 13)

3. **On highlighting and hiding:**
   > "The very systematicity that allows us to comprehend one aspect of a concept in terms of another (e.g., comprehending an aspect of arguing in terms of battle) will necessarily hide other aspects of the concept. In allowing us to focus on one aspect of a concept (e.g., the battling aspects of arguing), a metaphorical concept can keep us from focusing on other aspects of the concept that are inconsistent with that metaphor." (p. 10)

4. **On experiential gestalts:**
   > "Structuring our experience in terms of such multidimensional gestalts is what makes our experience coherent. We experience a conversation as an argument when the WAR gestalt fits our perceptions and actions in the conversation." (p. 82)

5. **On causation as experiential gestalt:**
   > "Causation is best understood as an experiential gestalt. A proper understanding of causation requires that it be viewed as a cluster of other components. But the cluster forms a gestalt—a whole that we human beings find more basic than the parts." (pp. 69-70)

6. **On prototype structure:**
   > "The twelve properties given above characterize a prototype of causation in the following sense. They recur together over and over in action after action as we go through our daily lives. We experience them as a gestalt, that is, the complex of properties occurring together is more basic to our experience than their separate occurrence. Through their constant recurrence in our everyday functioning, the category of causation emerges with this complex of properties characterizing prototypical causations." (pp. 71-72)

7. **On embodied grounding:**
   > "Orientational metaphors are not arbitrary. They have a basis in our physical and cultural experience. Though the polar oppositions up-down, in-out, etc., are physical in nature, the orientational metaphors based on them can vary from culture to culture." (p. 14)

8. **On metaphorical coherence:**
   > "When a concept is structured by more than one metaphor, the different metaphorical structurings usually fit together in a coherent fashion." (p. 86)

#### 2003 Afterword

9. **On similarity vs correlation:**
   > "Metaphor is, in general, not based on similarity, as we argued throughout this book. Instead, it is typically based on cross-domain correlations in our experience, which give rise to the perceived similarities between the two domains within the metaphor." (pp. 244-245)

10. **On neural grounding:**
    > "Metaphor is a neural phenomenon. What we have referred to as metaphorical mappings appear to be realized physically as neural maps. They constitute the neural mechanism that naturally, and inevitably, recruits sensory-motor inference for use in abstract thought." (p. 257)

11. **On primary metaphors:**
    > "Primary metaphors arise spontaneously and automatically without our being aware of them. There are hundreds of such primary conceptual metaphors, most of them learned unconsciously and automatically in childhood simply by functioning in the everyday world with a human body and brain." (pp. 257-258)

12. **On stability:**
    > "This neural learning mechanism produces a stable, conventional system of primary metaphors that tend to remain in place indefinitely within the conceptual system and are independent of language." (p. 256)

13. **On inevitability:**
    > "You don't have a choice as to whether to think metaphorically. Because metaphorical maps are part of our brains, we will think and speak metaphorically whether we want to or not." (p. 257)

14. **On partial mapping (neural account):**
    > "No overrides are necessary. The reason is this: Metaphors are learned when two experiences occur at once. If a metaphorical link would result in a contradiction in the target domain, it will not be learned. Neurally, contradictions are mutual inhibitions. Any would-be link that would lead to a contradiction with the inherent structure of the target domain will be inhibited; thus it will never be learned." (p. 259)

15. **On inference:**
    > "The heart of metaphor is inference. Conceptual metaphor allows inferences in sensory-motor domains (e.g., domains of space and objects) to be used to draw inferences about other domains (e.g., domains of subjective judgment, with concepts like intimacy, emotions, justice, and so on)." (p. 244)

### Connection to HPC Reframing

**Lakoff & Johnson 1980/2003 provides MASSIVE support for HPC reframing:**

1. **Metaphor = cross-domain HPC extension:**
   - L&J: metaphor maps property clusters from source to target domain
   - HPC: categories are property clusters held together by mechanisms
   - **Synthesis:** Metaphorical extension works when source and target share enough properties (partial overlap) and when mechanisms (neural recruitment, cultural transmission) stabilize the mapping.

2. **Accommodation to causal structure (Boyd):**
   - 2003 Afterword: "cross-domain correlations in our experience" ground metaphors (p. 245)
   - Metaphors succeed when they map onto REAL patterns: "Which metaphors we have and what they mean depend on the nature of our bodies, our interactions in the physical environment, and our social and cultural practices" (p. 247)
   - This IS Boyd's accommodation: metaphors work when they track actual regularities.

3. **Embodied schemas (Lakoff) + HPC mechanisms:**
   - Primary metaphors arise from embodied experience (Affection Is Warmth from being held)
   - These are STABILIZED by neural recruitment: "Neurons that fire together wire together" (p. 256)
   - **HPC parallel:** Experiential gestalts = property clusters; neural recruitment = homeostatic mechanism.

4. **Partial mapping = HPC boundary conditions:**
   - "Part of a metaphorical concept does not and cannot fit" (p. 13)
   - "Mappings tend to be partial" (p. 254)
   - **HPC:** Categories have fuzzy boundaries; tokens at the periphery share fewer properties.
   - **Metaphor:** Mappings preserve core structure but fail at periphery (TIME IS MONEY doesn't map diamonds: "if I give you a diamond, I no longer have it" / p. 254).

5. **Multiple metaphors = multiple stabilizing mechanisms:**
   - Love structured by Journey, War, Patient metaphors (p. 86)
   - **HPC:** Categories maintained by multiple mechanisms (acquisition, entrenchment, interactive alignment)
   - Each metaphor highlights different causal aspect; no single essence.

6. **Experiential gestalts = HPCs:**
   - Causation as "experiential gestalt... a cluster of other components... a whole that we human beings find more basic than the parts" (pp. 69-70)
   - This is EXACTLY Boyd's HPC: property cluster (12 properties of prototypical causation), emergent unity (gestalt), family resemblance structure (non-prototypical causation shares sufficient properties).

7. **Stability mechanisms explicitly addressed (2003):**
   - Neural recruitment (p. 256)
   - Embodied grounding (universal experiences → universal metaphors / p. 259)
   - Cultural transmission (metaphors shared within communities)
   - Inference preservation (image-schema structure maintained / p. 253)
   - **All are HPC homeostatic mechanisms!**

8. **Failure modes:**
   - Highlighting and hiding (Ch. 3): metaphors that obscure important structure fail
   - Contradictions block mapping (neural inhibition / p. 259)
   - **HPC parallel:** Categories fail when mechanisms don't maintain coherence (Chapter 8: thin clustering, fat clustering, negative diagnosis).

## Synthesis: HPC Reframes CMT

### The Argument in Full

**Boyd (1991, 1999):** Metaphors succeed by accommodating to causal structure in the world. Scientific metaphors work when they carve nature at its joints — when the cross-domain mapping tracks real regularities.

**Lakoff & Johnson (1980, 2003):** Metaphors express embodied experiential schemas. They arise from correlations in bodily experience and are grounded in sensory-motor systems. Primary metaphors (Affection Is Warmth) combine to form complex metaphors (Love).

**Gap:** Boyd explains WHY metaphors succeed (they track causal structure) but not HOW they're maintained. Lakoff explains HOW metaphors arise (embodied experience, neural recruitment) but underspecifies what makes mappings STABLE vs EPHEMERAL.

**HPC synthesis:** Metaphor = cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap, stabilized by homeostatic mechanisms.

1. **Metaphors are HPCs:**
   - **Property cluster:** A metaphor maps a cluster of properties from source to target (not all properties — partial mapping).
   - **Mechanisms maintain the cluster:** Neural recruitment (L&J 2003), cultural transmission (Noyes & Keil 2019), inference preservation (L&J 1980), experiential grounding (L&J 1980).
   - **Projectibility:** The mapping supports inductive inference in the target domain (L&J's "inference is the heart of metaphor" / 2003, p. 244).

2. **Boyd's causal accommodation = HPC grounding condition:**
   - Metaphors work when "cross-domain correlations in our experience" (L&J 2003, p. 245) reflect real patterns.
   - Example: Time Is Money works in industrial societies because time-for-wage exchange is a recurring causal regularity.
   - Example: Affection Is Warmth works universally because warmth-affection correlation is grounded in parent-infant contact.

3. **Lakoff's embodiment = one class of HPC mechanisms:**
   - Primary metaphors arise from conflated embodied experiences → neural recruitment → stable mappings.
   - This is a HOMEOSTATIC MECHANISM: co-activation during childhood → long-term potentiation → maintained neural connections.
   - But not the only mechanism: cultural practices also stabilize (e.g., Time Is Money maintained by wage-labor institutions).

4. **Partial mapping = HPC boundary behavior:**
   - Not all source properties map to target (L&J: "mappings tend to be partial" / 2003, p. 254).
   - HPC: peripheral tokens share fewer properties; categories have fuzzy boundaries.
   - Metaphor: core inference structure preserves (image schemas), peripheral details don't (paint on a building doesn't map to theories).

5. **Multiple metaphors = multiple stabilizers:**
   - Love = Journey + War + Patient + ... (L&J 1980, p. 86)
   - Each highlights different aspect; none exhaustive.
   - HPC: categories maintained by multiple mechanisms; no single "essence."
   - Noyes & Keil: kinds (rich inductive structure) don't require essences (internal natural causes).

6. **Failure modes:**
   - Metaphors fail when they contradict target-domain structure (neural inhibition blocks learning / L&J 2003, p. 259).
   - Metaphors fail when they hide critical structure (L&J 1980, Ch. 3: highlighting and hiding).
   - HPC parallel: thin clustering (insufficient property overlap), fat clustering (spurious groupings), negative diagnosis (residual category).

### Implications

1. **Metaphor is not comparison but categorization:** We don't note similarity; we TREAT target AS source, acting accordingly. This creates new causal structure in the target domain (L&J: "the persistent use of a metaphor may create perceived similarities" / 2003, p. 245).

2. **Metaphors are maintained, not just used:** HPC framework makes maintenance mechanisms explicit. Metaphors persist because neural recruitment + cultural transmission + experiential grounding stabilize the mappings.

3. **Non-essentialist metaphors:** Noyes & Keil show people recognize non-essential kinds. Metaphorically structured categories (Love, Time, Argument) have rich structure without essences — exactly what HPC predicts.

4. **Projectibility is field-relative:** Just as definiteness (semantic) ≠ deitality (structural), metaphor mappings preserve structure relevant to the domain's purposes. Journey metaphor for arguments highlights goal-directedness; Container metaphor highlights content. Each projectible for different analytical purposes.

5. **Metaphor extends HPC categories:** When we understand love via the Journey metaphor, we're extending the Journey HPC (with its property cluster: path, destination, companions, obstacles, progress) to a new domain. The extension succeeds when (a) enough properties map and (b) mechanisms stabilize the new categorization.

## Open Questions / Next Steps

1. **How does metaphorical extension differ from literal HPC formation?**
   - L&J: primary metaphors learned via conflation; complex metaphors composed.
   - HPC: categories emerge from recurring property co-occurrence.
   - Connection: Both involve detecting reliable property clusters. Metaphor maps existing cluster to new domain; literal HPC formation detects cluster in single domain?

2. **Can metaphors be "thin" HPCs?**
   - Some metaphors feel ephemeral (Inflation Is An Entity — used for reference but not deeply entrenched).
   - Others feel robust (Time Is Money — shapes economic practices, legal codes).
   - HPC diagnosis: thin clustering = weak mechanisms, fat clustering = spurious groupings?

3. **What maintains BAD metaphors?**
   - L&J: metaphors can be coherent but misleading (Conduit metaphor hides context-dependence of meaning).
   - HPC: homeostatic mechanisms can maintain maladaptive categories if feedback is slow/weak.
   - Example: "Drugs Are War" metaphor (Lakoff's politics work) maintained by institutional practices despite poor outcomes.

4. **Does the neural recruitment account generalize beyond primary metaphors?**
   - L&J 2003: primary metaphors are universal (embodied grounding).
   - Complex metaphors are cultural (composed of primaries + cultural frames).
   - HPC: some mechanisms universal (acquisition, entrenchment), others cultural (interactive alignment, institutional practices).
   - Open: How do cultural mechanisms stabilize complex metaphors? Is it just repeated use, or are there institutional/normative mechanisms?

5. **How does this connect to Peirce's interpretant/habit framework?**
   - L&J's "experiential gestalt" resonates with Peirce's "habit."
   - L&J's "cross-domain correlation in experience" could be Peircean mediation (not brute correlation but interpreted relation).
   - Metaphor = new interpretant for a sign? (e.g., "argument" sign → WAR interpretant generates combat-related projections)
   - Needs development.
