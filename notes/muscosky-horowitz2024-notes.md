# Muscosky & Horowitz (2024) -- Structured Notes

**Full citation:** Muscosky, L., & Horowitz, A. (2024). Distinguishing doors and floors on all fours: Landmarks as tools for vertical navigation learning in domestic dogs (*Canis familiaris*). *Animals*, *14*, 3316. https://doi.org/10.3390/ani14223316

**Read from:** `/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/metaphor-hpc-extension/literature/Brauer-et-al-2024-vertical-navigation-landmarks-dogs.pdf`

**Purpose of these notes:** Extract findings bearing on reviewer challenges to the TRAIL paper, especially: (1) Horowitz's demand for more than anecdotal evidence that dogs use visual spatial cues, (2) Keil's question about whether TRAIL is a genuine functional category or perceptual generalisation, (3) Multiple reviewers asking for evidence dogs integrate visual landmarks into navigational schemas, and (4) Godfrey-Smith's question about minimal cognitive prerequisites for cross-domain extension.

---

## Study Design

- **Subjects:** 29 pet dogs (12 M, 17 F), aged 6 months to 12 years, all living in Manhattan apartment buildings for at least 4 months (p. 2).
- **Starting point:** All selected dogs showed the "wrong door" phenomenon: approaching the same apartment-line door on the wrong floor as though it were their home floor (p. 2). This establishes a baseline failure to distinguish floors vertically.
- **Conditions:** Dogs randomly assigned to visual landmark (n = 15: umbrella stand or silver streamers near/on home door) or olfactory landmark (n = 14: unscented dryer sheet in metal canister on door frame) (p. 5).
- **Procedure:** Two visits, 3--7 days apart. Visit 1: baseline behaviour at wrong door and home door (no landmark). Landmark introduced after baseline. Visit 2: same procedure with landmark present on home floor. Dogs had a mean of 16 exposures to the landmark between visits (range 10--31) (p. 4).
- **Measure:** Approach latency (seconds from elevator to door), approach rate, and exploratory behaviours (tracking, sniffing, looking at owner, moving away), all coded from GoPro video frame-by-frame (p. 5).
- **Analysis:** Wilcoxon signed rank tests (non-parametric) for latency; chi-square for approach rates (p. 5).

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Baseline -- dogs do NOT distinguish floors

**Exact result:** No significant difference in approach latency between home door (mean = 9.98 s) and wrong door (mean = 12.05 s) at baseline (n = 18, Wilcoxon Z = -0.74, p = 0.46) (p. 6, Table 3).

**Page:** 6

**Bearing on reviewer challenges:**
- **Horowitz:** This is Horowitz's own lab establishing, with controlled video-coded data, that dogs default to treating visually identical corridors as interchangeable. The "wrong door" phenomenon is not anecdotal here; it is experimentally confirmed.
- **Keil:** The baseline confirms that floor identity is NOT distinguished by default perceptual mechanisms. Whatever discrimination emerges post-landmark is learned, not pre-existing perceptual generalisation.
- **Multiple reviewers (visual landmarks):** Establishes the pre-intervention state: dogs navigate these environments without differentiating floors, despite available visual and spatial cues.

### Finding 2: Post-landmark -- dogs differentiate floors by latency

**Exact result:** Significant difference in approach latency between home door (mean = 12.28 s) and wrong door (mean = 18.48 s) in the test trial (n = 18, Wilcoxon Z = -2.16, p = 0.03) (p. 6, Table 3).

**Page:** 6

**Bearing on reviewer challenges:**
- **Horowitz:** This is controlled experimental evidence from Horowitz's own lab that the introduction of a landmark changes dogs' navigational behaviour. The effect is measured, not anecdotal. It directly addresses the demand for systematic rather than anecdotal evidence of dogs using spatial cues.
- **Keil:** The change from no discrimination to discrimination after landmark exposure is a learning effect, not perceptual generalisation. The dog did not generalise from pre-existing perceptual similarity; it *learned* to use a novel cue to differentiate previously confusable environments.
- **Multiple reviewers:** Direct experimental evidence that dogs can integrate a landmark (visual or olfactory) into a navigational schema to solve a spatial problem they could not previously solve.

### Finding 3: Post-landmark -- approach rate diverges

**Exact result:** No significant difference in approach rate between home (86%) and wrong (73%) floors at baseline (chi-square p = 0.31). Significant difference in approach rate between home (90%) and wrong (66%) floors at test (chi-square p = 0.03) (p. 6).

**Page:** 6

**Bearing on reviewer challenges:**
- **Keil:** This is a categorical behavioural change, not a graded perceptual effect. Dogs increasingly *avoid* approaching the wrong door post-landmark. Avoidance of a previously approached door suggests a re-evaluation of the spatial context, not merely a shift in perceptual salience.
- **Godfrey-Smith:** The approach-rate shift is important for the minimal-prerequisites question. Dogs are not just faster/slower; some dogs stop approaching the wrong door altogether. This is a flexible, context-sensitive adjustment of navigational behaviour -- not a fixed response pattern.

### Finding 4: Visual vs. olfactory landmark comparison

**Exact results:**
- **Olfactory baseline:** Significant difference between home (mean = 6.92 s) and wrong (mean = 9.66 s), Z = -1.42, p = 0.03 (p. 8, Table 3).
- **Olfactory test:** Significant difference between home (mean = 12.31 s) and wrong (mean = 19.24 s), Z = -1.72, p = 0.02 (p. 8, Table 3).
- **Visual baseline:** No significant difference between home (mean = 13.03 s) and wrong (mean = 14.44 s), Z = -0.3, p = 0.27 (p. 8, Table 3).
- **Visual test:** Marginal difference between home (mean = 12.23 s) and wrong (mean = 17.72 s), Z = -1.36, p = 0.07 (p. 8, Table 3).

**Page:** 7--8

**Bearing on reviewer challenges:**
- **Multiple reviewers (visual landmarks):** The visual landmark condition approaches significance (p = 0.07) but does not reach it. This is *partial* evidence for visual landmark use. The authors note the olfactory landmark was "more effective" but caution against direct comparison given sample sizes (p. 9). Importantly, even the visual condition shows a trend in the predicted direction -- the absence of significance may be a power issue (n = 14).
- **Horowitz:** The olfactory landmark's clearer effect is consistent with Horowitz's broader research programme emphasising olfaction as dogs' primary modality (Horowitz & Franks 2020, cited as ref [11]). But the visual trend is still present, suggesting dogs can learn from visual landmarks even if olfaction is dominant.
- **Keil:** The modality comparison bears on whether TRAIL is a "genuine category" or "just perceptual generalisation." If dogs used only olfactory landmarks, one might argue pure olfactory matching. The visual trend (even if marginal) suggests dogs can flexibly integrate non-olfactory information into spatial navigation. The fact that the olfactory landmark was also inevitably visible (p. 9: "while the olfactory landmark was designed to be inconspicuous, it was inevitably also visible") complicates the modality separation.

### Finding 5: Behavioural indicators of spatial uncertainty on the wrong floor

**Exact results (descriptive, not statistically analysed due to small ns):**
- **Baseline wrong floor:** 7 subjects had indirect approaches; 14 subjects showed tracking (floor-sniffing while walking, mean = 5.57 s); 15 looked at owner (mean latency = 5.51 s); 7 moved away from the wrong door (p. 8).
- **Baseline home floor:** 3 subjects had indirect approaches; 14 subjects showed tracking (mean = 4.95 s); 21 looked at owner (mean latency = 8.77 s); 3 moved away (p. 8).
- **Test wrong floor:** 11 subjects moved away from the wrong door (up from 7 at baseline); tracking latency on wrong floor = 6.1 s; 17 looked at owner on wrong floor (mean latency = 6.24 s) (p. 8).
- **Test home floor:** 6 moved away; tracking latency = 7.03 s; 23 looked at owner (mean latency = 8.63 s) (p. 8).

**Page:** 8

**Bearing on reviewer challenges:**
- **Keil:** The increase in "move away" behaviour on the wrong floor from baseline (7/29) to test (11/29) is suggestive: dogs are not just slower but show signs of spatial discomfort or re-evaluation. This is the kind of flexible response that is harder to explain as pure perceptual generalisation. (But: not statistically tested, small ns, acknowledged as equivocal by authors.)
- **Godfrey-Smith:** The "look at owner" behaviour is interesting for minimal cognitive prerequisites. The authors note this is ambiguous -- it could indicate uncertainty/information-seeking or could just be waiting for the owner to open the door (p. 9). But the pattern (more looking at owner on the wrong floor in both conditions, especially post-landmark) is at least consistent with the dog monitoring its own spatial uncertainty.
- **Multiple reviewers:** The decline in direct approaches on the wrong floor post-landmark (p. 9: "we found a decline in direct approaches during the test trial on the wrong floor, possibly indicating subjects' recognition that they were on the wrong floor") is the authors' own interpretation that the landmark changed navigational behaviour qualitatively, not just quantitatively.

### Finding 6: Including non-approaches reveals baseline discrimination

**Exact result:** When non-approaches are scored as 120 s (trial ceiling), there IS a significant baseline difference: home (mean = 29.76 s) vs. wrong (mean = 49.71 s), n = 26, Z = -2.06, p = 0.04 (p. 6).

**Page:** 6

**Bearing on reviewer challenges:**
- **Keil:** This is a complication. When non-approaching dogs are included, dogs DO show some floor discrimination even at baseline. The authors note this analysis "would indicate that dogs were initially able to distinguish between floors in their building" (p. 6) but flag that the 120 s ceiling is "semi-arbitrary" and the result says "more about the particular number we chose than about subject behavior" (p. 9). This cuts against a clean "no baseline discrimination" story. It suggests partial, pre-existing spatial knowledge that the landmark sharpens rather than creates.
- **Godfrey-Smith:** This partial baseline sensitivity is actually useful for the minimal-prerequisites argument: dogs already have some spatial knowledge of their building (they live there), and the landmark provides a learnable cue that enhances already-existing (but insufficient) navigational ability.

## Discussion Points Relevant to the TRAIL Paper

### Dogs can learn to use visual cues for navigation

The paper explicitly frames its contribution as demonstrating that dogs can learn to use landmarks for vertical navigation, a domain where they are known to perform poorly (p. 1, p. 8--9). Previous work (Milgram et al. 1999 [ref 7], 2002 [ref 10]; Fiset 2009 [ref 8]) had shown dogs can use visual landmarks for horizontal spatial learning. This paper extends the finding to vertical navigation. The visual landmark trend (p = 0.07) is not conclusive but is consistent with dogs being able to learn from visual spatial cues even in a domain (vertical navigation) where they are known to struggle.

### Landmarks change navigational behaviour, not just response speed

The combination of latency changes, approach-rate changes, and qualitative behavioural shifts (more indirect approaches, more moving away on the wrong floor) suggests the landmark does not merely speed up a pre-existing response but restructures the dog's navigational behaviour. This is relevant to the category-vs-generalisation debate: perceptual generalisation predicts graded speeding; categorical re-evaluation predicts the kind of qualitative shifts observed here.

### The "wrong door" phenomenon itself is evidence for TRAIL-relevant cognition

The paper's starting premise -- that dogs go to the "same" door on a different floor -- is itself evidence for a spatial representation that identifies spatial position within a floor but fails across floors. Dogs have a robust horizontal spatial map but poor vertical differentiation. This is consistent with the TRAIL paper's claim that dogs have structured spatial categories: the category works well within its ecological niche (horizontal navigation) and fails when extended to an environment (vertical apartment buildings) it was not designed for.

### The authors' own interpretation of "confusion"

The discussion (p. 9) states: "While we are unable to confirm if dogs are able to generate a true three-dimensional map, this result demonstrates a new use for landmark learning: in the context of vertical navigation, an unnatural form of navigation for terrestrial animals." This cautious framing is useful: the paper does not claim dogs have a full 3D spatial representation, only that they can learn to use landmarks to solve a navigation problem.

## Limitations (as reported by the authors, pp. 9--10)

1. Non-approaches could not be directly compared to latency measures (pp. 9).
2. Two types of visual landmark used (umbrella stand vs. streamers), possibly with different salience (p. 9).
3. Olfactory landmark was not purely olfactory -- it was also visible (p. 9).
4. Landmark was near the home door, not at the elevator threshold, so some dogs may not have seen it when exiting the elevator (p. 10).
5. Owners were asked to let dogs lead, which may not have matched normal behaviour; owner cues could not be fully controlled (p. 10).
6. Small sample sizes for subgroup analyses (visual vs. olfactory) (p. 9).

## Specific Utility for Responding to Each Reviewer

### Horowitz (anecdotal evidence, simpler explanations)

This paper is co-authored by Horowitz herself. It provides controlled, video-coded, experimentally measured evidence that:
- Dogs do confuse floors at baseline (confirming the "wrong door" phenomenon with data, not anecdote).
- A learned landmark changes this behaviour (p = 0.03 for latency, p = 0.03 for approach rate).
- The effect is not just visual salience: the olfactory landmark (hidden in a canister, designed to be visually inconspicuous) produced the stronger effect. If visual salience alone drove the behaviour, the visual landmark should have been more effective.
- The approach-rate shift (dogs stop going to the wrong door entirely) is not consistent with simple contrast-tracking or salience effects, which would predict continued approach at modified speed, not approach cessation.

**Limitation for our purposes:** This study is about landmark learning for floor discrimination, not about TRAIL following per se. It does not directly test whether dogs follow painted lines. But it establishes the broader point that dogs can learn to use novel spatial cues to solve navigation problems, which is the cognitive infrastructure that would support TRAIL extension.

### Keil (functional category vs. perceptual generalisation)

- The baseline-to-test shift is a learning effect, not pre-existing generalisation.
- The qualitative behavioural changes (avoidance, indirect approach, increased owner-looking on wrong floor) suggest more than graded perceptual similarity effects.
- The wrong-door phenomenon itself shows dogs have a structured spatial representation (they identify the correct within-floor position) that fails in a specific, predictable way (across floors). This is the kind of structured-but-limited cognition that is hard to explain as "just perceptual generalisation."

### Multiple reviewers (visual landmark integration into navigation)

- Dogs can learn to integrate a novel landmark (visual or olfactory) into vertical navigational behaviour.
- Previous horizontal landmark studies (Milgram et al. 1999, 2002; Fiset 2009) already showed visual landmark use; this extends the finding to vertical navigation.
- The visual landmark condition showed a trend (p = 0.07), consistent with visual cue learning even if olfaction is dominant.

### Godfrey-Smith (minimal cognitive prerequisites)

- Dogs do not need language, deliberate reflection, or cultural scaffolding to learn a landmark-based navigational strategy.
- The learning occurs over ~16 exposures (mean), a naturalistic exposure rate.
- The approach-rate change (some dogs stop approaching the wrong door entirely) suggests flexible, context-sensitive behavioural adjustment, not a fixed action pattern.
- The partial baseline discrimination (significant when non-approaches included) suggests dogs have some pre-existing spatial knowledge that the landmark sharpens. This is consistent with Godfrey-Smith's question about whether you need a structured representational system: dogs appear to have structured but incomplete spatial representations that can be augmented by learnable cues.
