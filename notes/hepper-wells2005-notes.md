# Hepper & Wells (2005) – Direction Determination from Odour Trails

## Full Citation

Hepper, P. G., & Wells, D. L. (2005). How many footsteps do dogs need to determine the direction of an odour trail? *Chemical Senses*, 30(4), 291–298. doi:10.1093/chemse/bji023

## Core Findings

### Dogs can determine trail direction from odour alone

"Dogs have the ability to determine the direction of an odour trail left by a human" (p. 291, abstract). This ability relies on olfactory cues, not visual information (Wells & Hepper, 2003, cited throughout).

**Key result:** "All dogs were successfully able to determine the direction of the trail at a level greater than chance (P < 0.025)" (p. 294) when presented with a 21-footstep trail.

### Minimum information requirement: 5 sequential footsteps

"Dogs require information from five sequential footsteps to successfully determine direction of an odour trail" (p. 296, summary of Experiment 5).

- **11 footsteps:** All 6 dogs successful (9–10/10 correct; P < 0.01)
- **9 footsteps:** All 6 dogs successful (9–10/10 correct; P < 0.01)
- **7 footsteps:** 5/6 dogs successful (8–10/10 correct; P < 0.01); one dog 8/10 (not significant)
- **5 footsteps:** All 6 dogs successful (9–10/10 correct; P < 0.01)
- **3 footsteps:** All 6 dogs at chance level (3–6/10 correct)

(Table 2, p. 295)

### Temporal decay rate

"It takes ∼1–2 s for the odour information in footsteps to change to provide discernible information that can be used by dogs to determine direction" (p. 291, abstract).

Calculated from walking speed: "The time taken to complete 100 trails of 21 footsteps reveals an average time between footsteps of 0.486 s (SD ± 0.25). For 5 footsteps there is a time difference of 1.9 s between the first footstep and the fifth, whereas for 3 footsteps the time difference is 0.9 s" (p. 297).

### Individual odour, not ground disturbance

When individual odour was removed but ground disturbance remained (Experiment 3: trail-layer wore rubber overshoes covered with three layers of plastic overshoes), "dogs performed at the level of chance" (p. 295, Table 1: all 6 dogs scored 3–7/10, none significant).

"Thus dogs in this study were using the individual odour cue deposited by a footstep to determine direction" (p. 296).

### Sequential order is critical

When footstep order was randomized (Experiment 2a), "dogs performed at the level of chance" (p. 294, Table 1). "The behaviour of the dogs in experiment 2a also differed. They back-tracked much more frequently and often changed the direction in which they tracked" (p. 294).

## Methodology

### Subjects

- **N = 6** male dogs
- **Age:** ∼2 years
- **Breeds:** 2 German shepherds, 4 labrador retriever/golden retriever crosses
- **Selection:** Pre-screened for direction-tracking ability on 100 m grass trails; all achieved 9/10 or 10/10 correct (P < 0.025, binomial test)

"Our previous research (Wells and Hepper, 2003) demonstrated that the ability to track direction appeared better in young male dogs" (p. 293).

### Experimental Design

**Substrate:** Dark beige wool carpet (100% tufted wool, jute hessian backing, pile length 10.5 mm) cut into 45.8 cm squares, laid on concrete surface in a straight line (p. 293).

**Trail-laying procedure:** Same individual (experimenter) walked one footstep per square wearing the same shoes (∼2 year old hiking boots with worn leather uppers and rubber soles). Trail left for 60 min before testing (p. 293–294).

**Testing procedure:** Dog and handler brought at right-angles to trail center; dog instructed to track. Dog, handler, and outcome-recorder all blind to trail direction. Each dog given 10 trials per condition using fresh carpet squares each time. Minimum 20 min between trials (p. 294).

**Controls:**
- Experiment 2a: Randomized footstep order (computer-generated sequences)
- Experiment 2b: Handled squares but replaced in correct order (control for handling)
- Experiment 3: Individual odour removed (rubber + plastic overshoes)
- Experiments 4–5: Systematic reduction of footstep number

### Statistical Analysis

Individual binomial tests for each dog (Siegel, 1956). Significance threshold P < 0.025 (Experiments 1–3) or P < 0.01 (Experiments 4–5).

## Relevance to TRAIL Argument

### Evidence for multi-property TRAIL representation

This paper provides strong evidence that dogs represent trails as **structured temporal-spatial sequences**, not just isolated scent patches:

1. **Temporal gradient sensitivity:** Dogs require 1–2 s of odour decay to extract directional information. This suggests sensitivity to *temporal structure* of the trail, not just presence/absence of scent.

2. **Sequential ordering is critical:** Random scrambling of footsteps (same total odour quantity, same spatial extent) renders the trail uninterpretable. "Dogs were using cues present in the footsteps to determine directionality" (p. 294), specifically their *temporal sequence*.

3. **Comparative processing:** "It is most likely that the dog determines direction, in this task, through processing elements of the olfactory signal contained within a footstep" (p. 296). The dog "compares the cues offered by each individual footstep which differ systematically with direction" (p. 297).

4. **Discrete vs. continuous information:** "Previous observations (Steen and Wilsson, 1990) suggest that dogs are unable to determine direction from a continuous olfactory trail but can do so when discrete separate olfactory stimuli are present, e.g. footsteps" (p. 296).

### Properties clustered in canine TRAIL concept

This paper suggests dogs represent trails as having at least the following clustered properties:

- **Individual odour signature** (not just ground disturbance)
- **Temporal decay gradient** (fresher → older)
- **Spatial discreteness** (individual footsteps as units)
- **Sequential order** (position in series)
- **Directionality** (emergent from temporal gradient + sequence)

These properties are *maintained together* by the physical process of trail-laying (walking deposits odour that decays over time). This is precisely the HPC structure: a cluster of properties held together by mechanisms (here, locomotion + chemical decay).

### Cross-domain recruitment potential

If dogs extend this TRAIL representation to non-physical domains (e.g., tracking a conversation, following a line of reasoning), that would constitute metaphorical extension in the HPC sense: recruiting the multi-property structure of TRAIL to organize novel experience.

## Key Quotes

### On direction determination mechanism

"It is most likely that the dog determines direction, in this task, through processing elements of the olfactory signal contained within a footstep" (p. 296).

"The dog compares the cues offered by each individual footstep which differ systematically with direction. Footsteps present a number of possibilities for an odour gradient to be followed. The individual odour may smell stronger with more recent footsteps, and thus the dog has to determine the strongest smell, which equates to the most recent, and move in that direction. Alternatively, it could be a product of decay that provides the relative information" (p. 297).

### On discrete vs. continuous trails

"Previous observations (Steen and Wilsson, 1990) suggest that dogs are unable to determine direction from a continuous olfactory trail but can do so when discrete separate olfactory stimuli are present, e.g. footsteps" (p. 296).

### On types of odour cues

"In broad terms there are two general types of odours that the dog may detect and use to follow an odour trail. First, the individual odour of the person being followed. This comprises their own personal smell arising from their skin, sebaceous, apocrine and eccrine secretions and the smell of their clothes, perfume, washing powder, etc. Second, is what may be termed contact or disturbance odour. As the individual walks over the ground their footsteps impact on the surface, crushing vegetation, insects, etc., releasing an odour based on this contact" (p. 291).

### On temporal resolution

"In this study, a time of ∼1–2 s is required for the olfactory information contained in a footstep to change on exposure to the environment to provide a sufficient difference in its olfactory signature for the dog to determine the trail's direction" (p. 297).

### On number vs. time

"The dog has two pieces of information which it uses to determine the direction of a trail: (i) a number of discrete pieces of information, i.e. the number of footsteps; and (ii) the difference in the odour cue due to time. The interaction between these on the ability of the dog to determine direction has yet to be fully explored" (p. 297).

"If time difference were the only factor involved, it should be theoretically possible for a dog to determine direction from 2 footsteps separated in their deposition by 2 s. However, the determination of direction is not just reliant on a difference in cues as provided by a difference in time. The dog also has to make a comparison between the different sources of information and it may be that a certain number are required (e.g. 5 footsteps in this study) for an accurate comparison to be made" (p. 297).

## Limitations and Caveats

### Training effects

"Caution must be expressed in reaching these conclusions. The influence of training on the dog's performance cannot be underestimated" (p. 297). The dogs were trained on 100 m grass trails with abundant olfactory information; "specific training on the above task may reduce the amount of information required to make a determination of direction" (p. 297).

### Environmental conditions

"Environmental conditions will influence the change in olfactory stimuli over time. The experiments here were conducted in the absence of strong winds on days, which were dull, not sunny and not raining (a typical Belfast day!). Differences in wind, humidity and temperature could all be expected to change the rate, and perhaps the way, that the olfactory information present in a footstep changes with time" (p. 297).

### Sample characteristics

- All dogs were male, ∼2 years old
- Small sample size (N = 6)
- All pre-screened for tracking ability (selection bias)
- "There was no difference in the performance of different breeds in this experiment and no further comparison is made between dog breeds" (p. 293)

### Task-specific design

- Tested on flat concrete surface (not natural terrain)
- Carpet substrate (wool) may have distinctive odour-retention properties
- 60-minute delay between trail-laying and testing (consistent but arbitrary)
- Straight-line trails only (no turns, branches, or complex geometry)

### Head-down tracking bias

"Observations of the dogs' performance here, and by others (Steen and Wilsson, 1990; Thesen et al., 1993; Wells and Hepper, 2003), indicate that dogs perform this task with their nose just above the ground and not in the air. This suggests they are using the ground-based olfactory cues rather than airborne scents subject to turbulence" (p. 296).

This methodological setup favors "tracking dogs" (head-down, following surface odour) over "air-scenting dogs" (head-up, following airborne scent rafts). Results may not generalize to all tracking styles.

### Unknowns

"How the dog determines directionality is unknown" (p. 296, before Discussion section). The paper proposes comparative processing of temporal decay gradients but does not directly test this mechanism.

"Having detected the direction of the trail, what causes the dog to move towards the source is unknown. It may be the result of an innate response; however, these were trained dogs and so it may represent a result of their training" (p. 297).

## Notes for TRAIL HPC Argument

This paper is **critical evidence** for the claim that dogs have a structured, multi-property concept of TRAIL that goes beyond simple scent-following. The key insight is that dogs don't just detect odour—they extract **temporal-sequential structure** from discrete odour samples.

The 5-footstep minimum and 1–2 s decay requirement suggest dogs are performing *comparative analysis* across a structured sequence, not just responding to individual stimuli. This is precisely the kind of property-cluster reasoning that HPC theory predicts: multiple properties (individual scent signature, temporal position, spatial location, decay state) are bound together into a unified representation (TRAIL) by causal mechanisms (locomotion + chemical decay).

If this structured TRAIL representation can be recruited for non-physical tracking tasks, that would constitute cross-domain metaphorical extension in the HPC sense.
