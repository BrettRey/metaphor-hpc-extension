# Canine Trail Papers: Notes for TRAIL-as-Functional-Category Argument

## Overview

These notes extract evidence from three papers on canine olfactory tracking and scent-marking behavior. The goal is to support the argument that dogs possess a functional spatial category TRAIL (distinct from abstract linguistic PATH schemas), demonstrating that metaphor via cross-domain HPC extension is phylogenetically old.

---

## Paper 1: Thesen, Steen & Døving (1993) — Behaviour of Dogs During Olfactory Tracking

**Citation:** Thesen, A., Steen, J.B., & Døving, K.B. (1993). Behaviour of dogs during olfactory tracking. *Journal of Experimental Biology*, 180, 247–251.

### Core Argument

German shepherd tracking dogs exhibit three distinct behavioral phases when following human odour trails: (1) searching, (2) deciding on direction, and (3) tracking. Dogs compare scent strength across consecutive footprints to determine trail direction, demonstrating sophisticated olfactory processing that operates on discrete spatial cues rather than continuous gradients.

### Key Claims Relevant to TRAIL-as-Functional-Category

#### 1. **Trail as Coherent Linear Object**
- Dogs treat trails as **directional linear structures**, not point-by-point scent gradients
- Three-phase behavior (search → decide → track) indicates dogs recognize when they have encountered a *trail-as-object*
- "The transition from searching to deciding was clear because the dogs halted for a moment when they detected the track" (p. 250)
- Dogs don't just follow scent — they **determine direction** by comparing odour across 2–5 footprints in 3–5 seconds

#### 2. **Trail/Non-Trail Discrimination**
- Dogs reliably discriminate trail from background: 100% success rate across all tests (20 trials total: 10 on grass, 10 on concrete)
- Dogs approached trails at right angles and immediately shifted behavior upon detection
- "In every test the dogs tracked in the correct direction" (p. 249)

#### 3. **Spatial Rules Applied Differentially**
- **Searching phase:** Fast movement (3.1 steps/s on concrete), short sniffing bursts (13.7 sniffs/period)
- **Deciding phase:** Slowed movement (1.9 steps/s), extended sniffing periods (35.6 sniffs/period on concrete) — 3× longer than search/track phases
- **Tracking phase:** Return to fast movement (3.2 steps/s), shorter sniffing periods (11.5 sniffs/period)
- Constant sniffing frequency (6 Hz) across all phases suggests active sampling strategy, not passive chemotaxis

#### 4. **Geometric/Predictive Representation**
- Dogs compare **temporal gradients** across footprints deposited 1 second apart
- Deciding phase requires analyzing odour from 2–5 footprints to extract directional information
- "Dogs compare the strength of the scent of consecutive footprints and then follow the track in the direction of increasing concentration" (Steen & Wilsson 1990, cited p. 247)
- This is **cognitive processing of spatial structure**, not tropism

### Quotable Passages

> "The deciding phase lasted 3–5 s, during which the dogs sniffed at 2–5 footprints, usually within one sniffing period. During this phase the dog's nose was held close to the ground, about 1 cm from the concrete" (p. 250)

> "We assume that the dogs determine the track direction by perceiving differences in the concentration of certain substances deposited by the track-maker. This implies that, in some of our tests, the dogs must have determined a difference in the concentration of scent in the air above two consecutive prints which were made 1 s apart, either 3 min or 20 min earlier." (p. 251)

> "Thus, the deciding phase seems to be the most difficult one; it is certainly the most impressive one from a human point of view." (p. 251)

> "To a human, this feat may appear unrealistic. However, although the sensitivity of individual olfactory receptors is similar in humans and dogs (Moulton et al. 1960), dogs have many more receptors than do humans (Adrian, 1956)." (p. 251)

### Key References to Track Down
- **Steen, J.B. & Wilsson, E. (1990).** How do dogs determine the direction of tracks? *Acta Physiologica Scandinavica*, 139, 531–534. [Earlier study establishing directional tracking ability]
- Moulton, D.G., Ashton, E.H., & Eayrs, J.T. (1960). Studies in olfactory acuity. 4. Relative detectability of n-aliphatic acids by the dog. *Animal Behaviour*, 8, 117–128.
- Adrian, A.D. (1956). Olfactory discrimination. *Année Psychologique*, 50, 107–113.

### Connection to TRAIL Category Argument

This paper provides evidence that dogs:
1. **Segment space into trail vs non-trail zones** (behavioral shift at trail detection)
2. **Apply different action rules to trails** (phase-specific movement/sniffing strategies)
3. **Represent trails as geometric linear objects with direction**, not scalar scent fields
4. **Use predictive/comparative processing** (temporal gradient analysis) rather than reactive chemotaxis

This supports the claim that TRAIL is a **functional spatial category** — dogs have a domain-specific representation that licenses inferences about movement, direction, and spatial continuity. Not PATH (abstract schema), but TRAIL (grounded, perceptually anchored category).

## Paper 2: Hepper & Wells (2005) — How Many Footsteps Do Dogs Need to Determine Direction?

**Citation:** Hepper, P.G., & Wells, D.L. (2005). How many footsteps do dogs need to determine the direction of an odour trail? *Chemical Senses*, 30(4), 291–298.

### Core Argument

Dogs require **five sequential footsteps** (spanning ~1–2 seconds of deposition time) to reliably determine trail direction. Directional tracking relies on **individual odour cues** (not ground disturbance), and dogs use a **comparative process** analyzing discrete olfactory patches rather than continuous gradients. This demonstrates sophisticated spatial-olfactory integration.

### Key Claims Relevant to TRAIL-as-Functional-Category

#### 1. **Trail as Coherent Linear Object**
- Dogs treat trails as **sequences of discrete information units** (footsteps), not diffuse plumes
- When footstep order was randomized (Exp 2a), dogs **failed to determine direction** (chance performance), demonstrating they rely on **sequential spatial structure**
- "When given the randomized tracks (experiment 2a) dogs performed at the level of chance... They back-tracked much more frequently and often changed the direction in which they tracked. This suggests the cues presented were more confusing" (p. 294)
- Control (Exp 2b): Handling footsteps but preserving order = 100% success → confirms dogs use **spatial sequence information**

#### 2. **Trail/Non-Trail Discrimination**
- All 6 dogs reliably discriminated trails from background (90–100% correct over 10 trials, p < 0.025)
- Pre-screening: Dogs achieved 9/10 or 10/10 success on 100m grass trails
- Dogs required **1 hour aging** on grass, **3 minutes aging** on concrete to determine direction

#### 3. **Spatial Rules Applied Differentially to Trail vs Margin**
- Exp 1: Dogs successfully tracked 21-footstep trails (all dogs p < 0.025)
- Exp 4: Dogs required **minimum 5 sequential footsteps** to determine direction
  - 11 footsteps: all dogs successful (p < 0.01)
  - 9 footsteps: all dogs successful
  - 7 footsteps: 5/6 dogs successful
  - **5 footsteps: all dogs successful (p < 0.01)**
  - **3 footsteps: all dogs at chance level**
- Exp 5: Dogs failed when presented with 5 footsteps containing only 3 temporal intervals (paired simultaneous footsteps) → demonstrates **temporal information** is critical, not just spatial extent

#### 4. **Geometric/Predictive Trail Representation**
- Dogs use **comparative temporal analysis**: "the dog compares the cues offered by each individual footstep which differ systematically with direction" (p. 297)
- Two possible gradients: (1) **freshness** (recent footsteps smell stronger), or (2) **decay** (older footsteps smell more decayed)
- **Time difference required:** 1–2 seconds between footsteps for odour signature to change sufficiently (calculated from 0.486s average inter-footstep interval × 5 footsteps = 1.9s total span)
- "Thus, in this study, a time of ~1–2 s is required for the olfactory information contained in a footstep to change on exposure to the environment to provide a sufficient difference in its olfactory signature for the dog to determine the trail's direction." (p. 297)

#### 5. **Individual Odour (Not Ground Disturbance)**
- **Exp 3:** When individual odour removed (plastic overshoes covering feet), dogs performed at **chance level** despite ground disturbance remaining
- "Thus, although information was still present from ground disturbance as the individual walked the trail, this cue did not provide sufficient information for the dogs to determine direction" (p. 295)
- Contact vs airborne odour: Dogs used **ground-deposited individual odour**, not airborne scent rafts or vegetation disturbance

### Quotable Passages

> "Dogs have the ability to determine the direction of an odour trail left by a human... dogs trained to determine directionality required 5 sequential footsteps, i.e. discrete pieces of information, to determine the direction of an odour trail." (p. 291, Abstract)

> "It takes ~1–2 s for the odour information in footsteps to change to provide discernible information that can be used by dogs to determine direction." (p. 291, Abstract)

> "Dogs were unable to determine direction when the order of the footsteps was randomized by rearranging the order of the carpet squares... indicating that it was the odour of the individual that was used to determine direction." (p. 291, Abstract)

> "Footsteps provide a series of discrete odour cues that differ in time of deposition and hence may form a reasonably uniform stepwise olfactory gradient." (p. 296)

> "It is most likely that the dog determines direction, in this task, through processing elements of the olfactory signal contained within a footstep... the dog compares the cues offered by each individual footstep which differ systematically with direction." (p. 296–297)

> "The dog has two pieces of information which it uses to determine the direction of a trail: (i) a number of discrete pieces of information, i.e. the number of footsteps; and (ii) the difference in the odour cue due to time." (p. 297)

### Statistical Evidence

- **Pre-screening:** 9/10 or 10/10 correct on 100m trails (p < 0.025, binomial)
- **Exp 1 (21 footsteps):** All dogs 9/10 or 10/10 (p < 0.025)
- **Exp 2a (randomized):** All dogs 3/10 to 6/10 (chance level, n.s.)
- **Exp 2b (handled control):** All dogs 5/5 = 100% (p < 0.025)
- **Exp 3 (no individual odour):** All dogs 3/10 to 7/10 (chance level)
- **Exp 4 (5 footsteps):** All dogs 9/10 or 10/10 (p < 0.01)
- **Exp 4 (3 footsteps):** All dogs 3/10 to 6/10 (chance level)

### Key References to Track Down

- **Wells, D.L. & Hepper, P.G. (2003).** Directional tracking in the domestic dog, *Canis familiaris*. *Applied Animal Behaviour Science*, 84, 297–305. [Dogs use olfactory cues, not visual]
- **Kalmus, H. (1955).** The discrimination by the nose of the dog of individual human odours and in particular of the odours of twins. *British Journal of Animal Behaviour*, 3, 25–31. [Individual odour discrimination]
- **Budgett, H.M. (1933).** *Hunting by Scent.* Charles Scribner, New York. [Contact odour importance]
- Atema, J. (1996). Eddy chemotaxis and odor landscapes: exploration of nature with animal sensors. *Biological Bulletin*, 191, 129–138. [Comparative: lobster odour plume navigation]
- Vickers, N.J. (2000). Mechanisms of animal navigation. *Biological Bulletin*, 198, 203–212. [Comparative: insect orientation strategies]

### Connection to TRAIL Category Argument

This paper provides critical evidence that:

1. **TRAIL is not a continuous scalar field** — dogs fail when spatial sequence is disrupted (randomized footsteps), even though total odour remains
2. **TRAIL has internal geometric structure** — dogs require **5 sequential samples** spanning 1–2 seconds, suggesting a **minimal structural complexity threshold** for the category
3. **TRAIL licenses predictive inferences** — dogs extract directional information via temporal comparison, not reactive tropism
4. **TRAIL is perceptually grounded in individual odour**, not abstract features like "disturbance"
5. **TRAIL supports different action affordances than non-trail space** — dogs shift behavioral strategies (speed, sniffing) when on vs off trail

This demonstrates that dogs have a **domain-specific spatial category** with:
- **Entry conditions** (sequential odour patches, temporal gradient)
- **Structural properties** (directionality, linearity, continuity)
- **Functional consequences** (licenses movement-toward-source, predicts continuation)

Not PATH (too abstract, too linguistic), but **TRAIL** — grounded in canine perceptual ecology, stabilized by predatory/scavenging selection pressures, projectible for navigation.

## Paper 3: Cafazzo, Natoli & Valsecchi (2012) — Scent-Marking Behaviour in Free-Ranging Dogs

**Citation:** Cafazzo, S., Natoli, E., & Valsecchi, P. (2012). Scent-marking behaviour in a pack of free-ranging domestic dogs. *Ethology*, 118, 955–966.

### Core Argument

Free-ranging dogs use scent-marking (urination, defecation, ground-scratching) to delineate territorial boundaries and communicate dominance/reproductive status. Both sexes mark preferentially along territorial boundaries (not interior), with dominant individuals marking more frequently. Raised-leg urination (males) and ground-scratching (both sexes) serve as multimodal signals (olfactory + visual). Spatial distribution of marking demonstrates dogs recognize and enforce **boundary zones** as functionally distinct from den/feeding areas.

### Key Claims Relevant to TRAIL-as-Functional-Category

#### 1. **Trail as Coherent Linear Object?**
- **Limited direct evidence.** This study focuses on scent-marking at boundaries, not trail-following behavior
- However: Dogs **concentrate markings along territorial boundaries** (linear/peripheral zones), suggesting recognition of **linear spatial structures** as functionally significant
- "Males scent-marked more frequently along territorial boundaries and in the interior of the territory than in rest of the home range" (Table 4, p. 960: boundary vs interior, t = 5.00, z = 2.98, p = 0.003)

#### 2. **Spatial Zones with Different Behavioral Rules**
- **Strong evidence:** Dogs apply **zone-specific marking rules**
  - **Territorial boundaries:** High marking rate (males: raised-leg urination, ground-scratching)
  - **Territorial interior:** Moderate marking (feeding sites, denning sites)
  - **Home range periphery:** Low marking
  - **Crossroads (boundary intersections):** Extremely high marking concentration
- "Males scent-marked via raised-leg urination mainly at the crossroads along territorial borders (t = 5.00, z = 2.98, n = 16, p = 0.003)" (p. 960)
- Females also marked differentially: "Females scent-marked more frequently at denning sites than at feeding sites and somewhere else (Table 5)" (p. 960)

#### 3. **Trail/Boundary Discrimination**
- Dogs **never marked outside territorial area**: "Males scent-marked more frequently along territorial boundaries and in the interior of the territory than in rest of the home range (Table 4)" (p. 960)
- Boundary marking hypothesis supported: "Males scent-marked more frequently along territorial boundaries vs. the interior... influenced by the sex of dogs" (p. 959–960)
- **Crossroads as strategic sites:** "Also males scent-marked via raised-leg urination mainly at the crossroads along territorial borders" (p. 960) — demonstrates recognition of **topological/geometric structure**

#### 4. **Marking Location Statistics**

| **Site Type** | **Male Raised-Leg Urination Rate** | **Female Flexed-Leg Urination Rate** |
|---------------|-------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Territorial boundaries** | Higher than interior (t = 0.00, z = 3.51, p = 0.0004) | Higher at boundaries (Table 4) |
| **Crossroads (boundaries)** | Highest rate (t = 5.00, z = 2.98, p = 0.003) | — |
| **Denning sites** | — | Higher than feeding/elsewhere (t = 0.00, z = 2.98, p = 0.003) |
| **Feeding sites** | Higher than elsewhere (t = 10.00, z = 1.12, p = 0.26, n.s.) | Lower than denning (t = 0.00, z = 2.22, p = 0.02) |
| **Outside territory** | Near-zero | — |

- Total observations: 782 urinations, 143 ground scratches, 89 defecations over 282.53 hours
- 27 dogs studied (6 adult males, 5 adult females, 16 juveniles)
- Territory size: 22.14 ha; periphery zone defined as 50m inside boundary

#### 5. **Multimodal Marking (Olfactory + Visual)**
- Ground-scratching follows raised-leg urination: "Ground scratching... often occurs after raised-leg urination and defecation (Rothman & Mech 1979; Pal 2003; Zub et al. 2003)" (p. 956)
- Visual component: "The visual component may involve either scent deposited from metatarsal glands or the dispersal of olfactory scent from deposited urine or faeces (Peters & Mech 1975; Fox & Cohen 1977)" (p. 956)
- Scratches send grass/earth flying backward → visible disturbance marker

#### 6. **Dominance/Threat Hypothesis**
- High-ranking males marked more: "Dominant animals would mark more frequently than subordinate animals (prediction 2a)" — **SUPPORTED**
- PCA Factor 1 (males): high negative scores = high-ranking, old individuals with high aggressive behavior frequency → these dogs marked **more frequently** (Fig. 1a)
- "High-ranking individuals are more likely to display marking behaviour" (p. 962, citing Ralls 1971)

### Quotable Passages

> "We found evidence that markings are used by dogs to form a 'property line' and to delineate territory, to threaten rivals over it. Raised-leg urination and ground scratching probably play a role in olfactory and visual communication in both males and females." (p. 955, Abstract)

> "Males scent-marked more frequently along territorial boundaries and in the interior of the territory than in rest of the home range (Table 4)... Also males scent-marked via raised-leg urination mainly at the crossroads along territorial borders (t = 5.00, z = 2.98, n = 16, p = 0.003)." (p. 960)

> "Our results provide evidence that scent marking by free-ranging domestic dogs may serve as demarcation of territorial boundaries according to the territorial defence hypothesis. Male dogs, as other species of canids (wolves; Harrington 1981; Asa et al. 1985a,b, 1990; Harrington & Asa 2003; Peters & Mech 1975; Zub et al. 2003; coyotes; Wells & Bekoff 1981; Gese & Ruff 1997; Allen et al. 1999), appear to delimit their territory by means of urine and scratching." (p. 962)

> "Territorial boundaries were assumed to be the same for all pack members because it has been observed that all pack members cooperated for territorial and resources defence against intruders from other neighbouring social packs (Bonanni et al. 2010a). Group territory size measured 22.14 ha and its boundaries were inside the boundaries of home range." (p. 959)

> "Scent marking by urination is a common practice in dogs (Canis lupus familiaris; Bekoff 1979a,b)... Dogs frequently approached a central road crossing the study area, especially in the early morning, to feed on the food (mainly meat from a slaughterhouse) brought every day by volunteer dog caretakers." (p. 957)

### Connection to TRAIL Category Argument

This paper provides **indirect but important** evidence:

1. **Spatial zones have distinct behavioral rules** — dogs recognize **boundaries, dens, feeding sites** as functionally different categories, applying zone-specific marking strategies
2. **Linear/peripheral structures are privileged** — territorial boundaries (linear zones) receive disproportionate marking attention, especially at **crossroads** (topological intersections)
3. **Trail-margin distinction?** — While this study doesn't directly address trail-following, the **boundary-marking pattern** suggests dogs recognize linear spatial structures (boundaries = trails?) as:
   - Geometrically distinct from homogeneous interior space
   - Functionally significant for navigation/communication
   - Topologically structured (crossroads matter)
4. **Elimination sites are spatially structured** — "All dens, resting and feeding sites were located inside the territory and none in the periphery" (p. 959) → **den ≠ trail ≠ margin** in functional terms

### Limitations for TRAIL Argument

- **No tracking behavior studied** — this paper examines *marking* (scent deposition), not *following* (scent detection/trail navigation)
- **Boundaries ≠ Trails** — territorial boundaries are semi-permanent zones; trails are ephemeral individual-specific paths
- **BUT:** Both involve recognizing **linear spatial structures** as functionally distinct from homogeneous space

### Integrated Insight

Combining with Thesen (1993) and Hepper & Wells (2005):
- Dogs recognize multiple **spatial zone categories**: TRAIL (ephemeral, individual-specific, directional), BOUNDARY (persistent, pack-maintained, peripheral), DEN (sheltered, central, protected), FEEDING-SITE (resource-concentrated)
- Each category has:
  - **Distinct perceptual cues** (odour type, spatial configuration)
  - **Distinct behavioral rules** (movement strategy, marking behavior, elimination patterns)
  - **Distinct affordances** (trail → follow-to-source; boundary → mark-to-defend; den → rest/shelter)

This supports the broader claim that **spatial categorization in dogs is fine-grained and functionally differentiated**, not a monolithic PATH schema.

## Synthesis: Evidence for TRAIL as Functional Spatial Category

### Convergent Evidence Across Three Papers

1. **Dogs treat trails as coherent linear objects**
   - Thesen: Three-phase behavior (search/decide/track) demonstrates trail-as-object recognition
   - Hepper: Sequential structure matters — randomized footsteps abolish directionality
   - Cafazzo: Dogs privilege linear structures (boundaries) for marking → sensitivity to linear spatial forms

2. **Trail/Non-Trail discrimination is robust**
   - Thesen: 100% success rate; immediate behavioral shift upon trail detection
   - Hepper: 90–100% discrimination on intact trails; chance performance when structure removed
   - Cafazzo: Sharp boundary/interior distinction in marking behavior

3. **Spatial rules applied differentially**
   - Thesen: Phase-specific movement (3.1 → 1.9 → 3.2 steps/s) and sniffing strategies
   - Hepper: Minimum 5 footsteps required → structural complexity threshold
   - Cafazzo: Zone-specific marking rules (boundary ≠ den ≠ feeding site)

4. **Geometric/predictive representation, not chemotaxis**
   - Thesen: Dogs compare temporal gradients (1s intervals) across 2–5 footprints
   - Hepper: Dogs extract directional information via comparative analysis (~1–2s odour decay)
   - Cafazzo: Dogs recognize topological structure (crossroads) → not just local cues

5. **Grounded in individual odour (Hepper) + multimodal cues (Cafazzo)**
   - Individual scent (not disturbance) carries directional information
   - Ground-scratching adds visual component to olfactory trail

### TRAIL vs PATH

| Feature | TRAIL (Dog Category) | PATH (Linguistic Schema) |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| **Grounding** | Perceptual (odour, visual marks, topology) | Abstract (metaphorical extension) |
| **Directionality** | Extracted from temporal odour gradient | Encoded in syntax/semantics |
| **Continuity** | Requires 5+ sequential samples | Conceptual (unbounded) |
| **Affordances** | Movement-to-source, track-maintenance | Source-goal inference |
| **Entry conditions** | Individual odour + sequential structure | Motion + traversable medium |
| **Phylogenetic age** | Ancient (predator/scavenger ecology) | Recent (language-dependent?) |

### Implications for HPC Metaphor Argument

**If TRAIL is a functional spatial category in dogs**, then:

1. **Cross-domain extension is phylogenetically old** — dogs may extend TRAIL → other linear structures (streams? fallen logs?) on partial property overlap (linearity, continuity, directionality)
2. **Metaphor ≠ uniquely human** — HPC-based analogical reasoning (partial property cluster overlap) predates language
3. **PATH may be human-specific elaboration** of ancestral TRAIL category — linguistic abstraction of perceptually-grounded dog cognition
4. **Stabilizing mechanisms differ** — TRAIL stabilized by predatory success (track prey → eat); PATH stabilized by communicative success (talk about motion → coordinate action)

### Outstanding Questions

1. **Do dogs generalize TRAIL to non-odour linear structures?** (visual tracking of tire tracks? Following streams?)
2. **Is there neural evidence for TRAIL-specific processing?** (Distinct from general olfaction or spatial navigation?)
3. **Do other carnivores show similar TRAIL behavior?** (Wolves, coyotes, foxes — comparative evidence)
4. **What are the developmental origins?** (Innate vs learned? Puppies vs adults?)
5. **Can TRAIL be experimentally dissociated from PATH?** (Dogs navigate trails without understanding linguistic descriptions)
