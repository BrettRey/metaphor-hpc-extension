# Collaborator Outreach Packet

## Working study title

Defecation site selection relative to bounded paths in domestic dogs

Alternative framings:
- Elimination-site choice relative to bounded linear features during dog walks
- Defecation-site selection under path boundedness constraints in domestic dogs
- Path-edge and junction effects on defecation-site choice in dogs

## Positioning

This should be pitched as a behavioral ecology / canine cognition / applied ethology study, not as a joke question and not as a side note to the metaphor paper.

The clean empirical question:

Do dogs, when they need to defecate, choose sites relative to path edges and junctions in systematic ways, and how does that choice change when off-path access is constrained by boundedness?

The practical design frame:

Use whatever facilities a collaborator already has access to: fenced fields, dog-walk lanes, shelter exercise runs, free-ranging dog sites, or managed outdoor test areas. Mississauga is not important.

## Core ask

Ask for a 20-30 minute conversation about:
- whether the question is publishable and worth piloting
- whether their facilities could support a bounded-path design
- whether they would consider advising, collaborating, or pointing you to a better fit

## Sixty-second pitch

I am developing a small empirical study on defecation-site choice in dogs. The core question is whether dogs choose sites relative to path edges and junctions in systematic ways, and whether those choices shift when off-path access is constrained by bounded paths. The design would be simple: repeated dog walks through standardized path conditions, coding the first defecation event and the search behavior immediately preceding it. I think the project is publishable if framed correctly, but it needs a collaborator with real canine behavior experience and access to an appropriate testing environment. I am trying to find the right methodological home for a pilot.

## One-paragraph summary

I am looking to develop a pilot study on defecation-site selection in domestic dogs relative to paths, path edges, and path junctions. The key manipulation is boundedness: compare open paths with paths where stepping off is easy, somewhat costly, or effectively blocked. The main outcome is not simply whether a dog defecates on a path, but where it defecates relative to available alternatives. I am looking for a collaborator who can help determine whether this is best treated as a controlled owned-dog study, a shelter-dog study, or a free-ranging-dog field study, and whether an existing site could be adapted for a bounded-path protocol.

## One-page collaborator brief

### Why this is worth studying

- Dogs clearly treat paths, edges, and linear features as behaviorally important, but defecation-site choice relative to path structure appears under-studied.
- The question is concrete and measurable.
- It bridges marking behavior, movement ecology, and applied dog-walk behavior.
- The bounded-path manipulation creates a clean way to separate preference from constraint.

### Main hypotheses

- Dogs will not place defecation sites randomly with respect to paths.
- Path edges will be preferred over path centers when off-path movement is easy.
- Junction-adjacent locations may be over-selected relative to straight segments.
- As off-path access becomes harder, defecation events will shift toward on-path placement.

### Minimal pilot design

- Within-dog repeated-measures design.
- Three path conditions: open, low-bounded, high-bounded.
- Same handler, leash length, path width, and walk protocol across sessions.
- Record the first defecation event only.
- Code the final search phase before defecation: sniffing, circling, urination, attempts to step off path, pace reduction, and exact location.

### What would make a collaborator especially useful

- Access to a controlled outdoor dog-testing space or a field site already used for dog behavior work.
- Experience with video coding, ethograms, or movement tracking.
- Ability to advise on whether the first study should use pet dogs, shelter dogs, or free-ranging dogs.
- Strong judgment about publishable scope for a pilot.

### What I am not asking for initially

- A commitment to the full project before a short feasibility conversation.
- A complicated sensor stack.
- A commitment to use drones.

### Practical first pilot

- 15-20 dogs
- 2-4 sessions per dog
- Fixed cameras plus mapped path geometry
- Simple location coding, not elaborate wearables

## Outreach strategy

Do not send to all five at once. Use waves. The goal is to learn from early replies and refine the ask before broadening the circle.

- Wave 1: Valsecchi and Marshall-Pescini
- Wave 2: Westgarth
- Wave 3: Feuerbacher
- Wave 4: Horowitz

This sequence gives you:
- one high-probability topical contact first
- one field-oriented contact first
- one walk-context contact after you see whether the question is being heard as marking, movement, or both
- one methods-heavy contact after the first round sharpens the likely study population
- one high-visibility stretch contact last

### 4. Erica Feuerbacher

Subject: Possible dog behavior collaboration: path-edge and junction effects

Dear Professor Feuerbacher,

I am developing a pilot study on defecation-site choice in dogs and am looking for a collaborator or advisor with strong instincts for clean behavioral design.

- Use "defecation-site choice" or "elimination-site selection" in writing.
