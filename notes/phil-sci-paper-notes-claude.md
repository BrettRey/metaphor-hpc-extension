# Philosophy of Science Paper Notes: HPC Reframing of CMT

Notes extracted for the argument that HPC theory reframes Lakoff & Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory (CMT). Key claim: Boyd says metaphors succeed by accommodating to causal structure in the world; Lakoff says they express embodied schemas. HPC adds stability mechanisms that CMT lacks. Metaphor = cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap.

## Field (1973): Theory Change and the Indeterminacy of Reference

**Full citation:** Field, Hartry. 1973. "Theory Change and the Indeterminacy of Reference." *Journal of Philosophy* 70(14): 462–481.

### Core Argument

Field argues that scientific terms are referentially indeterminate—there is no fact of the matter about what they denote. Using the case study of 'mass' in Newtonian mechanics vs. special relativity, he shows that Newton's term 'mass' cannot be said to refer determinately to either proper mass or relativistic mass. Both interpretations are equally plausible, but there is no basis for choosing between them. This indeterminacy reveals that reference is not determined by a pure causal link to the world, but requires additional semantic machinery involving partial denotation and partial signification.

### Key Claims Relevant to HPC Reframing of CMT

**Partial reference and referential indeterminacy:**
- Field introduces "partial denotation": a term can partially denote more than one quantity. A term that partially denotes exactly one quantity is said to *fully* denote that quantity; a term that (determinately) denotes that quantity fails to fully denote *either* (p. 475).
- Newton's word 'mass' partially denoted proper mass and partially denoted relativistic mass, and didn't partially denote anything else (p. 475, hypothesis HI).
- The novel feature is that a singular term can partially denote more than one thing (p. 474).
- Field develops a semantics for indeterminate expressions using the concept of "structure": a function that maps names/quantity terms into objects/quantities and predicates into sets. A sentence is m-true/m-false relative to structure m (p. 477).

**How theoretical terms fix reference:**
- Pure causal theories cannot handle referential indeterminacy. Field argues against the view that theoretical terms refer to "whatever aspect of reality prompted their use" (Devitt's approach). Without criteria of appropriateness, we cannot determine what "prompted" use of a term (pp. 466–467).
- The conjunction of Newtonian tenets (4R: momentum = mass × velocity) and (5P: mass transforms across frames) was false. Both were central to Newtonian mechanics. Relativity shows some physicists' tokens of 'mass' for proper mass make (4R) true and (5P) false; others using 'mass' for relativistic mass make (4R) false and (5P) true (p. 467).
- "There is no basis for choosing between (HR) and (HP)... there is no fact of the matter as to which of the two conjuncts was true and which false, and hence no fact of the matter as to whether the word 'mass' (as it occurred in them) denoted relativistic mass or proper mass" (p. 468).

**Mechanism, maintenance, or stability of reference:**
- Not directly addressed. Field's focus is on showing that reference is *not* stably determined by causal contact alone.

**Success conditions for metaphor or analogy in science:**
- Not directly addressed, but the indeterminacy thesis has implications: if terms are referentially indeterminate, then the "success" of a theoretical term may consist in partial reference to multiple quantities, not full reference to one.

### Quotable Passages

1. **Referential indeterminacy thesis:**
   > "In this paper I will argue that considerations about scientific revolutions show that many scientific terms are *referentially indeterminate*—there is no fact of the matter as to what they denote (if they are singular terms) or as to what their extension is (if they are general terms)." (p. 462)

2. **The 'mass' case:**
   > "My claim is that we *can't*. Before relativity theory was discovered (I will argue), the word 'mass' was *referentially indeterminate*: it did not *have* denotation, in any straightforward sense; on the contrary, there are *two* physical quantities that each satisfy the informal criteria for being the denotation of the term." (p. 466)

3. **Against pure causal theories:**
   > "The difficulty of choosing between (HR) and (HP) arises, then, from the fact that (4R) and (5P) were both extremely central to Newton's theorizing and to his scientific practice; and I see no basis for asserting that a particular one of them was more central than the other." (p. 468)

4. **No fact of the matter:**
   > "The conjunction of Newton's tenets (4R) and (5P) and their being both false, but there is no fact of the matter as to which of the two conjuncts was true and which false, and hence no fact of the matter as to whether the word 'mass' (as it occurred in them) denoted relativistic mass or proper mass." (p. 468)

5. **Partial denotation:**
   > "The novel feature of partial denotation is that a singular term can partially denote more than one thing, and a quantity term like 'mass' can partially denote more than one quantity. A term that partially denotes exactly one quantity is said to be *referentially determinate*; a term that partially denotes more than one quantity (or *determinately*) denotes that quantity; and a term that doesn't partially denote anything is said to be *denotationless*." (p. 475)

6. **Structures and truth:**
   > "In order to give a semantics for indeterminate expressions, let's introduce the term 'structure'; a *structure* for a sentence is a function that maps each name or quantity term of the sentence into some object or quantity, and maps each predicate into some set. The structure m *corresponds* to the sentence if each name or quantity term of the sentence partially denotes the thing that m assigns to it, and each predicate partially signifies the set that m assigns to it. Now, for each structure m, we can apply the standard referential (Tarski-type) semantics to m to determine whether the sentence is *m-true* or *m-false*, i.e., true or false *relative to m*." (p. 477)

### Connection to HPC Reframing Argument

**Direct relevance:**
Field's partial denotation framework shows how metaphorical extension works in science. When 'mass' partially refers to both proper mass and relativistic mass, this is structurally similar to how a metaphor based on partial property overlap can successfully refer across domains. The "partial denotation" apparatus could be adapted to explain how CMT metaphors work: they succeed not by full reference but by partial reference grounded in shared properties (the overlap that HPC mechanisms stabilize).

**Indirect relevance:**
Field undermines pure causal theories of reference. If causal contact doesn't determine reference, then something else must—plausibly, the clustering of properties that mechanisms maintain. This opens space for an HPC account where reference is stabilized by homeostatic mechanisms, not just causal baptism.

**For the HPC/CMT synthesis:**
- Field shows that reference can be *indeterminate* and still *successful*. This parallels the CMT claim that metaphors are productive even when the mapping is partial.
- The partial denotation framework could model how metaphors work: source and target domains share a property cluster (maintained by mechanisms), and the metaphor exploits this overlap.
- Field's critique of pure causal theories supports the HPC claim that mechanisms (not just causal contact) stabilize reference.

## Cummiskey (1992): Reference Failure and Scientific Realism

**Full citation:** Cummiskey, D. 1992. "Reference Failure and Scientific Realism: A Response to the Meta-induction." *British Journal for the Philosophy of Science* 43(1): 21–40.

### Core Argument

Cummiskey argues that pure causal theories of reference cannot account for cases of theoretical term reference failure (e.g., 'phlogiston', 'optical aether', 'caloric') and thus cannot defend scientific realism against the meta-induction argument (that past theories failed to refer, so current theories probably do too). To handle reference failure and paradigm cases of referential success, a descriptive element must supplement the causal account. Cummiskey proposes Richard Boyd's "theory constitutive metaphors" as the necessary descriptive constraint: metaphors provide flexible descriptions that link terms to "appropriate" aspects of reality while allowing for discovery and error.

### Key Claims Relevant to HPC Reframing of CMT

**Partial reference and referential indeterminacy:**
- Endorses Field's partial reference framework (p. 37): "cases of partial reference and bifurcating natural kinds do not undermine scientific realism but instead lend support to the thesis that the development of science reveals an accommodation of language to the causal structure of the world."
- Partial reference shows that metaphorical success is not all-or-nothing: "In many cases we will succeed in referring only to some aspect of reality that subsequent research will show to be complex in nature, and thus further accommodation of language will be necessary if we wish to 'cut the world at its joints'" (p. 37).

**How theoretical terms fix reference:**
- **Theory constitutive metaphors** fix reference by providing a flexible descriptive constraint (pp. 22–23, 28–31):
  - Metaphor involves a primary subject (well understood) and secondary subject (the theoretical entity we're trying to understand).
  - "The specific nature of these similarities, however, displays what Boyd calls 'inductive open-endedness' and what Ernan McMullin calls 'tentative extension'" (p. 28).
  - Metaphors are "invitations to future research": they introduce terms without specifying defining characteristics but leave it to scientists to discover the yet-unspecified essential properties (p. 28).
  - Example: 'optical aether' was introduced with the metaphor of a fluid-like medium of transmission. The metaphor suggested light propagates through a medium analogous to waves in liquid. Scientists were invited to discover whether this was literally a liquid (it wasn't—it had to be impervious to all causes except propagation of light) (pp. 29–30).

- **Quasi-perception and causal link:**
  - Devitt's "quasi-perception through instruments" provides a causal link to the world (pp. 31–32).
  - But causal link alone is insufficient: "The descriptions must play more of a role than causal theorists have thus far allowed. Theory constitutive metaphors provide the descriptive constraint limiting the referent of the term to an 'appropriate' aspect of reality" (p. 27).

**Mechanism, maintenance, or stability of reference:**
- Metaphors stabilize reference by constraining the search space: they indicate which aspects of reality are relevant while remaining open-ended (p. 28).
- Success in discovering and articulating the metaphor's implied similarities stabilizes reference: "if the rough, tentative, programmatic, inductively open-ended specification is further *developed and articulated* by the subsequent history of a *successful* theory using the term in question, then we are surely justified in believing that the term has indeed locked onto a causally and explanatorily significant feature of the world" (p. 35).
- Reference failure occurs when the metaphor cannot be further articulated: "When a new explanatory mechanism can explain the successes and can be more fully articulated, it is reasonably concluded that no explanatory significant feature of the world was picked out by the central term of the theory" (p. 35).

**Success conditions for metaphor or analogy in science:**
- Four conditions for successful reference via metaphor (p. 39):
  1. Quasi-perception provides a causal link to the world.
  2. A theory constitutive metaphor provides a link to an underlying natural property or natural kind/family.
  3. The resulting research program is phenomenally successful.
  4. The explanatory metaphor is fruitfully articulated (not just tentatively specified but further developed).

- **Phenomenal success vs. articulation success:**
  - Caloric theory had phenomenal success (experiments on heat transfer, melting, boiling, thermal expansion) but the metaphor (heat as a conserved fluid) was not successfully articulated. The kinetic theory provided a better articulation (p. 34).
  - Optical aether theories had phenomenal success *and* adequate articulation of a mechanical model for light, but the metaphor (like-a-liquid medium) was never coherently articulated (p. 30, 35).

- **Mechanism articulation is key:**
  - "When a scientist introduces a new theoretical term, the point is to name a new explanatorily significant aspect of the world. As Enc has argued, in cases involving non-ostensible entities, 'the only reason we have for supposing that the entity exists is that we believe that our supposition will help us explain a set of phenomena. And these explanations can proceed only by our supposing that the entity has a set of properties and by our hypothesizing an explanatory mechanism which would show how the entity, given the properties it is supposed to have, brings about its effects.'" (p. 29, quoting Enc)

### Quotable Passages

1. **Metaphor as descriptive constraint:**
   > "Theory constitutive metaphors provide the necessary descriptive element and also capture the programmatic nature of scientific research. The basic idea is that new theoretical terminology is introduced in conjunction with explanatory metaphors. A metaphor involves a primary subject which is well understood and a secondary subject which is thought to have interesting similarities to the primary subject." (p. 28)

2. **Inductive open-endedness:**
   > "The specific nature of these similarities, however, displays what Boyd calls 'inductive open-endedness' and what Ernan McMullin calls 'tentative extension.' Boyd writes, 'The reader is invited to explore the similarities and analogies between features of the primary and secondary subject, including features not yet discovered or not yet fully understood... Theory constitutive metaphors are introduced when there is (or seems to be) good reason to believe that there are theoretically important aspects of similarity or analogy between the literal objects of the metaphors and their secondary subjects. The function of such metaphors is to put us on the track of those respects of similarity or analogy.'" (p. 28)

3. **Metaphors as invitations:**
   > "In short, theory constitutive metaphors are 'invitations to future research': they serve to introduce terms without specifying the defining characteristics of the referent of the term, but instead leave it open for the routine business of scientists to discover the yet unspecified essential properties of the referent in question." (p. 28)

4. **Causal link insufficient:**
   > "The descriptions must play more of a role than causal theorists have thus far allowed. Theory constitutive metaphors provide the descriptive constraint limiting the referent of the term to an 'appropriate' aspect of reality." (p. 27)

5. **Success conditions:**
   > "we are justified in believing that a theoretical term successfully refers when four necessary conditions are met: (1) quasi-perception provides a causal link to the world; (2) a theory constitutive metaphor provides a link to an underlying natural property or natural kind/family; (3) the resulting research program is phenomenally successful; and (4) the explanatory metaphor is fruitfully articulated (this last condition is a not clear that the research program, based on the inductively open-ended initial specification, is successful in further articulating the similarities between the literal subject and the secondary subject and thereby the essential nature of new explanatory kind is more fully discovered)." (p. 39)

6. **Phenomenal success vs. metaphor articulation:**
   > "Caloric theory is a good example of a successful theory with a central term which failed to refer. In order to explain the phenomena, scientists introduced 'caloric' as a term they understood to refer to some kind of subtle, conserved, fluid-like stuff... Caloric theory was quite successful in heat transfer experiments. Indeed, caloric theory generated a substantial research program and body of theory dealing with melting, boiling, and thermal expansion. Eventually, however, the irreversibility of heat and work showed that heat was not a conserved stuff... despite the phenomenal successes of the research program, the supposition that heat was a subtle fluid, which was responsible for heat, eventually became untenable." (p. 34)

7. **Articulation failure:**
   > "When a new explanatory mechanism can explain the successes and can be more fully articulated, it is reasonably concluded that no explanatory significant feature of the world was picked out by the central term of the theory." (p. 35)

### Connection to HPC Reframing Argument

**Direct relevance:**
Cummiskey's account of theory constitutive metaphors is the most directly relevant to the HPC/CMT synthesis. He argues that metaphors succeed by:
1. Providing a flexible descriptive constraint (not a rigid definition)
2. Inviting discovery of similarities between primary and secondary subjects
3. Allowing for progressive articulation of the underlying mechanisms

This maps almost perfectly onto the HPC reframing of CMT:
- CMT says metaphors work by mapping structure from source to target domain
- Boyd/Cummiskey say scientific metaphors work by suggesting similarities that prompt discovery of mechanisms
- HPC adds: the similarities are property clusters maintained by homeostatic mechanisms

**Key synthesis points:**
- **Metaphor = partial property overlap + mechanism articulation:** Cummiskey's "phenomenal success" corresponds to initial metaphor productivity (CMT's mappings work). His "articulation success" corresponds to discovering the mechanisms that maintain the property cluster (HPC).
- **Inductive open-endedness = mechanism discovery:** Boyd's "inductive open-endedness" of metaphors means scientists don't know all the relevant similarities in advance—they discover them. This is exactly what HPC predicts: property clusters are maintained by mechanisms, and discovering those mechanisms is the process of articulating the metaphor.
- **Reference failure = mechanism failure:** When a metaphor cannot be articulated (caloric, phlogiston, optical aether), it's because there's no underlying mechanism maintaining the presumed property cluster. The metaphor fails because the similarities are superficial, not maintained by homeostatic processes.

**For the argument:**
- Cummiskey shows that scientific progress requires *both* causal contact (Devitt) *and* descriptive constraint (Boyd's metaphors)
- HPC can explain the descriptive constraint: metaphors succeed when they track property clusters maintained by mechanisms
- This undermines Lakoff's embodiment-only account: metaphors succeed not just because of embodied schemas, but because they accommodate to causal structure (mechanisms) in the world

## Glynn (2010): Testing the Hypothesis: Objectivity and Verification in Usage-Based Cognitive Semantics

**Full citation:** Glynn, Dylan. 2010. "Testing the Hypothesis: Objectivity and Verification in Usage-Based Cognitive Semantics." In *Corpus-Driven Cognitive Semantics: Quantitative Approaches*, ed. D. Glynn & K. Fischer, 239–270. Berlin: Mouton de Gruyter.

### Core Argument

Glynn argues that purely semantic research using quantitative corpus methods is methodologically possible and can be objectively verified, despite the inherent subjectivity of semantic analysis. The key is operationalization: overtly identifying subjective analytical criteria so that (i) a second researcher can replicate the analysis, and (ii) statistical modeling can test hypotheses. Through a case study of the lexeme 'bother' in British and American English, Glynn demonstrates that even highly subjective semantic features (affect, agent, cause, theme) can be coded, verified through inter-coder agreement (Cohen's Kappa), and tested using Correspondence Analysis and Logistic Regression Analysis.

### Key Claims Relevant to HPC Reframing of CMT

**Partial reference and referential indeterminacy:**
- Not directly addressed, but the case study demonstrates polysemy as gradient clustering: 'bother' has multiple senses (Agentive Frame vs. Predicative Frame) that form coherent clusters relative to semantic features. This is compatible with partial reference—the word refers to a family of related constructions, not a single discrete sense (pp. 251–256).

**How theoretical terms fix reference:**
- **Operationalization of subjective features:** Semantic features (even abstract ones like AFFECT or AGENT) can be operationalized by overtly identifying analytical criteria (pp. 242, 247–250). This enables quantification and verification.
- **Multiple levels of subjectivity:** Glynn distinguishes "objectifiable variables" (formal features like tense, aspect, voice) from "non-objectifiable variables" (semantic features like affect, cause, agent). Even the latter can be treated objectively if the coding scheme is made explicit (pp. 245, 247).
- **Theme as extralinguistic factor:** The "theme" (topic of discourse: personal vs. societal) is coded as a semantic variable even though it's highly subjective. Inter-coder agreement was excellent (κ = 0.9367972), showing that subjective semantic judgments can be operationalized (p. 250).

**Mechanism, maintenance, or stability of reference:**
- **Form-meaning pairings as clusters:** The Correspondence Analysis reveals that semantic features cluster with grammatical constructions. The Oblique Transitive Construction with 'interruption' and 'imposition' affects forms a distinct cluster; the Direct Transitive Construction with 'physical pain', 'emotional pain', and 'anxiety' forms another (pp. 251–253). This clustering suggests that form-meaning pairings are maintained by usage patterns, not arbitrary convention.
- **Multidimensional semantics:** Glynn argues that "lexical senses are not discrete, but are often drawn towards certain formal variants of a word's use and should not be seen as pockets of meaning inside words waiting to be activated by situation–context. In Cognitive Linguistics, we have dismantled the syntax–lexicon distinction for good reason - the meaning of words is wrapped up with the semantics of grammatical variation" (p. 256). This is compatible with HPC: senses are property clusters maintained by interactive alignment and entrenchment, not essences.

**Success conditions for metaphor or analogy in science:**
- Not directly addressed, but the methodological framework is relevant: if semantic structure (including metaphorical mappings) can be operationalized and statistically verified, then we have a way to test CMT claims about metaphor empirically.

### Quotable Passages

1. **Subjectivity and objectivity:**
   > "It is often argued, and with some reason, that subjective semantic characteristics cannot be operationalised in a sufficiently objective manner to include them in quantitative studies. There exists, however, one strong counterargument. It is not objectivity that quantitative analysis offers us, but a better and more varied way of verifying the results. Seen from this perspective, quantitative methods are all the more important for subjective semantic analysis. Since the goal of descriptive research is accuracy, when an object of study does not possess clear observable features, verification is essential." (p. 242)

2. **Operationalization is key:**
   > "Operationalisation is crucial in quantitative research. It requires overt identification of criteria for the analysis. Without entering into a philosophical debate, meaning exists entirely in our minds and not in the observable 'real world'. Thus, with no objectively observable characteristics, one cannot operationalise *direct* semantic structure. It is for this reason that much quantitative research has avoided semantics. Although objectively operationalising subjective features may be technically impossible, using the principle of operationalisation to help overtly identify the analytical criteria acts as a perfect goal for improving semantic analysis. Most importantly, it permits quantification and therefore verification. If the semantic criteria, even subjective ones, are overtly identified, a second researcher can re-apply the analysis. Discrepancy between the analyses aids the process of refining the operationalisation and, thus, enhances its accuracy." (p. 242)

3. **Inter-coder agreement:**
   > "Following Szmrecsanyi (2003) and Zeschel (this vol.), secondary annotation was employed. Each of these four highly subjective variables, the semantics of the Agent, the Cause, the Affect, and the Theme of Discourse, were coded independently by two linguists. After discussion and trial samples, inter-coder agreement was excellent. Cohen's Kappa is used to calculate the degree of inter-coder agreement... Strength of agreement rating follow: Strength of agreement < 0.2 Poor; > 0.2 ≤ 0.4 Fair; > 0.4 ≤ 0.6 Moderate; > 0.6 ≤ 0.8 Good; > 0.8 ≤ 1 Very good. Despite the fact that this calculation is considered a conservative measure, the inter-coder agreement was approaching 1, which is perfect." (p. 250)
   - Affect: κ = 0.949181
   - Agent: κ = 0.9875858
   - Theme: κ = 0.9367972
   - Cause: κ = 0.9510682

4. **Form-meaning clusters:**
   > "Second, the Oblique Transitive and the Direct Transitive are the two constructions that profile the Agentive Frame of BOTHER. Although the Affects of 'imposition' and 'interruption' are clearly more associated with the use of the Oblique form, it is with the other Agentive construction that they share some association. In other words, although the Oblique and Direct Transitive Constructions are distinct, they are also semantically similar, relative to the factor of Affect. The Affect factor divides the plot into left and right halves – the constructions profiling an Agentive argument structure appear on the left, and the constructions profiling a Predicative structure on the right." (p. 252)

5. **Syntax and semantics inseparable:**
   > "lexical senses are not discrete, but are often drawn towards certain formal variants of a word's use and should not be seen as pockets of meaning inside words waiting to be activated by situation–context. In Cognitive Linguistics, we have dismantled the syntax–lexicon distinction for good reason - the meaning of words is wrapped up with the semantics of grammatical variation." (p. 256)

6. **Verification through statistical modeling:**
   > "There are three basic ways in which the quantitative treatment of found data allows us to verify the results of analysis: overt operationalisation, repeat analysis, and statistical modelling. Let us consider the importance of each of these steps for corpus-driven quantitative analysis in semantic research." (p. 242)

7. **Logistic Regression as explanatory power:**
   > "The principle of a Logistic Regression Analysis is quite simple. Given a binary response variable, such as the speaker's choice of a Predicative Frame Construction over an Agentive Frame Construction, the model calculates how accurately one may 'predict' that choice, with the given linguistic analysis of the data. Accordingly, the model is made up of predictor factors, each of which offers information to help the model accurately predict the outcome... How often you get the answer correct is the explanatory power of the model. Obviously, if one can accurately predict the speaker's choice in such a situation, one has analysed the data accurately and sufficiently." (p. 257)

**For the argument:**
- Glynn provides a methodological toolkit for testing whether metaphorical mappings create stable property clusters (as HPC predicts).
- If CMT metaphors are maintained by HPC mechanisms, we should expect them to show up as clusters in corpus data (like the 'bother' clusters).

## Cross-Paper Synthesis: Implications for HPC Reframing of CMT

**The argument:**
1. **Field (1973):** Reference is not determined by causal contact alone. Partial denotation is possible—terms can refer to multiple entities/properties simultaneously. This undermines pure causal theories and opens space for descriptive constraints.

2. **Cummiskey (1992):** Theory constitutive metaphors provide the necessary descriptive constraint. Metaphors succeed when they (i) suggest similarities between primary and secondary subjects, (ii) prompt discovery of underlying mechanisms, and (iii) are progressively articulated. Reference failure occurs when the metaphor cannot be articulated—when there's no underlying mechanism.

3. **Glynn (2010):** Semantic structure (including polysemy) can be operationalized and statistically verified. Form-meaning pairings cluster in usage data, suggesting they're maintained by mechanisms (not arbitrary convention).

**The synthesis:**
- **CMT is right that metaphors map structure from source to target.**
- **Boyd is right that metaphors succeed by accommodating to causal structure.**
- **HPC adds the missing piece:** The causal structure is property clusters maintained by homeostatic mechanisms. Metaphors succeed when they track these clusters; they fail when the presumed similarities aren't maintained by mechanisms.

**Evidence:**
- Field shows that partial reference is possible (metaphors don't need full reference to succeed).
- Cummiskey shows that metaphor articulation = mechanism discovery (caloric failed because the fluid metaphor had no mechanism; kinetic theory succeeded because molecular motion provided one).
- Glynn shows that semantic clusters are maintained by usage patterns (interactive alignment, entrenchment)—evidence for HPC mechanisms in action.

**Open questions:**
- Can we distinguish "phenomenal success" (metaphor productivity) from "articulation success" (mechanism discovery) empirically?
- How do we operationalize "mechanism articulation" for corpus analysis?
- Can statistical modeling (like Glynn's Logistic Regression) predict which metaphors will succeed based on property cluster overlap?
