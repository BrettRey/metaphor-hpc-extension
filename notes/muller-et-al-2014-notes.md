## Summary

37 Border Collies tested on Piaget's support problem: two boards, one with a food reward on it, the other with a reward beside it. Dogs must pull the correct board to retrieve the reward. 13/37 dogs learned to solve the basic on-off condition within 60 trials. These 13 were then tested on three transfer tasks (contact, perceptual containment, gap) designed to reveal which perceptual cues they relied on. The central finding: dogs solve the problem by learning to associate perceptual cues (specifically, the vertical level of the reward) with reward, *without understanding the causal structure* of the support relation.

## Key findings with page references

### 1. No spontaneous understanding; gradual learning

- Group performance in the first 10 trials did not differ from chance (mean 0.498; GLM: z = -0.01, p = 0.96) (p. 1076)
- Performance improved significantly across sessions (GLMM: z = 4.78, p < 0.001) and was above chance by session 3 (p. 1076)
- 13/37 dogs reached the individual learning criterion after 3--6 sessions (median 5 sessions, i.e., roughly 50 trials) (p. 1076)
- No dog solved the task at the earliest possible opportunity (after 2 sessions) (p. 1076)

**Implication for the paper:** Dogs do not have spontaneous causal understanding of support relations. This is trial-and-error learning, not insight. Directly relevant to the Keil challenge: category extension in dogs operates without explanatory understanding of the underlying causal structure.

### 2. Transfer task results

The 13 successful dogs were tested with 48 intermixed trials (12 per condition):

- **On-off** (retest): 74.5% correct, significantly above chance (p < 0.001) (p. 1077)
- **Contact** (off reward touching the board): 72.3% correct, significantly above chance (p < 0.001), not different from on-off (p = 0.65) (p. 1077)
- **Perceptual containment** (off reward surrounded but not supported by board): 60.9% correct, not significantly above chance (p = 0.10), significantly lower than contact (p = 0.03) (p. 1077)
- **Gap** (discontinuous boards): 41.7% correct, significantly *below* chance (p = 0.04) (p. 1077)

**Implication for the paper:** The variable performance across conditions is the key evidence. If dogs understood the *causal* support relation (board supports food, pulling board moves food), they should have succeeded in all transfer conditions. Instead, performance tracks which perceptual cues are preserved vs. disrupted across conditions.

### 3. Identification of the learned perceptual cue

The pattern of results (high in contact, low in perceptual containment and gap) best fits the hypothesis that dogs learned to choose the reward presented at a *higher vertical level* (reward-on-board is elevated relative to reward-beside-board). Table 1 (p. 1073) lays out predictions for each possible cue; the vertical-level cue uniquely predicts the observed pattern.

The authors conclude: "many of the dogs had learned to choose the reward that was on the board rather than the one that was off the other board and thus on a lower level" (p. 1078).

**Alternative cues ruled out:**
- Color/brightness of background: would predict immediate transfer to perceptual containment (not observed) (p. 1078)
- Contact between reward and board: would predict failure in contact condition (not observed) (p. 1078)
- Alignment of reward with board: would predict chance in both perceptual containment and gap (partially consistent, but doesn't explain the below-chance gap performance) (p. 1073)
- Perceptual containment: would predict reduced performance in contact (not observed) (pp. 1078--1079)

### 4. Rapid cue-switching when old cues fail

In the perceptual containment condition, dogs started at chance but improved significantly across just 12 trials (GLMM: z = 2.69, p = 0.007), while their on-off performance simultaneously *decreased* (z = -2.14, p = 0.03) (p. 1077). This is evidence of rapid perceptual re-learning: when the vertical-level cue became unreliable, dogs quickly adopted alternative cues.

The improvement was steeper than their initial learning of the on-off condition, though this did not quite reach significance (condition-by-trial interaction: z = 1.74, p = 0.08) (p. 1077).

**Implication for the paper:** This is not just stimulus generalization. Dogs are *flexibly switching between perceptual cues*, which implies they are not rigidly locked to a single stimulus dimension. They track which perceptual features are reliable predictors and update accordingly. This is closer to category-level reasoning than to simple conditioning.

### 5. The gap condition: persistent bias

Dogs performed *below* chance in the gap condition, preferring the shorter non-rewarded board segment (41.7% correct, p = 0.04). This replicates a finding from string-pulling studies where dogs also preferred shorter strings (Range et al. 2012, cited p. 1078). The authors suggest "a species-specific predisposition, for example a preference for a smaller over a larger non-food object that needs to be handled or moved" (p. 1078).

**Implication for the paper:** Some perceptual biases are hard to override. This is relevant to Morgan's Canon: the "simpler" mechanism (bias toward shorter/smaller object to manipulate) actively *interferes* with performance. This shows that perceptual strategies are not neutral default explanations; they have specific content and can produce systematically wrong behavior.

### 6. The authors' explicit conclusion on causal understanding

"Our study shows that at least some dogs can learn to solve the classic Piagetian support problem, but appear to do so by associating perceptual cues that are not causally related to the physical underpinnings of the task with the obtaining of a reward" (p. 1079).

"Our results thus add to a growing body of evidence that animals typically rely on, or learn to attend to, perceptual cues that may be correlated with the causally relevant information, but are not representing the underlying causal structure, when facing a problem-solving task (cf. Povinelli 2000; Penn and Povinelli 2007; Yamazaki et al. 2011; Albiach-Serrano et al. 2012; Gajdon et al. 2013)" (p. 1079).

### 7. Broader comparative context

The authors frame the literature as offering a spectrum of strategies (p. 1071--1072):
- "development of an intuitive understanding through experience" (Auersperg et al. 2009)
- "a causality bias during associative learning" (Hanus & Call 2011)
- "reliance on perceptual correlates of the causal mechanism" (Povinelli 2000)
- "heuristic strategies" (Hunt et al. 2006)
- "reliance on perceptual feedback" (Taylor et al. 2012; Riemer et al. 2013)
- "simple trial-and-error learning with subsequent generalization"

Dogs are placed firmly at the perceptual-cue end of this spectrum.

## Relevance to the three reviewer challenges

### Challenge 1: Keil -- Does cross-domain HPC extension require explanatory understanding?

This paper provides direct evidence that dogs can learn to solve problems in novel physical contexts *without* understanding the causal structure. They attend to statistical regularities among perceptual features (specifically the vertical level of the reward) and update these when they become unreliable. This is category use based on property co-occurrence, not on understanding *why* properties co-occur.

**The HPC argument this supports:** HPC theory already distinguishes between the *properties* that cluster and the *mechanisms* that maintain the clustering. An organism can track the cluster without representing the mechanism. The dog tracks "elevated object + board = reward obtainable" without representing the support relation. Analogously, a dog could track "elongated cleared feature + directional continuity + scent concentration = TRAIL" without representing *why* trails have these properties. HPC extension does not require what Keil calls "explanatory understanding" (causal knowledge of mechanisms). It requires only sensitivity to the property cluster.

**What this means for the metaphor claim:** If cross-domain HPC extension operates on partial property overlap without explanatory understanding, then the *same basic operation* is available to creatures with very different cognitive architectures. This does not make it "the same as human metaphor" (which layers propositional, linguistic, and reflective capacities on top), but it does make it the *same basic category operation*. Human metaphor is cross-domain HPC extension plus linguistic-conceptual elaboration, not a fundamentally different kind of thing.

### Challenge 2: Horowitz/Morgan's Canon -- Simpler perceptual mechanisms

This paper is a gift for the Morgan's Canon challenge, but it cuts both ways.

**What it provides for the canon-adherent:** Dogs solve the support problem via perceptual cue learning. They do not need to be credited with causal understanding, physical reasoning, or folk physics. The simpler explanation (perceptual association) is sufficient.

**What it provides for the HPC argument:**

1. "Simpler" does not mean "contentless." The dogs' perceptual strategy has specific content (vertical level), is flexibly updated when unreliable, and produces systematic errors when biases interfere. This is structured perceptual category use, not an undifferentiated association.

2. The rapid cue-switching in the perceptual containment condition (learning within 12 trials, faster than original acquisition) suggests the dogs are not starting from scratch each time. They appear to bring something like a search space of potentially relevant perceptual features. This is consistent with category-level representation rather than stimulus-response chains.

3. The below-chance gap performance shows that the "simple" mechanism is not a blank-slate associative process. It incorporates species-specific biases (preference for smaller objects to manipulate). These biases are themselves structured, reflecting the organism's ecological niche. This fits the HPC framework: categories are shaped by homeostatic mechanisms that include both environmental regularities and organismal biases.

4. Morgan's Canon says not to invoke higher cognition when simpler mechanisms suffice. But HPC extension *is* a relatively simple mechanism: category extension based on partial property overlap. It is *simpler* than Lakoff's full CMT apparatus (embodied schemas, source-target mappings, inference projection, etc.). The HPC account is actually the more parsimonious explanation of cross-domain extension.

### Challenge 3: Gentner/Holyoak -- What distinguishes HPC extension from structure-mapping or stimulus generalization?

This paper helps sharpen the distinction:

**HPC extension vs. stimulus generalization:**
- Stimulus generalization = responding to a novel stimulus in proportion to its similarity to a conditioned stimulus along a single dimension. The gap condition shows this is NOT what's happening: dogs don't generalize smoothly across all conditions. They track a specific perceptual cue, switch cues when one becomes unreliable, and show species-specific interference effects. This is more structured than gradient generalization.
- HPC extension = extending a category to a new instance that shares a subset of the clustered properties. The partial overlap is what matters, and which properties are tracked can shift. This is what the dogs appear to do: track a cluster of properties (vertical level, spatial relationship to board), update which properties are diagnostic, and extend "correct choice" to new configurations sharing those properties.

**HPC extension vs. structure-mapping:**
- Structure-mapping (Gentner) requires representing *relational structure* (A:B :: C:D) and aligning systems of relations. The dogs in this paper do not appear to represent relational structure (they don't represent "board supports food" as a relation). They track perceptual features.
- HPC extension operates on *property overlap*, which can include perceptual, functional, and relational properties, but does not *require* explicit relational alignment. It is a more basic operation that structure-mapping elaborates.
- The key difference: structure-mapping explains *how humans construct elaborate analogies*. HPC extension explains *why certain cross-domain mappings are non-arbitrary* (they track real causal-structural similarities in the world). These are complementary, not competing: structure-mapping is a cognitive mechanism; HPC extension is a claim about what makes the mapping epistemically successful (Boyd's accommodation).

## Connection to other notes

- **Thesen (1993), Hepper & Wells (2005), Pelgrim et al. (2025):** Those papers document dogs' actual TRAIL-following behavior. This paper shows the *cognitive mechanism* dogs use when solving physical-spatial problems: perceptual cue tracking without causal understanding.
- **Boyd (1979):** Boyd's distinction between properties and mechanisms in HPC kinds maps directly onto this paper's finding. Dogs track the property cluster; they don't represent the mechanism. Boyd's point about accommodation: the perceptual cues the dogs learn are "correlated with the causally relevant information" (Muller et al.'s own words). The cues work because there IS a causal structure that produces the correlation. The dog's learning accommodates to that structure without representing it.
- **Muscosky & Horowitz (2024):** Their work on dog olfactory categorization complements this: dogs solve categorization problems via perceptual (olfactory) features without requiring conceptual-causal models.
- **Simulated reviews:** Directly addresses the Keil and Horowitz objections anticipated there.
