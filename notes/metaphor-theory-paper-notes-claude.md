# Metaphor Theory Literature Notes
## HPC Reframing of Conceptual Metaphor Theory

**Core claim:** Boyd says metaphors succeed by accommodating to causal structure in the world; Lakoff says they express embodied schemas. HPC adds stability mechanisms that CMT lacks. Metaphor = cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap.

## Murphy (1996) - On Metaphoric Representation

### Core Argument

Murphy critically evaluates the claim that conceptual structure is metaphoric, distinguishing between two interpretations: (1) **strong view** – concepts are literally represented through metaphoric relations to source domains (e.g., understanding ARGUMENT requires accessing WAR structure); (2) **weak view** – metaphors influence conceptual structure but concepts have independent representations. Murphy argues the strong view is untenable but the weak view is empirically plausible. He proposes **structural similarity** as an alternative: metaphors succeed because domains share relational structure, not because one domain is understood through the other.

### Key Claims Relevant to HPC Reframing

**Supports partial mapping (against wholesale transfer):**
- "the categorization view assumes that the metaphor comprehends operates on a comparison of the two individual concepts alone" (p. 195) – categorization doesn't require wholesale domain transfer
- Multiple metaphors for one domain (LOVE IS A JOURNEY, LOVE IS WAR, LOVE IS A VALUABLE COMMODITY) create problems for strong metaphoric representation: "it is difficult to see how these two metaphors could simultaneously inform the same concept" (p. 186)
- "metaphors add the 'flesh' to the skeleton by filling in information that is not directly represented" (p. 187) – partial, not total structure

**Distinguishes comparison from categorization:**
- Comparison view: "metaphors are comparison statements that highlight processing of patterns common to two domains" (p. 179)
- Structural similarity view: "concepts such as love, journey, disease, insanity, anger, and so on, are represented separately, with their own structures" (p. 195)
- Problem of multiple metaphors solved by structural similarity: "Each metaphor type simply picks out different aspects of the concept's content" (p. 196)

**What makes metaphors succeed or fail:**
- **Structural alignment**: "it is the *structure* of the concepts, rather than superficial properties, that are similar" (p. 196) – relations map, not attributes
- **Salience imbalance**: "metaphoric categories will contain fewer predicates than the literal concepts they were derived from, and a higher proportion of these predicates can be mapped to relevant target concepts" (p. 199)
- Failure modes: polysemy (many putative "metaphors" are literal polysemy, e.g., *inflation is rising*), asymmetry (LOVE uses JOURNEY metaphors but not vice versa suggests discourse factors, not representation)

**On mechanism/maintenance/stability:**
- Conventionalization: "metaphors become increasingly conventional, there will be a shift in mode of alignment from comparison to categorization" (Bowdle & Gentner career-of-metaphor hypothesis, discussed p. 199)
- Etymology vs. psychology: "the word *furnace* has taken on a metaphorical sense" (historical) vs. "it is not clear a priori why one would need to further conceptualize it as a battle" (synchronic representation) – historical metaphor ≠ current cognitive structure

### Quotable Passages

**Against strong metaphoric representation:**
> "If the link is a normal one, as typically understood in cognitive psychology, then incorrect attributes of wars would routinely be attributed to arguments. If the link itself is metaphoric, then the theory seems to be appealing to a homunculus who interprets the metaphor." (p. 182)

**On multiple metaphors:**
> "If correct, then, metaphors are not serving as organizing after the fact to describe or characterize the directly-represented domain. (This issue will be discussed further in regard to the Invariance Principle below.)" (p. 185)

**Structural similarity alternative:**
> "It is worth remembering that it is the *structure* of the concepts, rather than superficial properties, that are similar on this account... The structure of the LOVE and JOURNEY concepts are separately represented and journey did not cause the structure of love." (p. 196)

**On conventionalization:**
> "metaphors become increasingly conventional, there will be a shift in mode of alignment from comparison to categorization" (p. 199) – supports a developmental/stability account

### Key References to Track Down

- Gentner & Clement (1988) - structure-mapping experiments
- Gentner (1983) - structure-mapping theory (READ NEXT)
- Glucksberg & Keysar (1990) - categorization view
- Black (1962, 1979) - interaction theory
- Ortony (1979) - salience imbalance

### Connection to HPC Reframing

Murphy's **structural similarity view** is proto-HPC: concepts are independent clusters of properties whose relational structure can be aligned. Metaphors succeed when:
1. **Partial property overlap** exists (not wholesale transfer)
2. **Relational structure aligns** (HPC: causal/functional relations among properties)
3. **Salience gradients differ** (source domain properties are more entrenched/accessible)

**Missing from Murphy but supplied by HPC:** What stabilizes these property clusters? Why do some structural alignments persist (conventionalize) while others remain novel? HPC answer: mechanisms maintain coherent property clusters; metaphors extend to new domains when partial overlap + mechanisms can sustain the extension.

**Murphy's asymmetry problem = HPC stability asymmetry:** LOVE uses JOURNEY metaphors but JOURNEY doesn't use LOVE metaphors. HPC: source domain has stronger homeostatic maintenance (journey-qua-physical-motion is stabilized by perception, action, cultural routines). Target domain (love-qua-emotion) is more abstract, less tightly constrained → borrows structure from better-maintained domains.

## Gentner (1983) - Structure-Mapping: A Theoretical Framework for Analogy

### Core Argument

Gentner's structure-mapping theory argues that analogy is characterized by **mapping relational structure from base to target, not attributes**. The key claim: analogies preserve higher-order relations (relations between relations) and systematically connected structures (the **systematicity principle**), while discarding object attributes. This contrasts with literal similarity (both attributes and relations overlap) and mere appearance matches (attribute overlap only). The Structure-Mapping Engine (SME) implements this as a computational model.

### Key Claims Relevant to HPC Reframing

**Supports partial mapping:**
- "Discard attributes of objects" (p. 158) – first mapping rule
- "Try to preserve relations between objects" (p. 158) – second mapping rule
- Analogy is selective: "in a complex nominal metaphor, participants first wrote out individual attribute descriptions taken from the eight metaphors (used previously by Ortony, 1979). Afterward they were presented with the metaphors, either in their normal order (e.g., 'Encyclopedias are gold mines') or in the reversed order ('Gold mines are encyclopedias')" (p. 166) – relations dominate

**Systematicity principle = HPC coherence:**
> "To decide *which* relations are preserved, choose systems of relations: R'{R₁(b₁, b₂), R₂(b₁, b₂)} — → R'{R₁(t₁, t₂), R₂(t₁, t₂)}" (p. 158)

Higher-order relations constrain lower-order ones. HPC parallel: mechanisms (higher-order causal relations) constrain which properties cluster together. Example from Gentner: solar system/atom analogy maps entire relational structure (CAUSE₁ → REVOLVES AROUND, ATTRACTS → CAUSE₂) not individual attributes (sun is yellow, nucleus is not).

**What makes analogies succeed:**
- **Systematicity**: "people recognize (however vaguely) that the system of predicates connected with central force is the deepest, most interconnected mappable system for this analogy" (p. 164)
- **Higher-order relations**: "MORE MASSIVE THAN is preserved while HOTTER THAN is not. Only MORE MASSIVE THAN participates in the central-force system of predicates" (p. 164)
- **One-to-one mapping constraint**: Each element in base maps to at most one element in target (prevents incoherent mappings)

**On mechanism/maintenance/stability:**
- No explicit stability account, but systematicity principle implies: coherent relational systems are preferred over isolated matches
- "As another demonstration of the operation of the systematicity principle, consider the analogy 'Heat is like water,' used to explain heat transfer from a warm house in cold weather." (p. 164) – dynamic causal knowledge (water flow) transferred

### Quotable Passages

**The central claim:**
> "an analogy is an assertion that a relational structure that normally applies in one domain can be applied in another domain" (p. 156)

**Systematicity principle:**
> "relations that belong to a mappable system of mutually interconnecting relationships is more likely to be imported into the target than is an isolated predicate" (p. 163)

**On higher-order relations:**
> "Relations have priority over object-attributes in analogy. However, as mentioned earlier, not all relations are equally likely to be preserved. For example, in the Rutherford analogy between solar system and the atom, the relation MORE MASSIVE THAN (sun, planet) is mapped across to the atom, but the formally similar relation HOTTER THAN (sun, planet) is not." (p. 163)

**Against attribute matching:**
> "A theory based on the mere relative numbers of shared and non-shared predicates cannot provide an adequate account of analogy, nor, therefore, a sufficient basis for a general account of relatedness." (p. 156)

### Key References to Track Down

- Falkenhainer, Forbus, & Gentner (1989) - SME implementation
- Clement (1981, 1982) - analogy in scientific problem-solving
- Burstein (1983), Carbonell (1981) - computational analogy models

### Connection to HPC Reframing

**Structure-mapping = HPC relational structure:**
Gentner's relational focus maps directly onto HPC: what gets preserved in analogy is the **causal/functional structure** that holds property clusters together, not the intrinsic properties themselves.

**Systematicity principle = homeostatic coherence:**
The preference for interconnected relational systems (systematicity) is the HPC stability requirement: mechanisms are systems of relations that maintain property clusters. Gentner: "mutually interconnecting relationships" more likely imported. HPC: mechanisms are mutually reinforcing causal processes.

**Career-of-metaphor precursor:**
Gentner distinguishes:
- **Literal similarity**: many shared attributes + many shared relations
- **Analogy**: few shared attributes + many shared relations
- **Mere appearance**: many shared attributes + few shared relations

This is proto-Bowdle & Gentner (2005): novel metaphors are analogies (relational mapping); conventional metaphors are category statements (shared properties). **HPC addition**: conventionalization happens when mechanisms extend from source to target, stabilizing the borrowed structure.

**Missing:** Why do some analogical mappings persist (become metaphors, then dead metaphors) while others remain one-off explanatory devices? HPC answer: when the relational structure imported from source can be maintained by mechanisms in the target domain, the metaphor stabilizes. Otherwise it remains a processing strategy, not a representational change.

## Bowdle & Gentner (2005) - The Career of Metaphor

### Core Argument

Bowdle & Gentner propose the **career-of-metaphor hypothesis**: metaphors have an evolutionary path from **comparison** (novel metaphors) to **categorization** (conventional metaphors). Novel metaphors (e.g., "A mind is like a kitchen") invite structural alignment between target and base concepts (analogy/comparison). As metaphors conventionalize through repeated use, the base term acquires a domain-general abstract category, enabling direct categorization (e.g., "My job is a jail" → *jail* has abstract sense 'confining unpleasant situation'). Three experiments confirm: (1) people prefer simile form for novel, metaphor form for conventional; (2) conventional metaphors comprehend faster than novel; (3) repeated exposure to novel metaphors shifts preference toward categorization.

### Key Claims Relevant to HPC Reframing

**Distinguishes comparison from categorization:**
- **Comparison mode**: "aligning the target concept with the literal base concept, or by horizontally aligning the target with the metaphoric category named by the base" (p. 199) – structural mapping
- **Categorization mode**: "vertically aligning the target concept with an abstract metaphoric category named by the base term during comprehension" (p. 199) – apply pre-existing category

**Career of metaphor = shift from comparison to categorization:**
> "as metaphors are conventionalized, there is a shift in mode of alignment from comparison to categorization as metaphors are conventionalized" (p. 193)

Figure 4 (p. 209) shows four stages:
1. **Novel metaphor**: literal target ← literal base (comparison)
2. **Conventional metaphor**: literal target ← metaphoric category (categorization)
3. **Dead metaphor**: literal target ← metaphoric category (no longer computed)
4. **Dead₂ metaphor**: literal target and literal base both subsumed under metaphoric category

**What makes metaphors succeed/conventionalize:**
- **Polysemy from repeated use**: "Like analogies, metaphors can lead additional structure to problematic target concepts, thereby making these concepts more coherent. However, this is not the only way in which metaphors may lead to knowledge change. It is often claimed that metaphors are a primary source of polysemy" (p. 197)

- **Abstraction through comparison**: "When a metaphor such as *An acrobat is a butterfly* is first encountered, both the target and base terms refer to specific concepts from different semantic domains, and the metaphor is initially interpreted by (a) aligning the two representations and (b) importing predicates from the base to the target, which can serve to amplify the target representation. As a result of this mapping, a shared abstraction will become strongly activated" (p. 198)

- **Conventionalization = vertical alignment becomes easier than horizontal**:
  Novel metaphors require costly structural alignment (horizontal: target ↔ base). Conventional metaphors activate abstract category (vertical: target → category). Experiment 2: conventional metaphors comprehend faster (M = 2,063 ms) than novel (M = 3,058 ms); **similes** comprehended even faster than **metaphor form** for novel (suggesting comparison), but no difference for conventional.

**On mechanism/maintenance/stability:**
- Implicit stability account: **frequency + consistency** → conventionalization
  - Experiment 3 manipulation: repeated exposure to novel metaphors with same base term shifts preference from simile to metaphor form (p. 206-207)
  - "If a base term has been used frequently enough to become polysemous and to automatically elicit a metaphoric category, it is only natural to assume that the abstraction can contribute to the meaning of a metaphor" (p. 198)

**Grammatical form tracks processing mode:**
- Simile form (*X is like Y*) → comparison processing
- Metaphor form (*X is Y*) → categorization processing
- Experiment 1: subjects prefer metaphor form for conventional figuratives (M = 4.35), simile form for novel (M = 2.54) on 10-point scale

### Quotable Passages

**The career hypothesis:**
> "the career of metaphor hypothesis states that aligning the literal target and base concepts of a metaphor can lead to the induction and eventual lexicalization of domain-general relational schemas, which can act as metaphoric categories. This predicts that as metaphors become increasingly conventional, there will be a shift in mode of alignment from comparison to categorization" (p. 197)

**Abstraction through use:**
> "Once a base term reaches this level of conventionality, target concepts can be vertically aligned with the abstract relational schema named by the base term during comprehension. Alignments involving the domain-specific meaning of the base term, by contrast, will typically be slower (and perhaps more complex) in comprehension. At this point, we might say that the metaphor has 'become a categorization.'" (p. 199)

**On polysemy:**
> "Like analogies, metaphors can lead additional structure to problematic target concepts, thereby making these concepts more coherent. However, this is not the only way in which metaphors may lead to knowledge change. It is often claimed that metaphors are a primary source of polysemy-they allow words with certain specific meanings to take on additional, related meanings" (p. 197-198)

**Grammatical form reflects function:**
> "form reflects function in figurative language: there should be a shift in preference for expression from the comparison (simile) form to the categorization (metaphor) form as figurative statements become increasingly conventional" (p. 200)

**Dead metaphors:**
> "Dead₁ metaphors are similar to conventional metaphors, except that the two representations evoked by the base term are no longer explicitly linked. That is, dead₁ base terms are homonymous rather than polysemous" (p. 209) – e.g., *culture* 'agriculture' vs. 'culture of knowledge'

### Key References to Track Down

- Glucksberg & Keysar (1990) - categorization view of metaphor
- Gentner & Wolff (1997) - alignment and categorization
- Blank (1988), Giora (1997) - conventionality effects
- Kintsch (2000) - predication model (conceptual combination alternative)

### Connection to HPC Reframing

**Career-of-metaphor = HPC stabilization trajectory:**

1. **Novel metaphor (comparison stage)** = exploratory HPC extension
   - Structural alignment tests whether source-domain relational structure (mechanisms) can apply to target
   - Costly because mechanisms are source-specific, must be actively analogized

2. **Conventionalization** = mechanism generalization
   - Repeated successful applications → abstract the relational structure (Boyd's "accommodation to causal structure")
   - Base term becomes polysemous: literal sense + abstract category sense
   - The abstract category = **generalized mechanism template** (not tied to source domain specifics)

3. **Conventional metaphor (categorization stage)** = HPC category membership
   - Target is now *a member* of the abstract category
   - Vertical alignment is cheaper because the abstract category (generalized mechanisms) applies directly
   - Example: *jail* (literal building) → *jail* (abstract 'confining unpleasant situation'). The mechanisms (physical/social barriers, restriction of movement, negative valence) have generalized from concrete to abstract domains.

4. **Dead metaphor** = HPC category split
   - Original connection (literal base ↔ metaphoric category) no longer active
   - Two independent categories, historically related but synchronically distinct
   - Example: *culture* (agriculture) vs. *culture* (intellectual domain) – mechanisms diverged

**Missing from Bowdle & Gentner, supplied by HPC:**
- **Why** does conventionalization happen for some metaphors but not others? HPC: when the relational structure (mechanisms) can be maintained in the target domain. If target lacks support for the mechanisms, metaphor remains novel/fails.
- **What** is the "abstract category" psychologically? HPC: a cluster of properties maintained by generalized mechanisms, no longer tied to source domain specifics.

**Bowdle & Gentner's experimental findings support HPC stability claims:**
- Frequency + consistency → conventionalization (Experiment 3): HPC requires repeated activation + stable mechanisms
- Conventional metaphors faster (Experiment 2): generalized mechanisms are more accessible than domain-specific analogy
- Form tracks function (Experiment 1): grammatical form externalizes HPC membership (categorization) vs. exploratory extension (comparison)

## Holyoak & Stamenkovic (2018) - Metaphor Comprehension: A Critical Review

### Core Argument

Holyoak & Stamenkovic critically review three major theoretical positions on metaphor comprehension: **analogy**, **categorization**, and **conceptual mapping**. They conclude:
1. Simple metaphors are generally processed as categorizations, but complex metaphors require analogical comparison
2. The analogy/categorization debate reflects **processing differences, not a categorical distinction** – both mechanisms can apply
3. **Conceptual mapping** (Lakoff/CMT) lacks strong empirical support; metaphors do not require online access to full conceptual mappings
4. Multiple mechanisms should be integrated; metaphor comprehension depends on conventionality, grammatical form, individual differences, and context

### Key Claims Relevant to HPC Reframing

**Supports partial mapping (against wholesale transfer):**
- **Against full conceptual mapping access**: "none of the many neural studies of metaphor processing revealed clear activation of the RLPFC, the region that has emerged as a neural marker of complex analogical reasoning" (p. 653) – simple metaphors don't recruit analogy network

- **Selective property transfer**: "property-transfer interpretations in which the information used to identify the base is highly salient for the base and of low salience for both items... people follow one of two main strategies. One strategy, *property transfer*, is to find a salient property from it, which is than applied to the head" (p. 647)

- **Partial structure in categorization**: "The categorization view assumes that metaphor comprehension operates on a comparison of the two individual concepts alone... metaphoric categories will contain fewer predicates than the literal concepts they were derived from" (p. 195, citing Murphy 1996)

**Distinguishes comparison (analogy) from categorization:**
- **Analogy position**: "metaphor involves similarity between concepts drawn from dissimilar domains" – structural alignment, costly, for novel metaphors

- **Categorization position**: "metaphors (at least some of them) are interpreted as category statements" (Glucksberg & Keysar 1990) – fast, for conventional metaphors

- **Career-of-metaphor synthesis**: "the career of metaphor hypothesis: the hypothesis assumes that for more conventional metaphors the base term refers both to a literal concept and to an associated abstract category... Novel metaphors are processed strictly as comparisons, whereas conventional figurative statements can be processed either as comparisons or as categorizations" (p. 199)

**What makes metaphors succeed or fail:**

- **Aptness**: "aptness of a metaphor – measuring how well the source-target pairing is at capturing an important similarity between the target and base" (p. 204) – better predictor than conventionality in some studies (Jones & Estes 2006)

- **Systematicity/coherence**: structure-mapping theory's systematicity principle: "relations that belong to a mappable system of mutually interconnecting relationships is more likely to be imported into the target" (p. 163, citing Gentner 1983)

- **Salience imbalance**: "information used to identify the base is highly salient for the base and of low salience for both items" (p. 647) – one-way property transfer

- **Working memory/analogical reasoning**: "people who scored high on a working memory test generated better interpretations of metaphors more quickly" (Chiappe & Chiappe 2007) – complex metaphors tax WM

**On mechanism/maintenance/stability:**

- **Conventionalization**: "as metaphors become increasingly conventional, there will be a shift in mode of alignment from comparison to categorization" (p. 199, Bowdle & Gentner 2005)

- **Frequency effects**: "conventional metaphors were read as quickly as literal sentences, whereas novel metaphors took significantly longer to comprehend" (Blank 1988) – entrenchment

- **Embodiment** (weak support): "metaphors may serve as conduits that link sensorimotor experience with abstract concepts" (p. 655) – but evidence is "intriguing, but fall far short of conclusive"

- **Neural substrate**: temporal cortex for semantic processing, RLPFC for complex analogy (but *not* activated in simple metaphor comprehension) – suggests simple metaphors use semantic memory, not online reasoning

### Quotable Passages

**Against strong CMT embodiment claims:**
> "the evidence that inconsistent mappings impair comprehension of novel metaphors (Boronat, 1996; Gentner, Bowdle, et al., 2001; Keysar, Shen, Glucksberg, & Horton, 2000) ... does not support the extreme view that comprehension of expressions ordinarily considered literal, such as 'I'm depressed,' necessarily requires access to a conceptual mapping such as SAD IS DOWN" (p. 654)

**On the career of metaphor:**
> "which is it—analogy, conceptual combination, or both? Our answer is this: Our analysis of the back-and-forth debate between the analogy and categorization advocates encouraged the development of a kind of compromise: the career of metaphor hypothesis. Although proposed most explicitly by Bowdle and Gentner (2005), elements of the idea trace back at least to Black (1962) and Gentner (1982)" (p. 652)

**Analogy for complex metaphors:**
> "The analogy position bears the burden of showing that a full-blown process of mapping multiple elements and relations across source and target domains is in fact required to comprehend simple metaphors. Our review did not uncover any compelling evidence of this sort" (p. 656)

**Categorization for simple metaphors:**
> "For simple nominal metaphors like 'My job is a jail,' people readily interpret the focal word (the source term) as alluding to an abstract metaphorical meaning (Glucksberg & Keysar, 1990). Kintsch's (2000) predication model provides a vector-based computational instantiation (based on semantic vectors for individual words) of how such a meaning shift is possible via 'one-shot' process mapping" (p. 652)

**Multiple mechanisms:**
> "metaphoric categories might be preferred over novel similes because they invite less representation (and are therefore simpler). In contrast, complex metaphors will continue to prefer the simile form, which signals that a more elaborate analogical mapping is called for" (p. 211, cited in H&S review)

**Critique of conceptual mapping:**
> "Conceptual mappings are stated in the form of category statements (e.g., according to the categorization view)" (p. 649) ... "But this raises an intriguing question: If novel similes, but not novel metaphors, can be comprehended on the basis of categorization, why would anyone select the metaphor form over the simile form for novel figuratives?" (p. 211, Bowdle & Gentner 2005)

- Barsalou (2008) - grounded cognition

### Connection to HPC Reframing

**HPC as integrative framework for analogy + categorization:**

Holyoak & Stamenkovic's conclusion – "multiple mechanisms" – maps onto HPC:
1. **Novel metaphor (analogy)** = testing whether source-domain mechanisms can extend to target
   - Costly structural alignment = checking whether causal/functional structure (HPC mechanisms) in source can apply to target
   - Succeeds when target has partial property overlap + can support the mechanisms

2. **Conventional metaphor (categorization)** = target is member of abstract HPC category
   - Fast categorization = abstract category (generalized mechanisms) applies directly
   - No need for online analogical reasoning because mechanisms are already established in target domain

**What HPC adds to Holyoak & Stamenkovic's review:**

- **Why conventionalization?** H&S: frequency. HPC: frequency + mechanism generalization. Not all frequent metaphors conventionalize; only those where mechanisms can be maintained in target domain.

- **What is "aptness"?** H&S: "how well the source-target pairing is at capturing an important similarity." HPC: degree to which source-domain mechanisms can be maintained in target domain. Apt metaphors = high mechanism transferability.

- **Why working memory predicts complex metaphor comprehension?** H&S: analogical reasoning taxes WM. HPC: checking whether multi-component mechanisms can co-adapt to new domain requires holding multiple property-mechanism relations in WM.

- **What are "conceptual mappings"?** CMT: embodied schemas. HPC: generalized mechanism templates. The "mappings" (LOVE IS A JOURNEY) are *not* online processing structures but **conventionalized HPC categories** – abstract property clusters maintained by generalized mechanisms.

**HPC resolves analogy/categorization debate:**
- Not two processes, but two **stages of HPC extension**
  - **Early stage (novel)**: analogical testing of mechanism transferability
  - **Late stage (conventional)**: categorical membership in abstract HPC

- **Partial mapping** is inevitable in HPC: mechanisms transfer, but only those properties supported by the mechanisms. Attributes not maintained by mechanisms don't transfer.

- **Asymmetry** (LOVE → JOURNEY, not JOURNEY → LOVE): source domains have more stable mechanisms. Target domains borrow from better-maintained sources.

**Boyd's accommodation claim fits here:**
> "metaphors succeed by accommodating to causal structure in the world" (Boyd, paraphrased)

HPC version: Metaphors succeed when source-domain mechanisms (causal structure) can be maintained in target domain (accommodation = mechanism stability in new domain). Lakoff's embodied schemas are **one type** of stable mechanism (sensorimotor), but HPC allows for multiple mechanism types (social, cultural, functional).

## Synthesis: HPC Reframing of CMT

### Core Thesis
Metaphor is **cross-domain HPC extension on partial property overlap**. Boyd's "accommodation to causal structure" + Lakoff's embodied grounding, unified by HPC stability mechanisms.

### How HPC Reframes the Three Debates

**1. Comparison vs. Categorization** (Gentner vs. Glucksberg)
- **HPC integration**: Both are stages of HPC extension
  - **Comparison (novel)**: testing mechanism transferability across domains
  - **Categorization (conventional)**: membership in generalized HPC category
- Career-of-metaphor = HPC stabilization trajectory

**2. Attribute vs. Relational Mapping** (Murphy, Gentner)
- **HPC answer**: Relations = mechanisms; attributes = properties
- Only properties maintained by mechanisms transfer
- Systematicity (Gentner) = homeostatic coherence (HPC)

**3. Embodied Cognition vs. Abstract Structure** (Lakoff vs. Murphy/Gentner)
- **HPC reconciliation**: Embodied schemas are one mechanism type (sensorimotor)
- HPC allows multiple stabilizers: perceptual, motor, social, functional, cultural
- Lakoff's "conceptual mappings" = conventionalized HPC categories (generalized mechanisms)

### What HPC Adds

**Stability account:**
- Why some metaphors conventionalize, others don't
- Why source domains are asymmetric (better-maintained → less-maintained)
- Why polysemy emerges (mechanism generalization)

**Mechanism typology:**
- Sensorimotor (Lakoff): image schemas, motor routines
- Functional (Boyd): causal structure, teleology
- Social/cultural: norms, practices, institutions
- All can stabilize metaphoric categories

**Partial mapping explained:**
- Not arbitrary: only mechanism-supported properties transfer
- Multiple metaphors for one target: different mechanisms, different property subsets
- Dead metaphors: mechanism divergence, category split

### Open Questions

1. **What determines mechanism transferability?**
   - Domain distance? Abstraction level? Cultural scaffolding?

2. **How do multiple mechanisms interact in one category?**
   - Reinforcement? Competition? Hierarchy?

3. **What is the neural substrate of generalized mechanisms?**
   - Temporal cortex (semantic memory) for conventional metaphors
   - But what represents the abstract relational structure?

4. **Can failed metaphors teach us about mechanism constraints?**
   - Why does "My lawyer is a shark" work but "My lawyer is a filing cabinet" doesn't?
   - HPC prediction: shark mechanisms (predatory, aggressive) apply to lawyering domain; filing cabinet mechanisms (storage, retrieval) don't

### Implications for Linguistic Theory

- **Polysemy**: HPC mechanism generalization (not Lakoff's radial categories)
- **Grammatical metaphor** (Halliday): nominalization, grammaticalization as HPC category extension
- **Semantic change**: conventionalization = HPC stabilization in new domain
- **Cross-linguistic variation**: different languages stabilize different metaphoric categories (mechanisms vary culturally)
